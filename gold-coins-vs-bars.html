Gold Coins vs. Gold Bars — Which Should You Buy in 2026?

By GoldPriceTools Editorial Team · Updated 7 May 2026 · 10 min read

With gold trading above $4,600 per troy ounce in 2026, choosing between coins and bars is no longer a trivial decision — the format you choose affects your premiums paid, your tax bill, your ability to sell quickly, and how much capital you need to get started. This guide breaks down every variable so you can make the right choice for your situation.

The Core Difference: What You Are Actually Buying

Both gold coins and gold bars represent ownership of physical gold — the same underlying metal. The differences lie in form, legal status, manufacturing cost, and the market you can sell into.

Gold coins are minted by government or sovereign mints (such as the Royal Mint, US Mint, or Perth Mint). Many carry face value as legal tender, which in the UK triggers specific tax advantages. They are produced in standardised weights (most commonly 1 oz, 1/2 oz, 1/4 oz, 1/10 oz) with intricate designs that make them instantly recognisable worldwide.

Gold bars (also called bullion bars or ingots) are produced by private refiners or sovereign mints and are valued almost exclusively for their gold content. They are available in a wider range of weights — from 1 gram to 400 troy ounces (the London Good Delivery bar) — and carry lower premiums due to simpler manufacturing.

Premium Comparison: How Much More Are You Paying?

The premium is the percentage you pay above the raw spot price of gold. It represents manufacturing, distribution, and dealer margin. Understanding premiums is crucial because they directly affect your break-even point.

Break-Even Example: If you pay a 5% premium on a 1 oz gold coin when gold is at $4,600/oz, you effectively pay $4,830. Gold must rise to $4,830 before you break even — before any dealer spread on resale. A bar with a 1.5% premium requires a much smaller price increase to enter profit.

As a general rule: the smaller the unit, the higher the premium. A 1-gram gold bar may carry a premium of 15–20% simply because the fixed costs of refining, assaying, and packaging are spread over very little gold content.

Bars from LBMA-accredited refiners (such as PAMP, Valcambi, or the Perth Mint) command good prices, but unbranded bars may attract a discount or require assay testing before resale.

UK Investor Advantage: For UK residents, the CGT exemption on Britannia and Sovereign coins is one of the most powerful tax advantages available to private investors. In 2026, with the CGT annual exempt amount reduced to just £3,000, the ability to take unlimited gains on UK legal tender gold coins tax-free is exceptionally valuable. A higher-rate taxpayer selling £50,000 of gold bars at a £20,000 gain would pay approximately £4,800 in CGT. The same gain on Britannia coins: £0.

Storage and Insurance Costs

Physical gold requires secure storage — either at home or via professional vault facilities. Storage costs eat into your return over time, so format can matter.

  • Home storage: A quality home safe rated for fire and theft (e.g., Burg-Wachter or Chubbsafes) costs £200–£800. Check your home insurance policy — most policies cap cash and valuables at £1,000–£2,000 without a specific scheduled rider.
  • Bank safe deposit box: Costs £50–£300/year depending on size and bank. Note: the contents are typically not insured by the bank itself.
  • Professional vaulting: LBMA-approved vaults (such as Brink’s, G4S, or Loomis) typically charge 0.1–0.15% of value per year, with insurance included. The Royal Mint offers vaulted storage from 0.5% per year for smaller holdings.

On a storage cost basis, coins and bars are broadly equivalent — it is the value of your holdings that drives cost, not the format. The key difference: coins are generally easier to store at home (smaller, more portable, independently verifiable) while very large bars (100 g+) are better suited to professional vault facilities.

Which Should You Choose?

The right answer depends on your investment size, tax situation, and goals. Here is a clear framework:

Choose Coins If…
  • You are a UK investor who wants to avoid Capital Gains Tax on profits
  • You want maximum liquidity and the ability to sell small portions quickly
  • You are investing smaller amounts and value divisibility
  • You plan to store gold at home and want easily verifiable, portable pieces
  • You are building a US Gold IRA with widely accepted products
  • You value the prestige and collectibility of sovereign-minted gold
Choose Bars If…
  • You are investing large sums and want to minimise the premium cost per ounce
  • You are outside the UK (or your tax jurisdiction treats coins and bars equally)
  • You are building long-term wealth and plan to hold for many years without selling portions
  • You plan to use professional vault storage where storage cost is value-based, not unit-based
  • You are investing on behalf of a company or trust where CGT exemptions do not apply
  • You want the absolute lowest possible cost per gram of gold owned

The Optimal Strategy: A Blended Approach

Most experienced gold investors hold a combination of both. A practical allocation for a UK investor building a £20,000 gold position might look like:

  • £10,000 in Britannia 1 oz coins — CGT-exempt, highly liquid, IRA-eligible, easy to sell in portions
  • £5,000 in 1 oz gold bars (PAMP or Royal Mint) — lower premium, long-term core holding
  • £5,000 in Sovereigns — CGT-exempt, lower per-coin price, excellent secondary market

This blend maximises tax efficiency (80% in CGT-exempt products), ensures liquidity at multiple price points, and keeps average premiums competitive.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are gold coins or gold bars better for investment?

Neither is universally better — it depends on your goals. Coins offer greater divisibility, better liquidity, and significant tax advantages in the UK (CGT-exempt legal tender coins). Bars offer lower premiums and more gold per pound spent, making them better suited for large, long-term wealth preservation. Many investors hold both.

Do gold coins have a higher premium than gold bars?

Yes. Gold coins typically carry premiums of 3–8% above spot price, while gold bars range from 0.5–3%. The difference reflects the additional manufacturing cost for coins’ intricate designs and legal tender status. Over time this premium gap narrows for larger coin weights.

Are gold coins exempt from Capital Gains Tax in the UK?

Yes. UK legal tender gold coins — including Britannias and Sovereigns — are exempt from Capital Gains Tax (CGT) under the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992. Foreign coins such as Krugerrands and Maple Leafs are not CGT-exempt in the UK, though gains may still be sheltered within the annual CGT allowance (£3,000 in 2026).

Can you put gold coins or bars in a Gold IRA?

Yes, but only specific products qualify. The IRS requires gold held in an IRA to be at least 99.5% pure. Approved coins include the American Gold Eagle and Canadian Maple Leaf. All gold bars in an IRA must be produced by an LBMA-approved refiner. Gold Sovereigns and Krugerrands do not meet IRA purity requirements.

Which is easier to sell — gold coins or gold bars?

Gold coins are generally easier to sell because they are universally recognised and can be sold to any dealer, pawnbroker, or private buyer. Gold bars, particularly larger ones (100 g+), require specialist buyers and may need assay re-verification, which can slow the sale and reduce the price you achieve.

What is the best gold coin to buy in the UK?

For UK investors, the British Britannia (1 oz, 24K, 999.9 fine) is widely considered the top choice. It is VAT-free, CGT-exempt as UK legal tender, accepted by all major bullion dealers, and qualifies for US Gold IRAs. The British Sovereign (22K) is also popular for its lower individual price point and strong secondary market.

Are gold bars cheaper than gold coins?

Per gram of gold, yes — gold bars are cheaper than coins because they carry lower manufacturing premiums. A 1 kg gold bar might carry a premium of just 1–1.5% above spot, while a 1 oz gold coin typically carries a 4–6% premium. However, bars require larger upfront capital and may have lower liquidity than coins.
