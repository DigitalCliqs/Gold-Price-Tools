01 · The fundamentals

The core difference — what you are actually buying

Both formats represent ownership of physical gold — the same underlying metal. The differences lie in form, legal status, manufacturing cost, and the market you can sell into.

Gold coins are minted by government or sovereign mints (the Royal Mint, US Mint, Perth Mint and others). Many carry face value as legal tender, which in the UK triggers specific tax advantages. They are produced in standardised weights (most commonly 1 oz, ½ oz, ¼ oz, 1/10 oz) with intricate designs that make them instantly recognisable worldwide.

Gold bars (also called bullion bars or ingots) are produced by private refiners or sovereign mints and are valued almost exclusively for their gold content. They are available in a much wider range of weights — from 1 gram up to 400 troy ounces (the London Good Delivery bar) — and carry lower premiums due to simpler manufacturing.

06 · Tax by country

Tax treatment — a critical differentiator

Tax rules vary significantly by country and can dramatically affect your net return. This is where coins often win decisively over bars for UK investors.

Gold coins

UK legal tender coins — Britannia and Sovereign — are fully CGT-exempt under the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992. Unlimited tax-free gains. Foreign coins (Krugerrand, Maple Leaf, Eagle) are subject to normal CGT.

Gold bars

Subject to CGT at 18% (basic rate) or 24% (higher rate) on gains above the £3,000 annual allowance (2026). VAT-free as investment gold.

Concrete example: A higher-rate taxpayer selling £50,000 of gold bars at a £20,000 gain would pay roughly £4,080 in CGT after the allowance. The same gain on Britannia coins: £0.

Gold coins

Gains taxed as collectibles (28% max long-term) or ordinary income inside a Gold IRA. Capital gains rates may apply at lower rates via gold ETFs (still 28% for physically backed). State tax varies.

Gold bars

Same collectibles tax treatment — 28% maximum long-term capital gains rate. LBMA-approved bars qualify for Gold IRA tax deferral.

Gold coins

Investment gold coins exempt from VAT. Gains tax-free after 12-month holding period under §23 EStG.

Gold bars

Investment gold bars also exempt from VAT. Same 12-month holding period for tax-free gains.

Gold coins

CGT applies; 50% discount for assets held over 12 months. No GST on investment gold (99.5%+ purity).

Gold bars

Same CGT treatment as coins; 50% discount after 12 months. No GST on investment gold.

UK investor advantage. With the CGT annual exempt amount reduced to just £3,000 in 2026, the ability to take unlimited gains on UK legal tender gold coins tax-free is exceptionally valuable. For meaningful UK gold positions, the Britannia and Sovereign CGT exemption can save thousands of pounds versus the equivalent gain in bars.

08 · Storage & insurance

Storage and insurance costs

Physical gold requires secure storage — either at home or via professional vault facilities. Storage costs eat into your return over time, so format can matter.

  • Home storage: A quality home safe rated for fire and theft (Burg-Wachter, Chubbsafes) costs $250–$1,000. Check your home insurance policy — most cap cash and valuables at $1,500–$3,000 without a scheduled rider.
  • Bank safe deposit box: $65–$400 per year depending on size and bank. Note: contents are typically not insured by the bank itself.
  • Professional vaulting: LBMA-approved vaults (Brink's, G4S, Loomis) typically charge 0.10–0.15% of value per year, with insurance included. The Royal Mint and BullionVault offer vaulted storage from 0.12% per year for smaller holdings.

On a storage cost basis, coins and bars are broadly equivalent — it is the value of your holdings that drives cost, not the format. The key difference: coins are easier to store at home (smaller, more portable, independently verifiable) while very large bars (100 g+) are better suited to professional vault facilities.

10 · Which to choose

Which should you choose?

The right answer depends on your investment size, tax situation, and goals. Here is a clear framework with concrete decision criteria.

Choose coins if…
  • You are a UK investor who wants to avoid Capital Gains Tax on profits
  • You want maximum liquidity and the ability to sell small portions quickly
  • You are investing smaller amounts and value divisibility
  • You plan to store gold at home and want easily verifiable pieces
  • You are building a US Gold IRA with widely accepted products
  • You value the prestige and collectibility of sovereign-minted gold
Choose bars if…
  • You are investing large sums and want to minimise premium cost per ounce
  • You are outside the UK (or your jurisdiction treats coins and bars equally)
  • You are building long-term wealth and plan to hold for many years
  • You plan to use professional vault storage (value-based fee, not unit-based)
  • You are investing on behalf of a company or trust where CGT exemptions don't apply
  • You want the absolute lowest possible cost per gram of gold owned

12 · Frequently asked

Frequently asked questions

The questions readers ask most about choosing between coins and bars in 2026.

Are gold coins or gold bars better for investment?

Neither is universally better — it depends on your goals. Coins offer greater divisibility, better liquidity, and significant tax advantages in the UK (CGT-exempt legal tender coins). Bars offer lower premiums and more gold per dollar spent, making them better suited for large, long-term wealth preservation. Many investors hold both.

Do gold coins have a higher premium than gold bars?

Yes. Gold coins typically carry premiums of 3–8% above spot price, while gold bars range from 0.5–3%. The difference reflects the additional manufacturing cost for coins’ intricate designs and legal tender status. Over time this premium gap narrows for larger coin weights.

Are gold coins exempt from Capital Gains Tax in the UK?

Yes. UK legal tender gold coins — including Britannias and Sovereigns — are exempt from Capital Gains Tax (CGT) under the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992. Foreign coins such as Krugerrands and Maple Leafs are not CGT-exempt in the UK, though gains may still be sheltered within the annual CGT allowance (£3,000 in 2026).

Can you put gold coins or bars in a Gold IRA?

Yes, but only specific products qualify. The IRS requires gold held in an IRA to be at least 99.5% pure. Approved coins include the American Gold Eagle and Canadian Maple Leaf. All gold bars in an IRA must be produced by an LBMA-approved refiner. Gold Sovereigns and Krugerrands do not meet IRA purity requirements.

Which is easier to sell — gold coins or gold bars?

Gold coins are generally easier to sell because they are universally recognised and can be sold to any dealer, pawnbroker, or private buyer. Gold bars, particularly larger ones (100 g+), require specialist buyers and may need assay re-verification, which can slow the sale and reduce the price you achieve.

What is the best gold coin to buy in the UK?

For UK investors, the British Britannia (1 oz, 24K, 999.9 fine) is widely considered the top choice. It is VAT-free, CGT-exempt as UK legal tender, accepted by all major bullion dealers, and qualifies for US Gold IRAs. The British Sovereign (22K) is also popular for its lower individual price point and strong secondary market.

Are gold bars cheaper than gold coins?

Per gram of gold, yes — gold bars are cheaper than coins because they carry lower manufacturing premiums. A 1 kg gold bar might carry a premium of just 1–1.5% above spot, while a 1 oz gold coin typically carries a 4–6% premium. However, bars require larger upfront capital and may have lower liquidity than coins.
