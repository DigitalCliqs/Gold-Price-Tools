Best Gold & Silver Testing Kits in 2026 — Reviewed

The most reliable way to test gold and silver at home is with an acid test kit — a set of nitric acid solutions matched to different karats. Electronic XRF testers are more accurate but cost $300+. For most home users, a mid-range acid kit ($20–$40) is sufficient.

1. Types of Testing Methods

Before diving into the best kits, it's important to understand the different ways you can test gold and silver at home. The most common methods include:

  • Acid Testing: The industry standard for home testing. It involves scratching the metal on a stone and applying specific nitric acid solutions. It's highly reliable for determining karats.
  • Electronic Testers (XRF): These devices use X-ray fluorescence to analyze the metal composition. They are incredibly accurate and non-destructive but are significantly more expensive.
  • Magnet Test: A simple preliminary test. Gold and silver are not magnetic. If the item sticks to a strong neodymium magnet, it's plated or fake. Note: Some fake metals are also non-magnetic, so this isn't conclusive.
  • Specific Gravity Test: Involves weighing the item in air and then in water to determine its density. It's accurate but requires precise scales and setup.

4. How to Use an Acid Test Kit

Testing gold with an acid kit is straightforward but requires care. Here is a step-by-step guide:

  1. Preparation: Put on safety glasses and gloves. Nitric acid is corrosive. Ensure you are in a well-ventilated area.
  2. Scratch the Item: Firmly rub the piece of jewelry against the testing stone. You want to leave a distinct, thick line of metal on the stone. Tip: File a tiny notch in an inconspicuous area first to test the core, as heavily plated items might fool a surface scratch.
  3. Apply the Acid: Start with the lowest karat acid (e.g., 10K). Apply a single drop of the acid directly onto the scratch mark on the stone.
  4. Observe the Reaction:
    • If the scratch remains unchanged, the item is at least the karat of the acid used.
    • If the scratch fades slightly or turns brown, the gold is likely a lower karat than the acid used.
    • If the scratch disappears rapidly and bubbles green, the item is not gold (likely brass or copper base).
  5. Repeat: If the scratch survived the 10K acid, try again with a fresh scratch and the 14K acid. Continue stepping up the karat until the scratch dissolves. The true karat is the one just below the acid that dissolved the mark.
  6. Clean Up: Neutralize the acid on the stone using water and baking soda, then wipe it clean with a paper towel.

FAQ

Are acid test kits accurate?

Yes, acid test kits are very accurate for determining the surface karat of gold. However, they only test the part of the metal you scratch off. To ensure an item isn't thickly plated over a base metal, you may need to make a deeper file mark before scratching the stone.

Can acid testing damage my jewelry?

The testing acid itself does not damage the jewelry because the acid is applied to the stone, not the item. However, the act of scratching the jewelry against the stone does remove a microscopic amount of metal and leaves a small scratch mark.

How long do testing acids last?

Nitric acid solutions typically have a shelf life of about 6 to 12 months once opened. Over time, the acid loses its potency, which can lead to false readings. It's best to store them in a cool, dark place and replace them annually.

What's the difference between testing gold and silver?

Gold is tested by seeing which karat acid dissolves it. Silver testing usually involves a specific silver testing solution (often containing potassium dichromate). When applied to a silver scratch, the acid turns a specific color (e.g., bright red for fine silver, dark red for sterling) to indicate purity.
