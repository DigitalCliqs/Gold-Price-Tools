How the .900 coin silver price per gram is calculated

Silver is quoted globally in USD per troy ounce on COMEX (New York) and via the LBMA Silver Price benchmark in London. The remaining 10% is copper — added by US Mint metallurgists for durability in circulation.

Identification by date

No hallmarks — identify .900 silver by year

Unlike Continental European silverware, US circulating coins do not carry a fineness hallmark. The mint year determines composition. Use a coin loupe to verify the date clearly, then cross-reference with the four eras below.

90% silver
≤1964
Pre-1965 dimes, quarters, halves

Any Roosevelt or Mercury Dime, Washington Quarter, or Walking Liberty / Franklin / 1964 Kennedy Half Dollar dated 1964 or earlier is 90% silver. The cleanest scrap-silver identification rule in US numismatics.

Transition year
1964 / 1965
The Coinage Act cutoff

The Coinage Act of 1965 ended 90% silver in dimes and quarters. From 1965 onward, dimes and quarters became copper-nickel clad with no silver content. Edge appearance is the giveaway — clad coins show a distinct copper stripe; silver coins show solid white edges.

Kennedy Halves (intermediate)

Kennedy Half Dollars dated 1965 through 1970 are a unique 40% silver alloy — not 90%, not zero. They contain 4.6g pure silver per coin (0.1479 oz). The 1976 Bicentennial Half and Quarter (in Proof & Mint sets) also carry 40% silver clad.

What is .900 coin silver?

.900 coin silver is an alloy of 90.0% pure silver and 10.0% copper, the United States Mint's standard for circulating silver coinage from 1792 through 1964. The added copper produced a harder, more wear-resistant coin able to withstand daily handling. The "coin silver" name distinguishes it from sterling .925 (UK/US jewellery and flatware) and .999 fine bullion. It is also the standard for Morgan (1878–1904, 1921) and Peace Dollars (1921–1935), and was used historically for Scandinavian silverware.

How the LBMA spot price drives .900 coin silver value

Silver's global benchmark is the LBMA Silver Price, an electronic auction conducted at 12:00 GMT daily by ICE Benchmark Administration. Throughout the trading day, the live spot price is set by COMEX (New York) silver futures, with significant activity in Shanghai (SHFE) and London OTC markets. How to identify .900 US coin silver

US circulating coins do not carry a fineness hallmark. Identification is by mint year and denomination. The five rules to remember:

  • Pre-1965 dimes — Roosevelt (1946–1964), Mercury (1916–1945) and earlier Barber dimes (1892–1916) are all 90% silver, 2.5g total weight.
  • Pre-1965 quarters — Washington (1932–1964) and earlier Standing Liberty (1916–1930) quarters are 90% silver, 6.25g total.
  • Pre-1965 half dollars — Walking Liberty (1916–1947), Franklin (1948–1963) and 1964 Kennedy are 90% silver, 12.5g total. 1965–1970 Kennedys are 40% silver; post-1970 Kennedys are clad copper-nickel.
  • Silver dollars — Morgan (1878–1904, 1921) and Peace (1921–1935) are 90% silver, 26.73g. Eisenhower business strikes (1971–1978) are clad; only Mint & Proof Eisenhowers carry 40% silver.
  • Edge test — Hold the coin edge-on. Solid silver coins show a uniform white edge; copper-nickel clad coins display a distinct copper-coloured stripe sandwiched between two nickel-white layers.

How to calculate .900 coin silver melt value

The formula is:

.900 silver USD/g = (Spot USD/oz ÷ 31.1035) × 0.900

For example, at a silver spot price of $33.00 per troy ounce:

  • Pure silver USD/g = $33.00 ÷ 31.1035 = $1.061 / gram
  • .900 coin silver USD/g = $1.061 × 0.900 = $0.955 / gram
  • A single Morgan Dollar (26.73g) = $0.955 × 26.73 = $25.52 melt value
  • $1,000 face value junk silver bag (~715 oz pure silver) = $23,595 melt value

Junk silver — the $1 face rule
$1 face value of any combination of 90% silver dimes, quarters or halves contains theoretically 0.7234 troy oz of pure silver. In practice, the trade-standard figure is 0.715 oz per $1 face — the difference accounts for typical circulation wear. A $1,000 face value bag holds about 715 troy oz of silver. This is the most-quoted figure in the precious metals trade.

.900 coin silver vs sterling .925 — the math

Sterling silver (.925) contains 92.5% pure silver, making it only 2.8% richer in silver content per gram than .900. The melt-value ratio: 0.900 ÷ 0.925 = 97.3%. So a gram of .900 coin silver is worth about 97.3% of sterling. Other differences:

  • Hardness & durability — .900 was specifically selected by US Mint metallurgists for circulating coinage. Higher copper content makes it harder and more wear-resistant than sterling.
  • Tarnish rate — .900 tarnishes slightly faster than .925 due to higher copper content. Vintage coins develop characteristic patina (toning) that can increase collector value.
  • Colour — .900 has a subtle warmer, slightly duller hue than sterling. Old toned Morgans display rainbow / album toning prized by numismatists.
  • Use case — .925 is the jewellery/flatware standard; .900 is the US circulating coinage standard. Different markets, different premiums.
  • Numismatic value — .900 coins carry significant collector premiums for key dates. .925 sterling is mostly valued at scrap/melt with few collector items.
Verifying authenticity
Genuine .900 US coin silver is non-magnetic. If your coin sticks to a magnet, it is plated or counterfeit. Authentic pre-1965 dimes, quarters and halves have a distinct ring when dropped on a hard surface — called the "silver ping" by collectors. The visual edge test is most reliable: solid white edge = silver; copper stripe = clad. For high-value coins (especially Morgan Dollars), use PCGS or NGC grading for guaranteed authenticity.

How much silver is in a pre-1965 US quarter, dime or half dollar?
Pre-1965 US 90% silver coins contain: Roosevelt/Mercury Dime — 2.25g pure silver (0.07234 troy oz); Washington Quarter — 5.625g (0.18084 troy oz); Walking Liberty/Franklin/1964 Kennedy Half Dollar — 11.25g (0.36169 troy oz); Morgan/Peace Dollar — 24.057g (0.77344 troy oz). The trade convention for $1 face value of junk silver is 0.715 troy oz of pure silver content accounting for typical circulation wear.
How do I identify .900 US silver coins?
US coins do not carry a hallmark stamp. Identify .900 coin silver by date: any Roosevelt or Mercury Dime, Washington Quarter, or Walking Liberty / Franklin / Kennedy Half Dollar minted 1964 or earlier is 90% silver. Morgan (1878–1904, 1921) and Peace Dollars (1921–1935) are also 90% silver. 1965–1970 Kennedy Halves are 40% silver — a distinct intermediate alloy. Post-1971 dimes and quarters are copper-nickel clad with no silver content. The edge test (solid white edge vs copper stripe) is the fastest visual check.
What is junk silver and how do I calculate $1 face value?
Junk silver refers to circulated pre-1965 US 90% silver coins with no numismatic premium — valued purely for silver content. $1 face value of any 90% combination (10 dimes, 4 quarters, 2 halves) contains approximately 0.715 troy oz of pure silver (the trade-standard figure accounting for normal coin wear; uncirculated coins technically hold 0.7234 oz). At today's spot, a $1,000 face value bag holds about 715 troy oz of silver.
Is .900 coin silver the same as sterling silver (.925)?
No. .900 coin silver is 90.0% pure silver with 10.0% copper, historically used for US circulating coinage. Sterling silver (.925) is 92.5% pure and is the UK/US jewellery and flatware standard. By melt value, .900 coin silver is worth approximately 97.3% of sterling per gram (0.900 ÷ 0.925). The added copper in .900 produced harder, more wear-resistant coinage.
What is the difference between Morgan and Peace silver dollars?
Both Morgan (1878–1904, 1921) and Peace (1921–1935) Dollars are .900 silver with identical 26.73g total weight and 24.057g (0.77344 troy oz) pure silver content. Morgan Dollars feature Lady Liberty's portrait by George T. Morgan; Peace Dollars feature Anthony de Francisci's Liberty commemorating the end of WWI. Both share identical melt value — but numismatic premium for rare dates (1893-S Morgan, 1928 Peace, 1934-S Peace) can multiply melt 5×–100× at auction.
Why is silver priced in USD globally?
Silver is quoted in US dollars per troy ounce on the COMEX futures exchange and via the LBMA Silver Price daily auction in London. USD is the world's reserve currency and the most liquid quote unit for precious metals globally.
