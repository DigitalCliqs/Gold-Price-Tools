What Is the Gold/Silver Ratio?

The gold/silver ratio tells you how many ounces of silver are needed to buy one ounce of gold. If gold is $3,300/oz and silver is $33/oz, the ratio is 100:1. It is one of the oldest benchmarks in precious metals markets, with records dating back to ancient Egypt and Rome, where the ratio was fixed by law at 12:1 or 13:1.

Today the ratio floats freely with market prices. The 20th-century average is approximately 47:1, but the ratio has ranged from as low as 14:1 (1980) to over 120:1 (March 2020 at the height of the COVID-19 panic). A high ratio is often interpreted as silver being undervalued relative to gold.

How to Read the Ratio

Investors use the gold/silver ratio in two main ways:

  • Relative value signal: When the ratio is historically high (e.g. above 80), silver is considered cheap relative to gold. When it is low (below 50), gold is relatively cheap.
  • Ratio trading: Some traders buy the "cheap" metal and sell the "expensive" one, aiming to profit when the ratio reverts toward its historical mean.

The ratio is calculated simply: Ratio = Gold spot price ÷ Silver spot price. Our live calculator above updates this automatically every 60 seconds.

Why Does the Ratio Matter for UK Investors?

For UK investors, the gold/silver ratio has a practical dimension beyond market timing. Gold bullion is VAT-exempt in the UK, whereas silver bullion is subject to 20% VAT. This means silver needs to outperform gold by more than 20% just to break even on a tax-adjusted basis for UK buyers of physical metals.

Silver also tends to be more volatile — it typically rises faster than gold in bull markets and falls harder in bear markets. This amplified performance means the ratio often reaches extremes during market stress (high ratio = silver underperforms) and during commodity booms (low ratio = silver outperforms).

Gold/Silver Ratio Trading Strategies

Common approaches used by precious metals investors:

  • Buy silver when ratio is high: Accumulate silver (or silver ETFs) when the ratio is above 80–100, expecting reversion toward the mean. Switch back to gold when the ratio normalises.
  • Hold physical gold as the base: Use gold as the primary store of value and trade a smaller allocation between gold and silver based on ratio signals.
  • ETFs and managed funds: iShares Physical Silver (SSLN), WisdomTree Silver (SLVR), and Sprott Physical Silver Trust offer UK-accessible silver exposure without VAT complications.
  • Royal Mint silver coins: UK legal tender silver coins (e.g. Britannia) are CGT-exempt, making them attractive for UK investors despite the VAT on purchase.

Why is the gold/silver ratio so much higher today than in ancient times?

In ancient times, silver was the primary monetary metal and was relatively scarce. Modern silver mining produces large quantities of silver as a by-product of other mining, increasing supply. Meanwhile, gold's role as the global monetary reserve asset underpins sustained demand, widening the ratio over time.

Understanding the Gold-Silver Ratio

The gold-silver ratio is a simple but powerful metric used by precious metals investors. It represents how many ounces of silver it takes to purchase a single ounce of gold at current spot prices. For example, if gold is trading at $2,000 per ounce and silver is trading at $25 per ounce, the ratio is 80:1.

How to Use the Ratio

Historically, the ratio has fluctuated significantly. Many investors use it as a timing indicator to decide which metal to buy or sell:

  • High Ratio: When the ratio is historically high (e.g., above 80), it means silver is relatively cheap compared to gold. Investors might choose to buy silver.
  • Low Ratio: When the ratio is historically low (e.g., below 50), it means gold is relatively cheap compared to silver. Investors might prefer to buy gold.

Historical Context

In ancient times, the ratio was often fixed by governments or natural occurrence. The Roman Empire officially set the ratio at 12:1. In 1792, the US Coinage Act fixed the ratio at 15:1. However, in the modern era of free-floating fiat currencies, the ratio has been highly volatile, averaging around 60:1 over the last century but spiking above 110:1 during market panics (like early 2020) and dropping below 35:1 during precious metals bull markets (like 2011).
