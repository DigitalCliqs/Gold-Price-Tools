What Is the Gold/Silver Ratio?

The gold/silver ratio tells you how many ounces of silver are needed to buy one ounce of gold. If gold trades at $3,300/oz and silver at $33/oz, the ratio is 100:1. It is one of the oldest benchmarks in precious metals — records date back to ancient Egypt and Rome, where the ratio was fixed by law at 12:1 or 13:1.

Today the ratio floats freely with market prices. The 20th-century average is approximately 47:1, but the ratio has ranged from as low as 14:1 (1980 Hunt Brothers silver peak) to over 120:1 (March 2020 COVID-19 panic). A high ratio is often interpreted as silver being undervalued relative to gold — and vice versa.

“A ratio above 80 historically meant silver was on sale. Below 50 meant gold was. Mean reversion is rarely fast — but it has been remarkably consistent.” — GoldPriceTools Market Analysis

How to Read the Ratio

Investors interpret the gold/silver ratio in two practical ways:

  • Relative value signal — when the ratio is historically high (above 80), silver is considered cheap relative to gold; when low (below 50), gold is relatively cheap.
  • Ratio trading — some traders buy the "cheap" metal and sell the "expensive" one, aiming to profit when the ratio reverts toward its historical mean of about 60:1.

The ratio is calculated simply: Ratio = Gold spot price ÷ Silver spot price. Our live calculator above updates this automatically every 60 seconds.

Why the Ratio Matters for UK Investors

For UK investors, the gold/silver ratio has a practical dimension beyond market timing — and the tax code changes everything.

UK VAT & CGT — The Hidden Ratio

Gold bullion is VAT-exempt in the UK. Silver bullion is subject to 20% VAT. This means silver needs to outperform gold by more than 20% just to break even on a tax-adjusted basis for UK physical-metal buyers.

Silver is also more volatile — typically rising faster than gold in bull markets and falling harder in bear markets. The ratio reaches extremes during market stress (high ratio = silver underperforms) and during commodity booms (low ratio = silver outperforms).

Common Trading Strategies

Four approaches precious-metals investors use when interpreting the ratio:

Buy silver when ratio is high

Accumulate silver (or silver ETFs) when the ratio is above 80–100. Switch back to gold when the ratio normalises toward 60.

Hold physical gold as the base

Use gold as the primary store of value and trade a smaller allocation between gold and silver based on ratio signals.

ETFs and managed funds

iShares Physical Silver (SSLN), WisdomTree Silver (SLVR), and Sprott Physical Silver Trust give UK investors silver exposure without VAT complications.

Royal Mint silver coins

UK legal-tender silver coins (Britannia, Sovereign) are CGT-exempt — attractive for UK investors despite the VAT on purchase.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the Gold-Silver Ratio?

The gold-silver ratio is the amount of silver it takes to purchase one ounce of gold. It is calculated by dividing the current spot price of gold by the current spot price of silver.

Why is the Gold-Silver Ratio important?

Investors use the ratio to evaluate the relative value of gold and silver. A high ratio suggests silver may be undervalued compared to gold, while a low ratio suggests gold may be undervalued.

What is a high gold-silver ratio?

Historically, a gold-silver ratio above 80 is considered high, suggesting silver is relatively cheap. The ratio reached an all-time high of around 124 during the March 2020 market crash. In the 20th century, the average ratio was approximately 47:1.

How do investors use the gold-silver ratio?

Investors use the ratio as a switching signal — when very high (above 80), some switch from gold to silver expecting silver to outperform. When low (below 50), they may switch back to gold. This is known as ratio trading.

What is the historical average gold-silver ratio?

The long-run historical average is approximately 60:1 over the past century. The natural abundance ratio in the Earth's crust is about 17.5:1. Since the end of the gold standard, the ratio has generally stayed between 40 and 80, with notable spikes during financial crises.

Does a rising gold-silver ratio mean gold is outperforming silver?

Yes. A rising ratio means gold is appreciating faster than silver (or silver is falling faster than gold). During risk-off periods gold typically outperforms, raising the ratio. Silver tends to outperform during industrial expansions and bull markets, pushing the ratio lower.

What is the gold/silver ratio today?

The live ratio is shown in the primary card above, updated every 60 seconds. It is calculated by dividing the current gold spot price (USD/oz) by the current silver spot price (USD/oz).

Should I buy gold or silver based on the ratio?

A high ratio (above 80) historically favours silver as the undervalued metal; a low ratio (below 50) historically favours gold. However, ratio trading is speculative and ratios can remain at extremes for extended periods. UK investors must also account for VAT on silver. This is not financial advice — consult a qualified adviser.

Why is the ratio so much higher today than in ancient times?

In ancient times, silver was the primary monetary metal and was relatively scarce. Modern silver mining produces large quantities as a by-product of other mining, increasing supply. Meanwhile, gold's role as the global monetary reserve asset underpins sustained demand, widening the ratio over time.
