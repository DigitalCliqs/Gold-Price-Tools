Gold held firmly above **$4,500 an ounce** through the week, consolidating its
April gains as traders pared back bets on aggressive rate cuts. With the Federal
Reserve signalling it is in no hurry to ease further, the metal's next move hinges
on real yields, the dollar, and the steady drumbeat of central-bank buying.

## What moved the market

Two forces kept a floor under prices. First, **official-sector demand** stayed
strong — emerging-market central banks continued to diversify reserves away from
the dollar, a structural bid that has underpinned gold for three straight years.
Second, a **softer dollar** made bullion cheaper for non-US buyers, supporting
physical demand across Asia and the Middle East.

Working the other way, the Fed's "higher for longer" tone lifted real yields
modestly, which usually weighs on a non-yielding asset like gold. That the metal
shrugged it off says a lot about the resilience of the current bid.

## The levels to watch

- **Support:** the $4,460–$4,480 zone, which has been defended on every dip.
- **Resistance:** the prior high near $4,620; a weekly close above it opens the
  door to a fresh record.
- **Gold-to-silver ratio:** narrowing again as silver outperforms — historically a
  sign of broadening precious-metals strength.

## What to watch next week

Keep an eye on the next inflation print and any commentary from Fed officials.
A cooler-than-expected CPI would revive rate-cut hopes and could be the catalyst
for a breakout. For now, gold looks comfortable digesting its gains rather than
giving them back.
