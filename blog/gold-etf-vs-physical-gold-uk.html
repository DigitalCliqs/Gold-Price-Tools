Gold is having a moment. After surpassing $5,100 per troy ounce in early 2026 — a gain of nearly 18% in a matter of weeks — the precious metal has firmly cemented itself as one of the standout asset classes of recent years. For UK investors wondering how best to get involved, the central question is almost always the same: should you buy physical gold, or is a gold ETF (technically an ETC in the UK) the smarter move?[1]

The honest answer is that it depends on who you are and what you want from gold. This guide breaks down every meaningful difference between the two approaches — costs, taxes, liquidity, risk, and how each fits into a modern UK portfolio — so you can make an informed decision in 2026.

Defining the product

What Is a Gold ETF (or ETC)?

In the UK, what most investors call a "gold ETF" is, technically speaking, an Exchange Traded Commodity (ETC) rather than a fund. Under EU-derived UCITS regulations still applicable in Britain, a fund cannot hold a single underlying asset — which means a pure gold product must be structured as a certificate rather than a traditional fund. The practical difference for investors is minimal, but it's worth knowing the correct terminology.[2]

A physically backed gold ETC is a debt security issued by a financial institution and collateralised with real gold bars stored in secure vaults — typically in London. When you buy shares in one, you are not purchasing gold itself, but a financial instrument whose value is pegged to the spot price of gold. These products trade on the London Stock Exchange just like ordinary shares, meaning you can buy and sell during market hours through any standard brokerage account or Stocks and Shares ISA.[3]

The tangible alternative

What Is Physical Gold?

Physical gold means exactly what it sounds like — gold you can hold in your hands. In the UK, that typically means:

  • Gold bars, ranging from small 1g bars up to the institutional 400oz (12.5kg) good delivery bar
  • Gold coins, most notably the British Britannia and the Sovereign, both struck by the Royal Mint

The Royal Mint itself sells coins and bars ranging from around £100 to over £130,000, with insured UK delivery and optional secure vault storage. Other major UK bullion dealers include BullionByPost, Sharps Pixley, and Tavex.[10]

The key attraction of physical gold is absolute ownership. When you hold a gold coin or bar, there is no counterparty, no fund manager, no custodian bank that could fail. The gold is yours, outright, and its value does not depend on anyone else's promises being kept.[11][12]

Tracking mechanics

Why the Gold Price vs Gold ETF Price Can Differ

One of the most common questions from newcomers is why there sometimes appears to be a gap between the gold spot price and the price of a gold ETC. There are a few reasons for this:

  1. The management fee drag: Every day, a tiny fraction of your ETC's gold entitlement is deducted to pay the fund's running costs (the TER). Over time, this means each unit represents slightly less gold than it did when you bought it.[3]
  2. Currency effects: Most gold ETCs are priced in US dollars at the underlying level. UK investors see the price expressed in pounds, so the GBP/USD exchange rate also influences what you observe day to day.
  3. Bid-ask spreads: On any given day, you will pay slightly more to buy units than you receive when you sell them. For large, liquid ETCs like SGLN, this spread is extremely narrow.[13]
  4. Tracking error: Minor operational factors can cause a small, temporary divergence between an ETC's net asset value and the gold price it is meant to track.[14]

In practice, for the physically backed ETCs from major providers like iShares, Invesco, and WisdomTree, tracking is extremely tight and the price difference from gold itself is negligible for everyday investors.

Practical takeaway

For a £10,000 holding in SGLN, the daily fee drag is approximately £0.03. Over a full year, total tracking deviation against spot gold typically stays inside 0.15% — effectively indistinguishable from owning the metal directly.

The decisive factor

Gold ETFs vs Physical Gold: The Tax Picture in 2026

This is where the two approaches diverge most meaningfully for UK investors, and it is arguably the single most important factor in making a decision.

Capital Gains Tax (CGT)

For the 2026/27 tax year, the CGT annual exempt amount stands at just £3,000 — down sharply from £12,300 in 2022/23. CGT rates are 18% for basic rate taxpayers and 24% for higher and additional rate taxpayers on investment gains.[15]

Physical gold bars are fully subject to CGT when sold at a profit above the annual allowance. So are foreign bullion coins (Krugerrands, Maple Leafs, American Eagles) and most gold jewellery held as an investment.[16]

Gold ETCs held outside a tax wrapper are also subject to CGT in the same way.[17]

The Britannia & Sovereign exemption

UK legal tender gold coins — principally the Britannia and the Sovereign — are entirely exempt from CGT. Because these coins carry a sterling face value, HMRC cannot tax the movement of legal currency. This exemption has no annual cap — whether you make £5,000 or £500,000 in gains, there is no CGT liability on Britannias or Sovereigns sold by UK residents.[18][19][20]

Gold bars, ETCs, and all other coins do not share this exemption.

VAT

Investment gold in the UK is VAT-exempt, and this applies to both physical gold (bars and qualifying coins) and gold ETCs alike. Silver, by contrast, is subject to 20% VAT — an important distinction if you are considering a mixed precious metals strategy.[21][22]

The ISA and SIPP Advantage

For most UK investors, the most powerful tool available is wrapping a gold ETC inside a Stocks and Shares ISA or a SIPP. Inside either wrapper, all gains and income are completely sheltered from CGT and income tax.[23]

For the 2026/27 tax year, the ISA allowance remains at £20,000 and the SIPP annual allowance at £60,000. A basic rate taxpayer contributing to a SIPP receives 20% tax relief — meaning a £100 contribution effectively costs £80 — while a higher rate taxpayer can claim 40% relief.[24][25][15]

You cannot hold physical gold coins or bars inside an ISA or SIPP. This is a significant structural advantage for ETCs over physical gold for investors who are not buying UK legal tender coins.

Worked example

If you invest £20,000 in SGLN inside a Stocks and Shares ISA at the start of the tax year and the gold price doubles over five years, you pay zero CGT on the entire gain. The same investment held in a General Investment Account would leave you with a potentially large CGT bill.[26][23]

Gold ETC costs

The total cost of holding a gold ETC typically includes the annual management fee (TER), platform fees (most ISA and SIPP platforms charge 0.15%–0.45% per year to hold ETFs/ETCs), and trading commissions (often zero on modern platforms, though some charge a flat fee per trade).[13][7][5]

For a £10,000 investment in SGLN, the annual management fee is just £12 — genuinely minimal.

Physical gold costs

Physical gold carries a different set of costs. For popular items like the 1oz Britannia, premiums typically run 2–5% above spot.[19] Home storage is free but risky; a bank safe deposit box or professional vault typically costs £50–£200+ per year; third-party insurance for gold stored at home generally runs 1–2% of the gold's value annually.[27][28] Dealers typically buy back at a small discount to spot.

For a £10,000 physical gold holding stored in a professional vault with insurance, you might realistically pay 0.5–2% per year in combined storage and insurance costs — comparable to or slightly above ETC fees for smaller holdings, but with different ownership characteristics.

Often overlooked

Physically backed ETCs are also insured and stored by the fund. The difference is who bears those costs and who controls the gold — not whether they exist at all.

Time to cash

Liquidity: How Quickly Can You Access Your Money?

This is a clear win for gold ETCs. You can sell SGLN, SGLP, or any of the major ETCs during London Stock Exchange hours and have the cash proceeds in your account within the standard T+2 settlement window — just like selling any share.[29]

Selling physical gold is more involved. You need to contact a bullion dealer, arrange for secure delivery or collection, wait for the price to be agreed, and wait for the funds to transfer. For large holdings, this can take days. In a genuine financial emergency, that delay matters.

That said, physical gold stored with providers like BullionVault or directly through the Royal Mint's vault service can be sold relatively quickly, though still not as instantaneously as clicking "sell" on a brokerage platform.

Trust & control

Counterparty Risk: The Argument for Real Gold

Here is where physical gold's advocates make their strongest case. Gold ETCs, despite being "physically backed," involve multiple layers of counterparty exposure:

  • The ETF/ETC issuer (e.g., BlackRock, Invesco, WisdomTree)
  • The custodian bank holding the gold (e.g., JP Morgan Chase for SGLP)
  • Your brokerage or platform
  • The exchange settlement infrastructure

If any of these parties were to fail in extreme circumstances, recovery could be complicated and time-consuming. The gold exists, but accessing it in a systemic crisis — the very scenario some gold investors are preparing for — is not straightforward.[30][3]

Physical gold in your possession involves none of this. A gold Sovereign in a home safe is yours without qualification, regardless of what happens to any bank, broker, or fund.[11]

Why central banks are doing the same

This is not merely theoretical. It is the reason central banks globally have been steadily moving gold reserves back to domestic vaults and away from custodians in other countries. The same logic — at a retail level — leads some UK investors to maintain a portion of their gold in physical form even if they hold the majority via ETCs.

Leveraged exposure

Gold Mining ETFs: A Different Proposition Entirely

Beyond physically backed ETCs, UK investors can also access gold miners ETFs — funds that hold shares in companies that extract gold from the ground rather than the metal itself.

The leading option for UK investors is the VanEck Gold Miners UCITS ETF (GDGB), which tracks major miners including Newmont, Agnico Eagle, and Barrick Mining, and carries a TER of 0.53% and over £2.6 billion in assets. The iShares Gold Producers ETF follows a similar approach.[31]

These are fundamentally different investments from a physical gold ETC. Their performance in 2025 illustrates this vividly: gold mining ETFs returned approximately 96–110% while physical gold ETCs returned around 30% in sterling terms. The amplification works in both directions — miners fell much harder than gold in down years like 2023.[32]

The reason for this disparity is that mining companies have operating leverage. When the gold price rises, revenue rises while fixed costs remain broadly stable, making profitability expand at a higher rate than gold itself. They also carry risks entirely absent from owning gold: operational risk, geopolitical risk in mining jurisdictions, management decisions, environmental liabilities, and currency exposure from mining in multiple countries.

Mining ETFs are appropriate for investors who want leveraged exposure to the gold price and are comfortable with equity-level volatility. They also offer potential dividend income, which physical gold and ETCs do not. For pure gold price exposure and portfolio diversification, physically backed ETCs remain the cleaner choice.[32]

2025 performance gap

Gold miners returned 96–110% in 2025 against ~30% for physical gold ETCs in GBP terms. The same leverage works in reverse in down years — miners are not a substitute for a core gold allocation, they're an amplified satellite position.

Specialist instruments

Leveraged and Inverse Gold ETFs: Specialist Tools Only

Completeness requires a mention of leveraged gold ETFs (such as the WisdomTree Gold 2x Daily Leveraged ETC, LBUL, with a TER of 0.98%) and inverse gold ETFs — products designed to deliver a multiple of gold's daily return, or to profit when gold falls.[33]

These are sophisticated instruments built for short-term trading, not long-term investment. An inverse gold ETC profits when gold falls; a 2x leveraged ETC attempts to deliver twice gold's daily return. Both use derivatives rather than physical gold.[34][33]

The daily reset trap

Because leveraged and inverse products rebalance daily, the compounding effect in volatile markets means their actual returns over weeks or months can diverge significantly from the multiple of gold's return over that period — and not in your favour. These products are not suitable for long-term investors and carry significantly higher risk than either physical gold or standard ETCs.[34]

Portfolio sizing

Gold Allocation: How Much Should You Hold?

Whether you choose ETCs, physical gold, or a combination, the question of how much gold to hold is equally important.

Most financial advisors and institutional analysts recommend 5% to 15% of a portfolio in gold, with 10% serving as a widely cited benchmark. World Gold Council research shows that a 5–10% gold allocation historically improved Sharpe ratios — meaning better risk-adjusted returns — compared to portfolios with no gold exposure.[37]

From plan to action

Practical Next Steps for UK Investors

Getting started is straightforward:

For gold ETCs

Open or use a Stocks and Shares ISA with any major UK platform (Hargreaves Lansdown, AJ Bell, Interactive Investor, InvestEngine, Vanguard). Search for SGLN, SGLP, or RMAU. You can invest from as little as £1 on some platforms.

For physical gold

The Royal Mint is the most trusted source for UK investors, offering both coins and bars with secure vault storage options. BullionByPost and Sharps Pixley are reputable alternatives.[39]

Tax planning

If you are a higher-rate taxpayer with significant gold holdings outside a wrapper, consider the "bed and ISA" strategy — selling holdings to crystallise gains within your annual CGT allowance and repurchasing inside an ISA each tax year.[15]

Portfolio sizing

Start with a target allocation of 5–10% of your total investable assets. Build the position gradually rather than all at once, particularly given gold's strong run in 2025–2026.[37]

Common questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a gold ETF in the UK?

In the UK, what investors call a "gold ETF" is technically an Exchange Traded Commodity (ETC). UCITS rules prevent a fund from holding a single asset, so pure gold products are structured as debt securities collateralised by physical gold bars in London vaults. They trade on the LSE like ordinary shares. Major UK options include iShares SGLN, Invesco SGLP, WisdomTree PHAU and Royal Mint RMAU.

Is physical gold or a gold ETF better for UK investors in 2026?

It depends on the size and purpose of your allocation. For most UK investors, a physically backed ETC like SGLN inside a Stocks & Shares ISA is the cheapest, most tax-efficient and most liquid option. Larger investors who want true wealth preservation and unlimited CGT exemption often combine ETCs with Royal Mint Britannias or Sovereigns, which are legal tender and entirely CGT-free.

Can I hold gold ETCs in an ISA or SIPP?

Yes. Physically backed gold ETCs such as SGLN, SGLP, PHAU and RMAU are eligible for both Stocks & Shares ISAs and SIPPs. Inside either wrapper, all gains are sheltered from CGT and income tax. The 2026/27 ISA allowance is £20,000 and the SIPP annual allowance is £60,000. Physical gold coins and bars cannot be held in an ISA or SIPP directly.

Are Britannia and Sovereign coins really CGT exempt?

Yes. Britannias and Sovereigns are UK legal tender, so any capital gains on them are entirely exempt from CGT for UK residents — with no upper limit. This is unique to UK legal tender coins; foreign coins such as Krugerrands, Maple Leafs and American Eagles are not exempt, and gold bars and ETCs held outside an ISA/SIPP are also subject to CGT.

What is the TER of the cheapest UK gold ETC?

The two cheapest physically backed gold ETCs on the London Stock Exchange in 2026 are iShares Physical Gold ETC (SGLN) and Invesco Physical Gold ETC (SGLP), both with a Total Expense Ratio of 0.12% per year. WisdomTree PHAU charges 0.39% and Royal Mint RMAU charges 0.22%.

How much gold should a UK investor hold?

Most financial advisers and the World Gold Council suggest 5–15% of a portfolio in gold, with 10% widely cited as a benchmark. A 5–10% allocation has historically improved Sharpe ratios versus portfolios with zero gold. Conservative investors typically lean to 5–10%, balanced investors 7–12%, and growth investors can incorporate some gold miners ETF exposure on top of physical and ETC core positions.

Do gold ETFs have counterparty risk?

Yes — even physically backed gold ETCs involve multiple layers of counterparty exposure: the issuer, the custodian bank holding the gold, the brokerage, and exchange settlement. In normal markets these risks are very low and the underlying gold is segregated. In a systemic crisis, however, accessing the metal can be complicated, which is why some UK investors hold a portion of their gold in physical Britannias or Sovereigns at home or in a vault outside the banking system.

What is the difference between a gold miners ETF and a physical gold ETC?

A physical gold ETC tracks the spot price of gold itself, while a gold miners ETF (such as VanEck GDGB) holds shares in companies that mine gold. Miners offer operating leverage to the gold price — in 2025 gold miners ETFs returned 96–110% while physical gold returned around 30% in GBP terms — but they also carry equity-level volatility, operational risk and geopolitical risk that pure gold does not.
