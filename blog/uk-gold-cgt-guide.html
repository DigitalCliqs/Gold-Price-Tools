Capital Gains Tax on Gold in the UK

Key point

Gold bars, foreign coins (Krugerrands, Maples, Eagles) and gold ETFs held outside tax wrappers are subject to CGT in the UK. Gold Sovereigns and Gold Britannias — and other Royal Mint legal tender coins — are entirely exempt from CGT, with no cap on the amount of gain.

If you own gold in the UK, one of the most important things you can know about your investment has nothing to do with the gold price itself — it is understanding exactly when Capital Gains Tax applies, when it does not, and what you can do to legitimately reduce or eliminate your tax bill. The rules are more favourable than many investors realise, but only if you own the right type of gold in the first place.

This guide lays out the full picture for the 2026/27 tax year: the CGT rates, the annual exemption, precisely which gold is exempt and why, how to calculate tax on gold bars and foreign coins, and the legal strategies available to reduce your CGT exposure to zero.

What Is Capital Gains Tax on Gold?

Capital Gains Tax (CGT) is a tax charged on the profit you make when you dispose of an asset that has increased in value — not on the total proceeds of the sale. Disposing of gold means selling it, gifting it, or transferring it in most circumstances (transfers between spouses are excluded).

The critical point is that CGT is only due on the gain — the difference between what you paid (your "base cost") and what you received when you sold. If you paid £3,000 for a gold bar and sold it for £4,200, your gain is £1,200, not £4,200.

CGT does not apply automatically when you sell gold. It only becomes relevant when:

  1. The gold you own is a chargeable asset for UK CGT purposes (not all gold is); and
  2. Your total net gains across all assets in the tax year exceed the annual CGT exemption.

What this means for gold investors

With the allowance now just £3,000, even a moderate profit on a gold bar can trigger a CGT liability if you have made other gains in the same year. Careful planning around your gold holdings matters more than it ever has.

The rates themselves rose in the October 2024 Budget: basic-rate taxpayers now pay 18% (up from 10%) and higher-rate taxpayers 24% (up from 20%) on most assets, including gold bars and foreign coins. These rates apply to all disposals from 30 October 2024 onwards.

The Most Important Rule: It Depends on What You Own

Unlike income tax, which applies broadly under the same rules, CGT on gold in the UK depends heavily on the specific type of gold you hold. Two investors could sell gold of identical value on the same day and face completely different tax treatment.

Why Royal Mint coins are CGT-exempt

The legal basis

This exemption is not a loophole. It is grounded in primary legislation — the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992, s21(1)(b) — under which sterling currency is not a chargeable asset. Because Royal Mint gold coins are UK legal tender with a face value in pounds sterling, they fall outside the scope of CGT entirely.

Practical implication: you could buy £500,000 of Gold Sovereigns today, sell them in ten years for £2,000,000, and owe zero CGT on a £1.5 million profit — regardless of your income, tax band, or whether you have used your annual allowance.

The key test is simple: was it struck by The Royal Mint, and does it carry a face value in sterling? If yes to both, it is CGT-exempt. Foreign gold coins are not exempt even though they may be legal tender in their own countries — HMRC is explicit that coins which are currency but not sterling remain chargeable assets for UK residents, so Krugerrands, Maple Leafs, Eagles, Buffalos and Kangaroos are all taxable on the gain.

VAT on Gold: A Separate (and Simpler) Question

VAT is often confused with CGT by new investors. Investment-grade gold is VAT-exempt in the UK under HMRC VAT Notice 701/21A. To qualify, gold must:

  • have a purity of at least 995/1000 (99.5% fine) for bars; or
  • for coins: be minted after 1800, be at least 900/1000 fine, and be (or have been) legal tender in its country of origin.

This means most investment gold bars and bullion coins — including foreign coins like Krugerrands, Maples and Eagles — are VAT-free to buy. Silver, platinum and palladium bullion, by contrast, carry 20% VAT in the UK — a key structural difference between the metals.

The sweet spot

Britannias and Sovereigns are both CGT-exempt and VAT-exempt at the same time — making them uniquely tax-efficient among all investment assets available in the UK.

How to Calculate CGT on Taxable Gold

If you hold gold bars, Krugerrands, Maple Leafs or other taxable gold, here is exactly how CGT is worked out when you sell.

1
Calculate the gain

Deduct what you paid and your allowable costs (purchase price, reasonable selling costs, and — arguably — directly associated insurance; seek advice) from the sale proceeds.

Gain = Sale proceeds − Purchase price − Allowable costs
2
Subtract the annual exemption

Take off your £3,000 allowance if you have not already used it this tax year.

Taxable gain = Total gain − £3,000
3
Apply your CGT rate

18% if your total income is below £50,270 (basic rate); 24% if above it (higher or additional rate).

Do Gold Bars Have a Chattel Exemption?

A lesser-known relief is the chattel exemption: personal possessions ("chattels") sold for £6,000 or less are generally exempt from CGT. For gold investors this helps with smaller items — if a single small bar or piece of jewellery sells for £6,000 or less, no CGT arises on the disposal regardless of the gain.

However, HMRC looks at each item individually, and splitting a large holding into many small pieces does not create multiple £6,000 exemptions if HMRC treats them as a related "set". For items sold above £6,000, the chattel rules offer partial taper relief rather than full exemption.

Are Gold Bars CGT-Exempt in the UK?

No. Gold bars are not CGT-exempt — not even LBMA Good Delivery bars, not even Royal Mint gold bars. The CGT exemption applies exclusively to gold coins that qualify as UK legal tender, and bars, by definition, are not legal tender.

This trips up many investors who assume the investment-gold VAT exemption (which does apply to bars) and the CGT exemption are the same thing. They are not — they are completely separate rules, and only coins with a sterling face value qualify for the CGT exemption.

The £23,280 difference

A UK investor holding 10 kg of gold bars who makes £100,000 profit could face a CGT bill of up to £23,280 ((£100,000 − £3,000) × 24%). The same investor holding 10 kg of Gold Sovereigns with identical profit would owe nothing.

CGT on Gold Sovereigns: A Special Case Worth Knowing

Gold Sovereigns deserve particular attention: they are simultaneously one of the most liquid, most widely recognised and most tax-efficient gold investments available to UK investors.

Sovereigns have been minted continuously since 1817 (with minor interruptions), and all post-1837 Sovereigns carry a £1 face value, making them UK legal tender. Every gold Sovereign — a 2026 bullion Sovereign, a 1953 Elizabeth II, or a Victorian-era piece — is completely CGT-exempt when sold at a profit. A coin-of-the-realm is a coin-of-the-realm regardless of its date; the full, half, double and quintuple Sovereign all qualify.

The triple advantage

Combined with their VAT-exempt status, Sovereigns offer something almost unique in the UK tax system: no VAT on purchase, no CGT on sale, and no upper limit on tax-free gains.

How to Avoid CGT on Gold — 7 Legal Strategies

For investors holding taxable gold (bars, foreign coins or unwrapped ETFs), there are several legitimate ways to reduce or eliminate the liability.

Buy CGT-exempt coins, not bars

The single most effective move. Choosing Sovereigns or Britannias over bars or Krugerrands eliminates CGT entirely. For new buyers there is almost always a compelling tax case for coins.

Use your allowance every year

The £3,000 exemption is use-it-or-lose-it. Stage sales to crystallise up to £3,000 of gains tax-free each tax year (5 April deadline).

Split disposals across year-end

Selling half a large gain in March and the rest in April (a new tax year) doubles the available allowance to £6,000 with no other change.

Hold gold ETCs in a Stocks & Shares ISA

Physical metal can't go in an ISA, but gold ETCs can — and all gains inside the wrapper are CGT-free, whatever the amount. The 2026/27 ISA allowance is £20,000.

  • iShares Physical Gold ETC (SGLN) — 0.12%
  • Invesco Physical Gold (SGLD) — 0.12%
  • WisdomTree Physical Gold (PHAU) — 0.15%

Hold physical gold ETCs in a SIPP

A SIPP shelters gold ETCs from CGT, adds income-tax relief on contributions at your marginal rate, and allows a 25% tax-free lump sum. Trade-off: withdrawals above 25% are taxed as income and access is from age 57.

Transfer to a spouse or civil partner

Transfers between spouses or civil partners living together are "no gain, no loss", so a couple can combine two £3,000 allowances (£6,000) before a sale.

Offset capital losses

Losses on other assets (shares, funds, property) in the same year offset gains on taxable gold. A £5,000 stock loss cancels a £5,000 gold gain. Report losses to HMRC; they apply before the allowance.

CGT on Gold ETFs in the UK

Gold ETFs and ETCs held outside an ISA or SIPP are taxed exactly like gold bars — gains above the £3,000 allowance are taxed at 18% or 24%. Inside a Stocks & Shares ISA or SIPP, gains are completely CGT-free, which makes the ISA wrapper especially well-suited to long-term accumulation in gold ETCs — particularly for investors drip-feeding smaller amounts, where physical coin premiums would otherwise bite. Unwrapped ETCs at least avoid the storage, insurance and buy/sell-spread complications of physical holdings — but the CGT position is identical to bars.

Do You Have to Report Gold Sales to HMRC?

It depends on what you sold.

CGT-exempt coins (Sovereigns, Britannias, Royal Mint legal tender): you do not need to report the disposal. Since there is no chargeable gain, there is nothing to declare.

Taxable gold (bars, foreign coins, ETFs outside an ISA):

  • If total gains across all assets in the tax year are below £3,000, no report is required.
  • If total gains exceed £3,000 (or total disposal proceeds exceed four times the allowance — £12,000), you must report via Self Assessment.
  • CGT must be reported and paid by 31 January following the end of the tax year.
Your responsibility

The duty to report and pay CGT lies with you, not the dealer. Bullion dealers do not report sales to HMRC — keeping accurate records and filing correctly is your personal responsibility.

Record-Keeping for Gold CGT

For any taxable gold, accurate records are essential. HMRC can investigate CGT up to four years back for innocent errors, and longer for deliberate omissions. Keep the following for at least seven years from the tax year in which the disposal occurred:

  • Purchase receipts, invoices or contract notes showing the date, quantity, description and price paid;
  • Sale invoices confirming the sale date, quantity and proceeds received;
  • Storage or insurance costs potentially deductible against the gain (seek professional advice);
  • Bank statements confirming payment and receipt.

Gold CGT in Australia: A Brief Note

For non-UK readers

Australia taxes CGT on gold and precious metals similarly to the UK — gains are taxable — but residents who hold an asset for more than 12 months receive a 50% CGT discount, effectively halving the taxable gain. There is no Australian equivalent of the UK's legal-tender coin exemption. Australian investors should consult the ATO's guidance on collectables and precious-metal assets.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is gold CGT free in the UK?
It depends on the type of gold. Royal Mint legal tender gold coins — including Sovereigns and Britannias — are completely CGT-free with no limit on gains. Gold bars and foreign coins are subject to CGT on gains above the £3,000 annual allowance.
How much CGT do you pay on gold bars in the UK?
For the 2026/27 tax year: 18% if you are a basic-rate taxpayer and 24% if you are a higher or additional-rate taxpayer, charged on gains above the £3,000 annual exemption.
Are gold bars CGT exempt in the UK?
No. Gold bars are not CGT-exempt regardless of size, weight or refiner. The CGT exemption applies only to UK legal tender coins issued by The Royal Mint.
Which gold coins are CGT free in the UK?
All Royal Mint coins that qualify as UK legal tender: Gold Sovereigns (all years from 1837), Gold Britannias (all sizes from 1987), and other Royal Mint coins with a sterling face value such as the Queen's Beasts and Royal Tudor Beasts.
Are Krugerrands CGT exempt in the UK?
No. Krugerrands are South African legal tender but not UK legal tender, so they are chargeable assets and CGT applies to gains above the annual allowance.
Can you hold gold in an ISA?
You cannot hold physical gold bars or coins directly in a UK ISA. However, you can hold gold ETCs (physically backed exchange-traded commodities) within a Stocks & Shares ISA, and all gains on these are completely CGT-free. The annual ISA allowance for 2026/27 is £20,000.
What is the CGT annual allowance on gold for 2026/27?
£3,000 for individuals and £1,500 for most trusts — confirmed unchanged from 2025/26.
What is the best CGT-free gold to buy in the UK?
For most investors, Gold Sovereigns offer the best combination of CGT exemption, VAT exemption, dealer recognition and tight buy-sell spreads. Gold Britannias suit investors wanting a standard 1 troy ounce coin at 999.9 fineness.
Do I pay CGT on gold jewellery in the UK?
Gold jewellery qualifies for the chattel exemption if sold for £6,000 or less per item, in which case no CGT applies. Sales above £6,000 per item are chargeable, with gains above the annual allowance taxed at 18–24%.
Does gold CGT apply to non-UK residents?
Non-UK residents are generally not subject to UK CGT on the disposal of UK gold bullion, as it is a movable asset rather than UK property. Non-residents should take independent tax advice based on their country of residence.
