One formula, four purities

Silver is quoted globally in USD per troy ounce on COMEX (New York) and via the LBMA Silver Price benchmark in London. To get any purity per gram, divide the spot by 31.1035 (grams in a troy ounce), then multiply by the purity ratio (0.999, 0.925, 0.900 or 0.800). What is silver purity?

Silver purity is a millesimal fineness — the number of parts per thousand that are pure silver. A .999 hallmark means 999 parts pure silver, with just 1 part copper or germanium added; .925 sterling means 925 parts silver and 75 parts copper. The four most widely traded grades are .999, .925, .900 and .800. Each scales proportionally with the live LBMA spot price — the same XAG/USD figure that anchors the bullion market.

Why USD is the primary currency

Silver is quoted globally in US dollars per troy ounce on COMEX and via the LBMA London benchmark. USD is the world's reserve currency and the most liquid quote unit for precious metals. Every conversion on this page to GBP, EUR or INR is mathematically derived from the live USD spot — never the other way around. This matters for cross-page consistency: the .925 sterling figure here in GBP equals the same .925 figure on any of our purity calculators, down to the cent.

Reading silver hallmarks — the four key marks

In the UK, silver items must be hallmarked by one of the four Assay Offices (London, Birmingham, Sheffield, Edinburgh) before they can be sold as silver. Elsewhere, hallmarks are conventional but not always legally required. The fineness mark itself — the three-digit millesimal — is the universal element:

  • .999 / FINE / BRITANNIA 958 — investment bullion. .999 is global; .958 Britannia is the higher-purity UK variant introduced in 1697 to reduce coin clipping. Both trade at near-spot.
  • .925 / STERLING / lion passant — the jewellery and silverware standard. UK pieces carry a lion passant alongside the date letter and Assay Office mark; US sterling carries the word STERLING or 925.
  • .900 (no hallmark on US coins) — identification is by year and denomination. Any pre-1965 US dime, quarter or half dollar is 90% silver. See the .900 coin silver page for date-by-date identification.
  • .800 / Halbmond & Krone — the Continental antique standard. German pieces from 1888 carry the crescent moon and crown alongside the 800 stamp. Italian, French, Austrian and Dutch antiques use the same fineness with country-specific assay marks.

.999 vs .925 vs .900 vs .800 — the math

The four grades sit on a continuous scale of silver content per gram. At a silver spot of $33.00/oz, the per-gram melt values are:

.999 fine: $1.061 / g · .925 sterling: $0.981 / g · .900 coin: $0.955 / g · .800 European: $0.849 / g

Relative to .999 as the benchmark: sterling .925 = 92.5%, coin .900 = 90.0%, European .800 = 80.1%. These ratios hold at every spot price — if .999 doubles, so does every other purity. The universal calculator at the top of this page applies these factors live.

The 31.1035 rule
There are 31.1035 grams in one troy ounce of silver — not the 28.3495 grams of an avoirdupois ounce. Silver, gold and platinum are all quoted per troy oz, which is heavier. To convert spot to per gram for any purity, divide the USD/oz figure by 31.1035 first, then multiply by purity. See our troy ounce converter for a deeper dive.

.999 Fine Silver — the investment standard

At 99.9% pure, .999 fine silver is the bullion-grade standard used by every major mint. The remaining 0.1% is typically copper or germanium added for hardness during minting. .999 trades at spot — a 1 oz American Silver Eagle from the US Mint is priced at [live spot] plus a premium of 8–20% for minting, fabrication and dealer margin. LBMA Good Delivery wholesale bars (typically 1,000 troy oz) trade essentially at spot. Recognised .999 products include:

  • American Silver Eagle — 1 oz, .999 fine, minted since 1986. The bestselling silver bullion coin worldwide.
  • British Britannia — 1 oz, .999 fine since 2013 (was .958 from 1997). UK Capital Gains Tax exempt for British residents.
  • Canadian Silver Maple Leaf — 1 oz, .9999 fine (four nines). Often considered the purest minted silver coin available.
  • PAMP / Heraeus / Argor-Heraeus 1 kg bars — LBMA Good Delivery refiner bars for wholesale and serious accumulation.

.925 Sterling Silver — jewellery & flatware

Sterling silver at 92.5% pure is alloyed with 7.5% copper to dramatically improve hardness without compromising its bright white appearance. It is the global standard for silver jewellery, dinner flatware and decorative ware. The word "sterling" originates from "Easterling" silver coins traded in 12th-century England — the term was formalised in UK law in 1300. For valuation:

  • Weigh the piece in grams, then multiply weight × 0.925 to find pure silver content.
  • Multiply pure silver grams by the live .999 USD/gram figure shown in the hero above.
  • Or: skip the intermediate step — use the universal calculator with .925 selected.

Notable sterling makers include Tiffany & Co., Gorham, Mappin & Webb, Garrard, Georg Jensen, Christofle and Buccellati. Pieces from these houses can carry a 2×–10× premium over melt at auction.

.900 Coin Silver — US pre-1965 heritage

The United States Mint used 90% silver for all circulating coinage from 1792 through 1964. The remaining 10% is copper, giving coin silver the durability needed for daily handling. Identification is by year and denomination — US coins do not carry a fineness hallmark:

  • Dimes (1916–1964) — Mercury and Roosevelt designs, 2.5g total weight, 2.25g pure silver.
  • Quarters (1932–1964) — Washington design, 6.25g total, 5.625g pure silver.
  • Half Dollars (1916–1964) — Walking Liberty, Franklin and 1964 Kennedy, 12.5g total, 11.25g pure.
  • Silver Dollars (1878–1935) — Morgan and Peace Dollars, 26.73g total, 24.057g (0.7734 oz) pure silver.

Junk silver lots are traded at $1 face value = 0.715 troy oz of pure silver (accounting for circulation wear — the uncirculated figure is 0.7234 oz). For per-coin live valuation, see our .900 coin silver per gram page and the silver coins calculator.

.800 European Silver — Continental antique

From 1888 onwards, the German Empire mandated the Halbmond and Krone hallmark — a crescent moon and crown — alongside the 800 fineness number for silver items meeting the 80% purity standard. This became the de-facto Continental standard, adopted across Austria-Hungary, Italy, France (for second-quality), the Netherlands and Scandinavia.

Common .800 finds include German WMF, Wilkens, Bruckmann and Koch & Bergfeld flatware sets; Italian Buccellati-school cutlery; Austrian tea services from the Wiener Werkstätte era; and Hungarian Pest-Buda hallmarked decorative ware. Per gram at 80% silver, .800 sits at the bottom of the major trading grades but remains valuable both for melt and for antique premium when by sought-after makers.

Continental hallmark variations
Outside Germany, .800 silver carries country-specific marks: Italy — province number in a hexagonal cartouche (e.g. 1 AL for Alessandria); France — Minerva head (post-1838) for second-quality silver including some .800; Austria-Hungary — Diana head (Vienna) or Saint Stephen Crown (Hungary); Netherlands — sword (Lion's Head also used). Always weigh and verify against the 800 stamp.

Calculating scrap silver value across purities

For any silver lot, the universal scrap formula is:

Melt USD = weight (g) × (spot USD/oz ÷ 31.1035) × purity ratio

Verifying silver authenticity

Before selling at scrap value, confirm the piece is solid silver — not plated, not filled, not counterfeit. The three free tests every silver owner should know:

  1. Magnet test — genuine silver is non-magnetic. If the piece sticks to a magnet, it is silver-plated over a steel or iron base.
  2. Hallmark inspection — use a 10× loupe to read the fineness stamp. Genuine hallmarks are crisply struck; counterfeits often appear soft or shallow.
  3. Edge test (coins) — for US coins, view the edge: solid silver shows a uniform white edge, while clad shows a distinct copper-coloured stripe sandwiched between two layers.

For higher-value pieces, the gold standard is a silver acid test kit or professional XRF analysis at a refiner or assay office. Our silver test kit reviews cover home-use kits compatible with all four purities on this page.

Frequently asked questions

What are the four main silver purity grades?
The four most widely traded silver purity grades are .999 Fine Silver (99.9% pure — investment bullion standard), .925 Sterling Silver (92.5% pure — jewellery and flatware standard), .900 Coin Silver (90.0% pure — US pre-1965 circulating coinage) and .800 European Silver (80.0% pure — Continental antique flatware). Each is a millesimal fineness, with the balance alloyed with copper for hardness.
How is the price of each silver purity calculated?
The price per gram for any silver purity is (LBMA spot USD per troy ounce ÷ 31.1035) × purity ratio. USD is the primary currency because silver is quoted globally in US dollars. At a $33.00/oz spot: .999 = $1.061/g, .925 = $0.981/g, .900 = $0.955/g, .800 = $0.849/g. GBP, EUR and INR conversions use live ECB reference rates from the Frankfurter API.
How do I identify silver purity from a hallmark?
Look for a three-digit millesimal number stamped on the piece: 999 or FINE for bullion; 925 or STERLING for sterling, often with a UK lion passant; 900 has no hallmark on US coins — identify by year ≤1964; 800 is Continental European, frequently paired with the German Halbmond (crescent moon) and Krone (crown). Use a 10× loupe to verify the mark is crisply struck.
Which silver purity is the best investment?
.999 fine silver is the global investment grade — used by every major mint for bullion bars and coins including the American Silver Eagle, Britannia, Canadian Maple Leaf and Vienna Philharmonic. Its price tracks the LBMA spot one-for-one, minus an 8–20% minting premium. .925, .900 and .800 are valued at proportional melt — useful for scrap and antique resale, but not bullion-grade. For pure exposure to silver as an asset, choose .999 in 1 oz coins or 1 kg bars.
How do I calculate scrap silver value by purity?
Weigh the item in grams, multiply by the purity ratio (0.999, 0.925, 0.900 or 0.800), then multiply by the live USD-per-gram .999 price. Example: 100g of .925 sterling at a $1.061/g .999 price = 100 × 0.925 × $1.061 = $98.14 melt. Use the universal purity calculator at the top of this page — it handles any weight, any purity, any currency, live.
Why is silver priced in USD globally?
Silver is quoted globally in US dollars per troy ounce on COMEX (New York) silver futures and via the LBMA Silver Price daily auction in London. USD is the world's reserve currency and the most liquid quote unit for precious metals. Every conversion on this page to GBP, EUR or INR is mathematically derived from the live USD spot using ECB reference rates from the Frankfurter API.
What is the difference between .999 and .999 Britannia (.958) silver?
.958 Britannia silver is a UK historical standard introduced in 1697 to reduce coin clipping — 958 parts silver per thousand, slightly higher than sterling .925 but lower than modern .999. Britannia coins minted by The Royal Mint since 2013 are now .999 fine; older Britannia hallmarked silverware will be .958. For melt value, .958 is worth 95.8% of pure silver per gram, sitting between sterling and fine.
How accurate are these live silver purity prices?
Spot prices come direct from the LBMA via gold-api.com and refresh every 60 seconds. Currency conversion from USD to GBP, EUR and INR uses the Frankfurter API's live ECB reference rates. Figures shown are melt values — the raw silver content. Dealer buy prices typically sit 5–20% below melt; antique or rare-date pieces can fetch 2×–10× melt for collector premiums.
Is sterling silver (.925) magnetic?
No — genuine sterling silver, like all silver purities (.999, .925, .900, .800), is non-magnetic. Silver and copper (its main alloying metal) are both non-ferromagnetic. If your item is attracted to a magnet, it is silver-plated over a steel or iron base rather than solid sterling. This is the simplest authenticity test for any silver purity grade.
