A Pillar of Islam

Understanding Zakat on Gold & Silver

Zakat is the third Pillar of Islam — a mandatory annual purification of wealth (2.5%) given to qualifying recipients once a Muslim's eligible wealth exceeds the Nisab threshold and has been held for a lunar year (Hawl).

Conditions for Zakat to be Due

All four classical conditions must be satisfied for Zakat on precious metals:

  • Ownership: the gold or silver must be in your full ownership and control.
  • Nisab: your total qualifying wealth must reach or exceed the threshold (85g gold or 595g silver).
  • Hawl: the wealth has remained above Nisab for one complete lunar year (~354 days).
  • Intention: Zakat is given with the explicit intention of fulfilling the obligation.

The Hawl

One full lunar year (≈354 days) of continuous ownership above Nisab. The Hijri calendar is the reference, not the solar year.

The Rate

A fixed 2.5% (1/40th) of the current market value — not the historic purchase price — of the pure gold or silver content.

Pure Content Only

Calculated on the actual pure metal — 18K gold is 75% pure, sterling silver is 92.5% pure. Subtract gemstones, diamonds, and pearls before weighing.

8 Categories of Recipients

Defined in Surah At-Tawbah 9:60 — the poor, the needy, those who collect Zakat, reverts, those in bondage, debtors, in the cause of Allah, and the wayfarer.

Jewellery Zakat — Differences Between Madhabs

There is a recognised difference of scholarly opinion regarding Zakat on gold and silver jewellery that is worn for personal use. Always follow the ruling of a qualified scholar in your tradition.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about calculating Zakat on gold and silver in 2026.

Do I pay Zakat on jewellery I wear?

There is a recognised difference of opinion. The Hanafi school requires Zakat on all gold and silver, whether worn or stored. The Shafi'i, Maliki, and Hanbali schools generally exempt reasonable amounts of jewellery kept for personal, customary wear, but require Zakat on jewellery held for investment or saving purposes.

Follow the ruling of a qualified scholar in your school of thought.

Which Nisab should I use — gold or silver?

Most modern scholars advise using the silver Nisab threshold because it is significantly lower in value than the gold Nisab. Using the silver Nisab ensures more wealth qualifies for Zakat, benefitting more people in need.

If your wealth consists entirely of gold and no other Zakatable assets, you may use the gold Nisab. Otherwise, the silver Nisab is the safer default.

Is Zakat calculated on the purchase price or today's value?

Zakat must be calculated on the current market value on the day your Zakat is due (your Hawl date) — never the original purchase price. This calculator uses live spot prices refreshed every 60 seconds from global precious-metals markets.

Do I include gemstones, diamonds, or pearls in the weight?

No. Zakat on jewellery is only calculated on the weight of the gold or silver itself. Subtract the estimated weight of diamonds, rubies, pearls, or any other non-precious-metal materials before entering the weight.

A jeweller can usually give you the exact metal weight from a hallmark certificate or assay.

How does karat purity affect the Zakat I owe?

Zakat is paid on the pure gold content only. 24K gold is 99.99% pure; 18K is 75% pure; 14K is 58.33% pure; 9K is 37.5% pure. So 100g of 18K jewellery contains 75g of pure gold — and Zakat (2.5%) is calculated on the value of that 75g at today's spot price.

Our calculator handles this automatically — just select your karat from the dropdown.

Can I pay Zakat in cash instead of giving gold/silver?

Yes. The majority of contemporary scholars permit paying the cash equivalent of the Zakat due, as long as the cash value is calculated using the current spot price of the precious metal on the Hawl date.

This is what our calculator does — it shows you the exact cash amount equivalent to 2.5% of your pure gold and silver value at this moment.

When should I pay my Zakat?

Zakat is due once a full lunar year (~354 days) has elapsed since your wealth first reached or exceeded the Nisab. This is called your Hawl date. Use the Hawl Tracker above to calculate when your next Zakat is due.

Many Muslims choose to pay during Ramadan because the spiritual rewards are amplified — but you must still pay on or before your actual Hawl date if it falls outside Ramadan.
