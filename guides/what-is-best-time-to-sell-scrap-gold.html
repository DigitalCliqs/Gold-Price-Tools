There is a gold ring sitting in a drawer somewhere that you have been meaning to do something about for three years. Or a tangle of broken chains. Or earrings from an ex you kept telling yourself you would sell eventually. If you have ever wondered whether now is actually a good time — the answer in 2026 is that conditions are about as favourable as they have ever been.

But "the price is high" is not the same as knowing whether to sell today, wait until autumn, or hold out for an even higher number next year. Gold market timing is real, and understanding how it works — both on the macro scale and the day-to-day level — is the difference between a solid deal and leaving money on the table.

This guide covers it all in US dollars: where gold prices stand and where they may be headed, what the seasonal patterns actually show, when during the day the gold rate changes and why it matters, where to sell for the highest payout, and the practical details most guides skip. 02Is Now a Good Time to Sell Gold?

For scrap gold sellers — people selling unwanted jewelry, broken pieces, or items they no longer use — the honest answer is yes, 2026 is an excellent time.

Here is why that answer is fairly clear:

  • You are not giving up future use. Scrap gold is, by definition, gold you do not need. A broken chain, a single earring, an inherited ring that has sat in a box for a decade — none of these are serving you. Selling them at record-high prices is straightforwardly sensible.
  • Prices are near historic highs. Gold is not at its absolute January 2026 peak, but it remains close. The gap to the all-time high is roughly 15–20% — and while prices could rise further, they could equally pull back. Nobody rings a bell at the top of any market.
  • The structural drivers remain in place. Major institutions including JP Morgan, Deutsche Bank and UBS hold bullish outlooks through 2026–2027. The core reasons — central-bank buying, persistent inflation concerns, geopolitical instability, and ongoing dollar dynamics — have not resolved. That supports elevated prices, though exactly where they go from here is genuinely uncertain.

The counterargument is real but weak for scrap sellers. The case for waiting is essentially: what if prices go higher? JP Morgan's forecast of $5,055–$6,300 by year-end is plausible. But gold could equally drop to $4,000 on a dollar recovery or a surprise rate move. For scrap gold you do not need, the risk of waiting is asymmetric in a way that rarely favours the seller.

03Should You Wait for a Higher Price?

The question "should I sell now or wait?" comes up constantly, and the answer depends heavily on what you are selling and why.

For broken jewelry, single earrings, dental gold, and pieces you genuinely have no use for, the rational answer is nearly always to sell at a good price rather than trying to time the market precisely. You are not invested in gold as a strategy — you have gold through happenstance. Trading an accidental position like an investment adds complexity to what is fundamentally a decluttering exercise.

If you wait and price rises
You leave a little on the table

If gold climbs from ~$4,750 to JP Morgan's $6,300 target, a $1,000 lot might have fetched roughly $1,325 instead. A real gain — but only realised if you sell at the new peak, which nobody can time reliably.

If you wait and price falls
You give back a sure thing

A pullback to $4,000 turns that same $1,000 lot into roughly $840. You held an illiquid drawer item through a drawdown for nothing — the classic downside of trying to time a position you never chose to hold.

The practical approach most sellers find useful: set a floor, not a target. Decide what your items are worth at today's prices. If you receive a solid offer above that floor, accept it. Do not hold out indefinitely for a number that may never arrive. For investment-grade gold — coins, bars, deliberate holdings — the calculation is different, and this article is less relevant.

04Gold Market Timing: When Does the Rate Change?

"At what time does the gold price change?" is a common question, and the answer comes down to how gold markets actually work.

Gold trades on the global over-the-counter market and on exchanges including COMEX in New York and the Shanghai Gold Exchange. The spot market is open roughly 23 hours a day, five days a week — from Sunday evening through Friday evening (New York time), with only a brief daily maintenance pause. The gold price is therefore changing continuously during market hours, not once per day.

The LBMA Gold Price Fix

The London Bullion Market Association sets an official benchmark twice daily — at 10:30 AM and 3:00 PM London time. These are the AM and PM fixes, and most of the world's gold transactions, including scrap purchases from the public, are priced against them. When a buyer advertises rates, they typically base them on the most recent fix, so published rates update twice a day on working days. On volatile days, some buyers update more often.

The London–New York Overlap Window

The most actively traded window is the London–New York overlap, roughly 8:00 AM to noon US Eastern time (1:00–5:00 PM London). During these hours both the London OTC market and COMEX are simultaneously active, creating the day's highest liquidity and tightest spreads.

Practical note for sellers: If gold has had a strong day — up 1–2% — and you are mid-sale, it is worth asking whether the buyer's quoted rate reflects today's price or yesterday's close. Reputable buyers will tell you honestly. On stable days (which is most days) this is a non-issue. The bigger variable is not time of day but choice of buyer — worth far more attention than micro-timing your transaction.

Important caveat: These seasonal tendencies are real in aggregate across many years, but they are weak and frequently overwhelmed by bigger factors. In 2025, gold rose dramatically all year regardless of seasonality. In 2020, COVID disrupted every conventional pattern. In any year with major geopolitical events, Fed decisions, or data surprises, seasonal tendencies are simply noise.

The practical conclusion: do not hold off selling for months hoping to catch a seasonal peak. The seasonal premium, at best, is a few percentage points; the risk of prices moving against you over the same window is larger. Seasonal awareness is a tiebreaker, not a strategy.

07Where to Sell Scrap Gold: Payouts Compared

Where you sell matters as much as when. Getting the timing right and then accepting 50% of melt from an opportunistic buyer wastes the whole advantage. Here is roughly what each channel pays, as a share of melt value:

Specialist online buyers and refiners with direct refinery access lead on price because they cut out the middleman. Auction houses sit outside this scale — for antique or collectible pieces they can pay well above melt (see below).

How the leading channels work

  • Specialist online buyers & refiners — request a free insured mail-in pack, send via tracked, insured delivery (USPS Registered Mail, UPS, or Royal Mail Special Delivery), receive an offer within 24–48 hours, and accept payment by bank transfer — or have items returned free of charge. Best for all karats, mixed lots, dental gold, and broken pieces. Verify the buyer's reviews independently before sending anything.
  • In-person specialist dealers — bullion dealers with in-house refinery links offer rates comparable to the best online buyers, with same-day assessment and payment. In the US, the New York Diamond District (47th Street) and major-city dealers; in the UK, London's Hatton Garden and Birmingham's Jewellery Quarter. For larger quantities, in-person dealers may negotiate above their published rates.
  • Local jewelers — competitive for pieces in good, resellable condition (they value them as jewelry, not just metal), but weaker on plain broken scrap, which they must sell on to a wholesaler.
  • Online marketplaces (eBay etc.) — possible for unusual lots with collector interest, but selling fees, bargain hunters, payment disputes, and shipping risk make it a poor default for ordinary scrap.

08How to Get the Best Price When You Sell

Beyond choosing the right buyer type, a few habits make a real difference to what you receive:

  • 1
    Know your melt value before approaching anyone

    Use a live scrap gold calculator to find what your gold is worth at today's USD spot price. Weight × purity fraction × price per gram = melt value. An offer at 90% of melt is excellent; an offer at 50% deserves to be declined.

  • 2
    Weigh everything yourself first

    A kitchen scale accurate to 0.1 grams costs about $10. Weigh each karat separately and record the results. When a buyer weighs your items, confirm their numbers match yours — discrepancies over 0.2–0.3 grams should prompt a question.

  • 3
    Get at least two quotes

    The spread between buyers can be striking. Sending photos and weights to two or three specialists before deciding costs nothing and takes twenty minutes. On a $650 lot, the gap between a 75% and a 92% payout is over $110 — found money for minimal effort.

  • 4
    Don't mix karats

    Keep 9K, 14K, and 18K pieces separate. Buyers price each karat individually; presenting them mixed makes it easier for a buyer to apply the lowest rate to the whole lot.

  • 5
    Check whether any item has non-scrap value

    Before submitting a lot, look at each piece. Antique and hallmarked items, branded pieces (Tiffany, Cartier, Georg Jensen), and coins with numismatic interest should be assessed by a specialist first. Melt value is always the floor; for special pieces it should not be the ceiling.

09Scrap Gold vs. Auction: When the Math Changes

Most broken or plain scrap gold is genuinely best sold for its metal value — there is no premium available elsewhere. But specific categories leave real money behind when sold as scrap:

  • Antique hallmarked pieces with clear date letters and assay marks — particularly Victorian and Edwardian jewelry — attract collector interest beyond their metal value.
  • Georgian jewelry (pre-1837) is rare enough that even modest pieces regularly sell at auction above melt.
  • Signed pieces from known makers may be identifiable through maker's marks and carry collector value.
  • Gold coins with numismatic interest — older sovereigns and foreign coins in good condition — should be appraised by a coin specialist before being sold as bullion.
  • Pieces with intact gemstones — a genuine diamond, sapphire, or ruby is worth more than the gold alone, even if the setting is damaged.

If you are unsure whether a piece qualifies, a fifteen-minute visit to a reputable auction house's free valuation day will answer the question quickly.

10The Bottom Line: Is Now a Good Time?

For gold you genuinely do not need — broken jewelry, single pieces, inherited items you have no connection to — 2026 is one of the best selling environments in living memory.

If someone offered to buy something you do not want, at the best price it has ever commanded, would you say yes? For most scrap gold sellers, the answer is straightforward.

Gold is near all-time highs in US dollars. The structural factors supporting the price — central-bank demand, geopolitical uncertainty, inflation sensitivity — remain intact, and institutional forecasts suggest it could go higher still. But for a private seller with no ongoing use for their gold, "could go higher" is not a reason to wait indefinitely. Set your floor against the live price, get two or three quotes, and sell to whoever pays closest to melt.

11Frequently Asked Questions

Is now a good time to sell gold in 2026?

Yes. Gold is trading near all-time highs in USD — around $4,750 per troy ounce as of mid-2026, not far below the January 2026 record of $5,589/oz. For scrap gold and unwanted jewelry, current prices are a generational selling opportunity. Forecasts from JP Morgan and Deutsche Bank suggest further upside is possible, though uncertain. For gold you do not need, there is no compelling reason to wait.

How much is scrap gold worth per gram?

At a representative spot near $4,750/oz, approximate melt values per gram are: 9K — $57/g; 10K — $64/g; 14K — $89/g; 18K — $115/g; 22K — $140/g; 24K — $153/g. Most buyers pay 70–92% of these melt values. Use our scrap gold calculator for exact live figures at the current USD spot price, refreshed every 60 seconds.

What is the best place to sell scrap gold?

Specialist online gold buyers and bullion dealers with direct refinery access consistently offer the highest payouts — typically 85–95% of melt value. Local jewelers and pawn shops typically pay 40–75%. For pieces with potential collector or antique value, an auction house is worth considering before committing to a scrap sale.

How do I sell scrap gold online?

Request a free insured mail-in kit from a reputable specialist buyer, package your gold carefully, send it via tracked and insured delivery, receive an offer within 24–48 hours, and accept payment by bank transfer or have items returned. Always weigh and calculate your melt value first so you can evaluate any offer. Getting quotes from two or three buyers takes twenty minutes and often adds meaningful value.

Is scrap gold worth anything?

Very much so at current prices. A plain 9K gold ring weighing 4 grams has a scrap value of roughly $195 at an 85% payout. A 10-gram 18K chain is worth around $975. Even small quantities — including dental gold and broken pieces — are worth real money at 2026 prices.

What time does the gold rate change daily?

The gold spot price changes continuously during market hours (Sunday evening to Friday evening, New York time). There is no single daily "change time" — prices move every second in response to trades, news, and data. However, the LBMA sets official benchmark fixes at 10:30 AM and 3:00 PM London time on weekdays, and most buyers update their published rates against these benchmarks.

Should I sell my gold for scrap or at auction?

It depends on the piece. Plain, mass-produced, or broken gold is almost always best sold as scrap — there is no collector premium to capture. Antique, hallmarked, signed, or historically significant pieces can achieve prices well above melt value at auction. If you are uncertain, a free auction-house valuation is the quickest way to find out.

Where can I find scrap gold buyers near me?

Search "scrap gold buyers near me" to find local gold dealers, jewelers, and bullion shops. In the US, New York's Diamond District (47th Street) and major-city bullion dealers are hubs; in the UK, London's Hatton Garden and Birmingham's Jewellery Quarter. Online specialist buyers are often the better choice on price regardless of location, as they offer insured shipping, publish live rates, and have verifiable reviews.
