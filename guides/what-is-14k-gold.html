What Is 14K Gold? Purity, Value & How to Identify It

Published 24 April 2026 · GoldPriceTools Editorial

14K gold is one of the most widely used gold alloys in the world, particularly for everyday jewellery in the United States, Canada, and the UK. But what does “14K” actually mean, and how does it affect the value of a piece? This guide explains everything you need to know.

What Does 14K Mean?

The “K” in 14K stands for karat — a unit used to express the purity of gold as a fraction of 24. 14K gold contains 14 parts pure gold out of 24, which is equal to 58.33% pure gold. The remaining 41.67% consists of other metals such as silver, copper, zinc, or nickel, which are added to improve durability and alter colour.

Quick fact: 14K gold = 14 ÷ 24 = 58.33% pure gold. It is the minimum karat that can legally be sold as “gold” in the United States under FTC guidelines.

How Much Is 14K Gold Worth Per Gram?

The value of 14K gold per gram is calculated by taking the current 24K gold spot price per gram and multiplying by the 14K purity factor of 0.5833. For example, if gold is trading at $97.00 per gram (24K), then 14K gold is worth approximately $56.58 per gram in raw metal value.

How to Identify 14K Gold

Genuine 14K gold jewellery will always carry a hallmark or stamp indicating its purity. The most common markings you will find on 14K gold include:

  • 14K — the standard US marking
  • 585 — the European millesimal fineness mark (58.5% pure)
  • 14KT — an alternative US stamp
  • 14ct — the UK “carat” spelling
  • 0585 — sometimes seen on older European pieces

These stamps are typically found on the clasp of necklaces and bracelets, the inside of ring bands, or the post of earrings. A jeweller’s loupe (magnifying glass) is helpful for reading small hallmarks.

Colours of 14K Gold

Because 14K gold is an alloy, its colour depends on what metals are mixed with the pure gold:

  • Yellow gold (14K): Gold mixed with silver and copper in roughly equal proportions. The classic gold colour.
  • White gold (14K): Gold mixed with white metals like palladium or nickel, then often rhodium-plated. Popular for engagement rings.
  • Rose gold (14K): Gold mixed with a higher proportion of copper, giving a warm pinkish hue. Very popular in modern jewellery.

All three colours have the same gold content (58.33%) and the same base melt value — only the alloy metals differ.

14K Gold Durability

One of the key advantages of 14K gold over higher-karat alloys is its superior durability. The higher the karat, the softer the gold. Pure 24K gold is too soft for most jewellery and scratches easily. 14K gold strikes an excellent balance: it is hard enough to withstand daily wear while still containing enough gold to retain significant value. This is why 14K is the most popular choice for engagement rings and wedding bands in the United States.

Is 14K Gold a Good Investment?

14K gold jewellery is not typically bought as a financial investment in the same way that gold bullion bars or coins are. The retail premium paid for jewellery (design, labour, retail markup) means you will rarely recover the full purchase price if you sell. However, 14K gold does retain real intrinsic metal value — the melt value of a 14K gold ring is always worth something, regardless of whether the jewellery style remains fashionable.

If you are looking to invest in gold, consider 24K bullion coins or bars which trade close to spot price.

14K vs 18K Gold — Which Should You Choose?

The choice between 14K and 18K gold comes down to priorities. 18K gold is richer in colour and higher in purity (75% gold), making it more prestigious and valuable per gram. 14K is more affordable and more durable for everyday wear. For a ring worn every day, many jewellers recommend 14K. For a special occasion piece or fine jewellery, 18K may be preferred. Check our 18K gold price calculator to compare current values.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is 14K gold real gold?

Yes. 14K gold is genuine gold. It contains 58.33% pure gold, with the remainder being other metals to improve strength and durability. It is a real gold alloy that has real monetary value based on its gold content.

Will 14K gold tarnish?

Pure gold does not tarnish. However, 14K gold contains other metals (such as copper and silver) that can oxidise over time, potentially causing slight discolouration, especially with rose gold or lower-quality alloys. Regular cleaning with mild soap and warm water will keep 14K gold looking its best.

Can 14K gold cause allergic reactions?

Some people are allergic to nickel, which is sometimes used in white gold alloys. If you have a known nickel sensitivity, look for nickel-free 14K white gold alloys, or choose yellow or rose gold instead.
