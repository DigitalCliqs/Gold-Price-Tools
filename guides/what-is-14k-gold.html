What Is 14K Gold? Purity, Price & Uses

It is not the purest gold you can buy, nor the cheapest — yet 14K is the single most popular gold jewellery standard in the world. Durable enough for daily wear, rich enough to hold its colour, and priced within reach. Here is everything it means, what it is worth today, and how to buy, care for, and sell it.

Walk into any jewellery shop in North America, Central Europe, or the Middle East and the most prominent karat you will encounter is 14K. It sits in a deliberate sweet spot — durable enough for daily wear, rich enough in gold content to hold its colour and value over time, and priced accessibly enough that it has become the world’s default gold jewellery standard. Yet most people who own 14K gold cannot answer the basics: What percentage is actually gold? What is it worth per gram today? Does it tarnish? Will it turn your skin green?

This guide answers every one of those questions clearly and factually — with current 2026 prices, the exact formulas to calculate value, and practical advice on how to buy, care for, and sell 14K gold.

What Does 14K Gold Mean?

The “K” in 14K stands for karat — a unit that expresses the purity of gold as a fraction of 24 parts. 14K gold means that 14 out of every 24 parts are pure gold, with the remaining 10 parts made up of other metals. Expressed as a percentage: 14 ÷ 24 = 58.33% pure gold.

The remaining 41.67% consists of metal alloys — typically copper, silver, zinc, nickel, or palladium — chosen depending on the intended colour and application of the final product.

58.33%
Pure Gold

The 14K Hallmarks You Will See

The same 58.33% gold standard is stamped in several formats depending on where the piece was made and sold:

14K
The most common North American marking (also 14k).
585
The European millesimal fineness mark — 585 / 1,000 = 58.5%.
14KP
“14 Karat Plumb” — guarantees the item is at least full 14K.
14Kt
An older variant of the karat abbreviation.

The 585 stamp and the 14K stamp mean exactly the same thing — 58.3% gold — and are completely interchangeable. If you find a piece stamped 585, it is 14K gold.

14K Gold Purity and Composition

14K gold is an alloy — it is real gold, but it is not pure gold. The exact make-up of the non-gold portion varies by the colour being produced. All three colours share the identical 58.33% gold content, so their intrinsic value per gram is the same.

Gold ColourGold ContentTypical Alloy Metals
14K Yellow Gold58.33%~29% silver, ~13% copper
14K White Gold58.33%~32.5% palladium / silver, ~9.2% copper
14K Rose Gold58.33%~32–36% copper, ~5–9% silver

Source: World Gold Council typical alloy compositions. Exact mixes vary by manufacturer.

14K Yellow Gold
Silver + Copper balance

The warm, classic gold tone. More copper makes it warmer and redder; more silver gives a slightly greener-yellow hue.

14K White Gold
Palladium / Nickel + Rhodium

Silvery-white from palladium or nickel, then rhodium-plated for brightness. As the plating wears, re-plating restores the finish.

14K Rose Gold
High Copper content

The pink/rose colour comes from a higher copper proportion. Gold content is identical to yellow or white — same value per gram.

Is 14K Gold Real Gold?

Yes
14K gold is genuine gold — absolutely.

The alloy metals are added to improve durability and adjust colour; they do not make the gold “fake.” Any item properly hallmarked 14K (or 585) is confirmed by international standards to contain a minimum of 58.33% pure gold.

The confusion usually comes from cheap jewellery that is falsely stamped, or from “14K gold plated” items that are not solid gold at all — a critical distinction covered further down.

14K Gold Price Per Gram Today (2026)

The 14K gold price per gram is calculated directly from the live gold spot price. Because 14K is 58.33% gold, you take the pure-gold price per gram and multiply by 0.5833.

14K Price Per Gram Formula
14K / gram = (Spot per oz ÷ 31.1035) × 0.5833

At ~$4,513/oz (May 21, 2026): ($4,513 ÷ 31.1035) × 0.5833 = $145.09 × 0.5833 = ≈ $84.64 / gram

Gold has traded near all-time highs throughout 2026 — the spot price hit a record $5,589/oz earlier in the year before pulling back. The current level of ~$4,500/oz is a meaningful pullback but still roughly double the price of two years ago.

14K Gold Scrap Price Per Gram

The scrap price — what a dealer actually pays for 14K jewellery — is typically 70–90% of the melt value. At ~$84.64/gram melt value, expect:

A pawn shop is typically the lowest-paying channel because it carries the highest overhead relative to transaction size and applies the widest margins. For significant quantities of 14K gold, an online bullion dealer or specialist gold buyer will consistently offer a higher percentage of melt value.

14K Gold Calculator: How to Calculate Your Value

To calculate the melt value of any 14K gold item, follow four steps:

1

Weigh the item in grams

Needs: precision digital scale (0.01g accuracy)

Use a calibrated jewellery scale. Even small weight errors compound at today’s high gold prices.

2

Confirm it is 14K

Look for: 14K · 585 · 14Kt hallmark

Check the clasp, inside of a ring band, or the post of an earring. No hallmark? A jeweller can verify purity with acid or XRF testing.

3

Get the live gold spot price

Source: GoldPriceTools, Kitco or APMEX (USD / troy oz)

The spot price moves continuously through the trading day, so use a live source rather than yesterday’s figure.

4

Apply the formula

Formula: Weight × (Spot ÷ 31.1035) × 0.5833

For items with stones, gemstones are removed and valued separately — only the gold’s weight counts.

Worked Example
7.5g ring × ($4,513 ÷ 31.1035) × 0.5833

7.5 × $145.09 × 0.5833 = 7.5 × $84.64 = $634.80 melt value

A pawn shop might offer $380–$500 for this ring; an online dealer might offer $540–$585.

Is 10K or 14K better? Neither is objectively “better.” 14K is the better choice for most people seeking a balance of purity, colour, durability and value retention. 10K suits the tightest budgets or applications needing maximum hardness. Is 14K or 18K better? For everyday wearability, 14K’s extra alloy content makes it noticeably more scratch-resistant — a real practical advantage. Does 14K Gold Tarnish, Turn Skin Green, or Attract Magnets?

Three of the most-asked questions about 14K gold, answered directly.

Does 14K Gold Tarnish?

Barely
It can dull slightly — but not like silver.

Pure gold (24K) does not tarnish at all. The vulnerability in 14K comes from its 41.67% alloy content — mainly copper and silver, which react slowly with atmospheric oxygen and moisture, sulfur compounds, chlorine (pools, cleaning products), and acids in sweat, perfume and skincare.

The result is a gradual dulling of surface lustre, not the dramatic black tarnish of sterling silver. 14K white gold is most susceptible because its rhodium plating slowly wears, revealing the slightly yellowish base alloy.

With reasonable care — keeping it clean, removing it before swimming or using cleaning products, and storing it properly — most 14K jewellery keeps its appearance for years or decades without meaningful tarnishing.

Does 14K Gold Turn Your Skin Green or Black?

Rarely
Occasionally — and it is harmless.

Neither gold nor silver causes the green colour. The culprit is copper in the alloy. When copper meets acidic perspiration, skincare or certain medications, it oxidises into copper salts that leave a greenish stain — the same reaction that greens copper coins and copper roofs.

It is not an allergic reaction (which would itch or cause a rash) — it is a simple chemistry reaction, and it washes off easily with soap and water.

You are most likely to see it if you have naturally acidic skin, sweat heavily, apply lotions or sanitisers before wearing jewellery, take certain medications, or are experiencing hormonal changes. To prevent it:

  • Apply lotions and perfumes before putting on jewellery, and let them absorb first
  • Remove rings before exercising, swimming or showering
  • Keep jewellery clean and dry between uses
  • A thin coat of clear nail polish on the inside of a ring acts as a temporary barrier
  • If it persists, upgrade to 18K (lower copper content) or platinum

Is 14K Gold Magnetic?

No
Solid 14K gold is not magnetic.

Pure gold is non-magnetic, and the standard 14K alloy metals (copper, silver, palladium, zinc) are non-magnetic too. The magnet test is a simple home authenticity check: bring a strong neodymium magnet close to the item. If it is attracted, it is not solid gold (iron, steel and some nickel alloys are magnetic). If there is no pull, it passes — though this is a pass/fail test, not a karat identifier.

14K Gold Plated vs Solid 14K: What’s the Difference?

14K gold plated means the item has a base-metal core — usually brass, copper or stainless steel — with a thin layer of 14K gold electroplated onto the surface. It is not solid 14K gold, and its scrap value is negligible. Here is how the four “14K” constructions compare:

TypeGold ContentDurabilityValue
Solid 14K gold58.33% throughoutLifetimeHigh — real melt value
14K gold filled1/20th of weight is 14K (~5%)Years of daily wearModerate — some scrap value
14K gold platedTrace (0.5–2.5 micron layer)Months to yearsNegligible scrap value
14K gold vermeilThin 14K layer over sterling silverModerateLow (silver value only)
Yes
Does 14K gold plated tarnish? Yes — much faster than solid gold.

The thin gold layer wears away at friction points (ring shanks, clasps, high-contact spots), exposing the base metal beneath, which tarnishes and can cause noticeable skin discolouration. The thicker the gold layer (measured in microns), the longer the plating lasts.

14K gold filled is far better than plating. A piece stamped “14K GF” or “1/20 14K” has a much thicker gold layer — at least 1/20th (5%) of total weight — mechanically bonded under heat and pressure. It will not peel or tarnish quickly and gives years of wear at a fraction of the cost of solid gold. This is regulated by US FTC and EU standards.

Is 14K Gold Good? Should You Buy It?

Yes
14K gold is excellent quality — the optimal choice for most jewellery.

It has become the dominant global standard for fine jewellery for good reason, especially for pieces worn every day. Here is why.

Durability

With 41.67% alloy, 14K is significantly harder than 18K or 22K — resisting scratches, dents and bending in everyday wear.

Value Retention

Unlike plated jewellery, solid 14K holds intrinsic metal value that rises and falls with the gold price — a tangible asset, not just an ornament.

Colour

58.33% gold is enough to keep the warm, recognisable gold colour most buyers want — it does not look pale or washed out like 10K.

Accessibility

Meaningfully less expensive per gram than 18K or 22K, making fine jewellery affordable to a wider audience without sacrificing quality.

Versatility

Available in yellow, white and rose gold with consistent quality and identical gold content across all three colours.

When 14K might not be ideal: people with nickel allergies (choose 18K or palladium alloys), buyers who prioritise maximum gold purity for cultural or investment reasons (22K or 24K), and those on an extremely tight budget (10K).
