What Is 925 Sterling Silver?

By GoldPriceTools Editorial Team · Published 3 May 2026 · Updated 3 May 2026

925 sterling silver is an alloy containing 92.5% pure silver and 7.5% other metals, typically copper. The “925” stamp is the hallmark that confirms this purity level. Sterling silver has been the standard for silverware, jewellery, and coins in the UK since at least the 14th century, and remains the most widely traded silver purity worldwide.

What Is 925 Silver Worth Per Gram?

Sterling silver is worth 92.5% of the pure (999) silver spot price. At $33 per troy ounce (XAG/USD), the calculation is:

$33 ÷ 31.1035 = $1.062/g (fine silver) × 0.925 = $0.982/g (sterling silver)
At GBP/USD 0.79: approximately 77.6p per gram for sterling silver. Our live calculator shows today’s exact rate.

How to Identify Real 925 Sterling Silver

  • Look for the hallmark: In the UK, genuine sterling silver will have a “925” stamp plus an Assay Office mark (lion passant for London, crown for Sheffield, castle for Edinburgh)
  • Magnet test: Real silver is not magnetic. If a magnet sticks strongly, it’s likely a base metal with silver plating
  • Tarnish test: Genuine silver tarnishes slowly to yellow/grey. Silver-plated items tarnish faster and more unevenly
  • Acid test: A jeweller can apply nitric acid; sterling silver produces a creamy white reaction
  • XRF analysis: The most accurate test — available at most reputable jewellers and assay offices

Sterling vs Silver-Plated: What’s the Difference?

Silver-plated items have only a thin layer of silver over a base metal (usually brass or copper). The silver content is negligible — typically less than 1% of the item’s weight. Silver-plated jewellery and silverware have almost no scrap value. Sterling silver items are solid silver alloy throughout and have real melt value based on weight and purity.

Caring for 925 Sterling Silver

To slow tarnishing: store silver in anti-tarnish bags or with silica gel; avoid exposure to chlorine, bleach, and perfumes; remove jewellery before swimming or cleaning. To remove tarnish: use a specialist silver polishing cloth (e.g. Goddard’s), mild silver dip, or a baking soda paste for heavier tarnish.

The History of the 925 Hallmark

Sterling silver's 92.5% standard dates to 13th-century England, when the English Crown established a minimum silver content for coinage. The word "sterling" may derive from "Easterling" (the Hanseatic League merchants whose coins were known for consistent quality) or from the Old Norman French word for "little star" (which appeared on early Norman pennies).

The 925 hallmark means 925 parts per thousand of pure silver — equivalent to 92.5%. It is applied in the UK by one of the four Assay Offices (London, Birmingham, Sheffield, Edinburgh) and is the most common silver standard in UK and US jewellery, flatware, and decorative items. You may also encounter the mark STER or STERLING on older American pieces.

Why Copper? The Role of Alloy in Sterling Silver

Pure silver (999 fine) is too soft for most practical applications — it bends easily and scratches quickly. Adding 7.5% copper to create 925 sterling dramatically increases hardness and durability while preserving silver's bright appearance and lustre. Copper is the preferred alloy partner because it is compatible with silver's melting temperature and doesn't significantly alter the colour.

Some modern sterling alloys replace part of the copper with germanium (Argentium silver) or platinum to improve tarnish resistance, though these are more expensive to produce and less common in standard UK hallmarked items.

Understanding Tarnish: Causes and Prevention

Sterling silver tarnishes through a chemical reaction between the copper alloy and hydrogen sulphide (H₂S) in the atmosphere, forming a dark layer of silver sulphide on the surface. Tarnish is accelerated by:

  • Humidity and moisture
  • Exposure to rubber, wool, eggs, and onions (high sulphur content)
  • Chlorine (swimming pools, tap water)
  • Skin contact (perspiration contains sulphur compounds)
  • Air pollution in urban environments

To slow tarnishing: store silver in anti-tarnish pouches or zip-lock bags with the air squeezed out, avoid contact with rubber bands, and clean regularly with a microfibre silver cloth. For heavily tarnished pieces, an electrolytic cleaning bath (aluminium foil + bicarbonate of soda + hot water) removes tarnish safely without abrasion.

Calculating 925 Sterling Silver Melt Value

The melt value of sterling silver items is straightforward to calculate:

  1. Weigh the item in grams (remove any non-silver components — handles, clasps made from other metals)
  2. Find the current silver spot price per gram (use our silver calculator)
  3. Multiply: Melt value = weight (g) × silver gram price × 0.925

Example: 100g sterling silver tea spoons, silver at £0.82/g: 100 × £0.82 × 0.925 = £75.85 melt value.

Note: UK scrap silver dealers typically pay 70–90% of melt value. For antique or designer sterling pieces in good condition, specialist dealers or auction houses may offer significantly more than melt value.
