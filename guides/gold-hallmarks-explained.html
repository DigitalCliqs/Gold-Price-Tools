Gold Hallmarks Explained: The Complete Identification Guide

Pick up almost any piece of gold jewellery, look closely, and you will find a tiny constellation of stamps in the metal — a number, a small symbol, perhaps a heraldic animal. These are gold hallmarks, and they are one of the most useful and underappreciated pieces of information in the jewellery world. This guide covers everything: British, European, Russian, Fabergé, Indian, Middle Eastern and Chinese marks — plus the live value of every fineness.

What Is a Gold Hallmark?

A gold hallmark is an official mark stamped onto a piece of gold jewellery or metalwork by an independent testing body — called an assay office — after the item has been chemically tested and confirmed to meet a legal standard of purity.

The word itself has a very specific origin. From the 16th century onwards, goldsmiths in London were required to bring their work to Goldsmiths' Hall on Foster Lane, where it would be tested and, if it met the required standard, struck with an official mark. That mark — applied at the Hall — became known as a "hall mark." The name stuck, and today it is used worldwide to describe any official precious metal certification stamp.

The purpose has always been the same: consumer protection. Gold can be alloyed with cheaper metals at any proportion — a piece that looks like solid 18-karat gold could be 9-karat, or less. Without an independent test, a buyer has no way to verify what they are actually purchasing. Hallmarks solve that problem by transferring the verification to an independent, legally accountable body whose mark carries legal weight.

The British Hallmarking System: The World's Most Rigorous

The United Kingdom operates the oldest and most comprehensive hallmarking system in the world. British hallmarking dates back to 1300, when Edward I decreed that all gold and silver must be assayed before sale. In 1478, the date letter system was introduced in London, allowing items to be dated to within a year. By 1757, counterfeiting a hallmark had become a felony punishable by death — reflecting just how seriously the system was taken.

Today, the system is governed by the Hallmarking Act 1973, which requires that any item described as gold and weighing more than one gram must be hallmarked before sale in the UK. Hallmarking must be carried out by one of four UK assay offices, each supervised by the British Hallmarking Council.

The Four Components of a British Hallmark

A full UK hallmark consists of up to four distinct marks, three of which are compulsory under the 1973 Act:

1. The Sponsor's Mark (Maker's Mark)

The sponsor's mark identifies the company or individual who sent the item for hallmarking — typically the manufacturer, importer, or retailer. It usually consists of two or three initials in a distinctive shape (shield, lozenge, rectangle), which the sponsor has registered with their assay office. Assay offices and specialist reference books hold registration records going back centuries.

2. The Fineness Mark (Purity Mark)

The fineness mark tells you the gold content of the item, expressed in parts per thousand — a system called millesimal fineness. Common UK gold fineness marks are:

Prior to 1975, UK gold hallmarks used a different system — a crown (indicating gold) plus the carat numeral (9, 15, 18, 22). If you find this combination on an older piece, it is the pre-decimal British purity mark. The change to millesimal fineness brought the UK into line with international standards.

A fifth office — Chester — operated until 1962, using a sword with three wheatsheaves. Dublin's office (using a crowned harp) was active until 1923. Both are perfectly valid historical hallmarks encountered on antique pieces.

4. The Date Letter (Optional since 1998)

The date letter is an alphabetically cycling letter, changed each year, which identifies when the item was hallmarked. Date letters were compulsory until 1998 and are now voluntary, though many manufacturers still use them — particularly for commemorative or high-value pieces. Each assay office uses its own date letter cycle with different typefaces and shield shapes. Bradbury's Book of Hallmarks contains the complete cycles for all offices and is the standard resource for dating antique British pieces.

Where to Find Hallmarks on Gold Jewellery

Hallmarks are small — often tiny — and placed in locations that are practical from a manufacturing standpoint but not always obvious to a buyer. Here is where to look:

  • Rings: Inside the band (inner circumference), usually near the join
  • Necklaces and bracelets: On or very near the clasp; sometimes on a small attached tag
  • Earrings: On the post or earring back; sometimes on the face of a stud
  • Pendants: On the reverse of the pendant, or on the bale (the loop the chain passes through)
  • Pocket watches: On the inner case back, sometimes also on the outer case; watch cases typically carry a full set of hallmarks
  • Brooches: On the reverse, near the pin mechanism
  • Bangles: On the inner surface

A 10× jeweller's loupe makes a significant difference when reading small or worn hallmarks. A phone camera with zoom can also be useful for photographing marks and examining them on screen.

Reading a Complete British Hallmark

To make this concrete, here is how to read a complete hallmark on a hypothetical gold ring:

Example: A ring with the following marks inside the band: AT (in a shield) · 750 · Leopard's head · Letter N in a plain square.

  • AT — the sponsor's mark (the maker's registered initials)
  • 750 — 18-carat gold (75% pure gold)
  • Leopard's head — London Assay Office; tested and certified in London
  • N — the date letter, identifying the year of hallmarking by cross-reference with London's date letter cycle

Taken together: solid 18-carat gold, tested by the London Assay Office, with a precisely dateable year of hallmarking — a remarkable amount of information from four tiny stamps.

Antique Gold Hallmarks: What Changes Over Time

Antique British gold hallmarks differ from modern ones in ways that matter to collectors and dealers. Understanding what changed — and when — helps with both identification and authentication.

Pre-1975: Crown and Carat Numeral System

Antique gold of 18ct and 22ct will typically show a crown symbol followed by the numeral 18 or 22 — this was the British gold standard mark used up to 1975. After 1854, the standards were broadened to include 15ct, 12ct, and 9ct in addition to the traditional 18ct and 22ct. Finding a 15ct hallmark dates a piece to between 1854 and 1932, when the 15ct and 12ct standards were discontinued and replaced with 14ct.

Victorian Gold Hallmarks (1837–1901)

Victorian jewellery is among the most actively collected antique category in the world, and Victorian gold hallmarks are consequently one of the most commonly researched topics. Key characteristics:

  • The crown-plus-numeral purity system was in universal use throughout the Victorian period
  • Date letters allow precise dating of Victorian pieces to individual years
  • 15-carat gold is particularly characteristic of later Victorian work — widely used for sentimental lockets, brooches, and chains
  • 18-carat and 22-carat were used for fine jewellery and high-quality work
  • Some small, delicate Victorian items were exempt from hallmarking on the grounds of size — genuine period pieces may carry no marks

Georgian Gold Hallmarks (1714–1837)

Georgian jewellery presents a particular challenge because compulsory hallmarking of all precious metals did not begin until the later Victorian period. Some Georgian pieces carry clear assay office marks and date letters; others — particularly small or delicate items — were made without marks entirely and are authenticated on stylistic, constructional, and provenance grounds.

When Georgian pieces do carry marks, 18ct and 22ct are the most common standards — Georgian goldsmiths typically worked with higher purities than is standard today. The presence of a date letter from the Georgian period on a well-executed piece can date the item to within one year.

Rare and Unusual British Hallmarks

Beyond the standard four marks, British jewellery sometimes carries additional marks:

  • The Lion Passant: A walking lion in profile, primarily associated with English sterling silver but occasionally appearing on pre-decimal gold pieces as a traditional mark.
  • Coronation and Jubilee Marks: Special commemorative marks struck in specific years — for Queen Elizabeth II's coronations and jubilees. Voluntary additional marks of interest to collectors that can confirm a piece as an authentic period item.
  • Convention Common Control Mark (CCM): A scale-of-balance mark used on items certified under the Vienna Convention — an international hallmarking treaty involving 19 member countries. Items bearing a CCM are recognised across all member states without requiring local re-hallmarking.
  • Chester Assay Office Mark: A sword with three wheatsheaves, used from the 17th century until Chester's closure in 1962. Pieces bearing this mark are authentic British hallmarks and relatively uncommon.

European Gold Hallmarks: A Country-by-Country Guide

European hallmarking varies significantly by country — some operate government-mandated assay systems similar to the UK; others use self-declaration by manufacturers; some use pictorial symbols; others rely purely on numeric fineness marks.

French Gold Hallmarks

France developed one of the most elegant hallmarking systems in the world, built around pictorial symbols rather than numbers. The system was standardised in 1838 and remains largely unchanged today.

Eagle's head (facing left)18K gold (750)National guarantee mark since 1838 — the most commonly encountered French gold mark

The eagle's head is the mark most collectors and buyers encounter on antique French jewellery — tiny, discreet, but decisive. It appears on virtually all 18-karat French gold from 1838 onwards. Historically, different animals were used for 18K gold at different periods — the horse's head, rhinoceros head, and even the weevil were all used before the standardisation of the eagle. The owl mark on a piece confirms it was sold in France but made elsewhere.

German Gold Hallmarks

Germany does not operate a compulsory government hallmarking system for gold. Instead, manufacturers use the millesimal fineness system (375 585 750 999) with their own maker's marks. The crescent moon and crown symbol was historically used as the German assay guarantee mark for gold and silver. Items carrying this symbol alongside a fineness number are reliably identifiable as German-certified precious metal.

Italian Gold Hallmarks

Italy has one of the world's most prolific gold jewellery manufacturing industries — Arezzo, Vicenza, and Valenza are among the world's leading production centres — but Italy does not require government assay hallmarking in the same way the UK does. Italian pieces carry a maker's mark (a number identifying the manufacturer, registered with the state) and a fineness mark (375 585 750 999). The shape of the cartouche around the fineness mark differs: pieces above 750 fineness use a star-shaped or oval cartouche; pieces at or below 750 use a different shape.

Arezzo in Tuscany is historically significant as one of Italy's oldest goldsmithing centres, recognised globally for quality. The numeric maker's code is the key identifier — registries are held by the Italian Chamber of Commerce.

Spanish Gold Hallmarks

Spain operates a dual hallmarking system: government assay offices in major regions certify gold, and manufacturers must also register their mark. The system uses millesimal fineness (375 585 750) with regional assay codes that make Spanish gold particularly informative for tracing provenance:

Swiss Gold Hallmarks

Switzerland introduced gold hallmarking in 1880. Swiss pieces carry a fineness mark alongside an assay office mark and maker's mark. The most distinctive Swiss hallmark symbols are the squirrel (for gold 585/14K and above, used since 1934) and the Helvetia head (for 18ct, 750 fineness). Swiss watch cases are a rich area for hallmark study — the combination of maker's code, fineness, and assay office mark allows detailed authentication of both case and movement.

Austrian Gold Hallmarks

Austria uses the Convention Common Control Mark alongside national marks, and uses a shield format for its assay marks. Austrian pieces typically carry millesimal fineness numbers alongside a letter-and-number code identifying the assay district. Vienna was historically one of Europe's most important centres of fine goldsmithing, and antique Austrian pieces with clear assay marks are common in specialist markets.

Dutch Gold Hallmarks

The Netherlands uses a lion rampant alongside the fineness number. The fish symbol appears on Dutch import marks, serving a similar function to the French owl — indicating an item was imported for sale in the Netherlands. Dutch pieces also carry a maker's mark and a millesimal fineness number. The Netherlands has a long tradition of fine goldsmithing, particularly in Amsterdam.

Russian Gold Hallmarks

Russia has one of the most historically layered hallmarking systems in the world, and one of the most sought-after by collectors — primarily because of the extraordinary work produced in the Tsarist era and the specific marks associated with Fabergé.

Pre-Revolutionary Russia (Before 1917)

Imperial Russian gold was marked using the zolotnik system — a unit of purity based on a traditional Russian weight. The numeral appears alongside a city crest identifying the assay office location. St. Petersburg pieces carry two crossed anchors and a sceptre; Moscow pieces show St. George slaying the dragon.

Zolotnik MarkModern EquivalentFineness
5614K gold583 / 585

From 1899, the iconic kokoshnik mark was introduced — a woman's head shown in left profile wearing a traditional kokoshnik headdress. This single mark replaced the old city crests, combining purity and assay location into one symbol. A Greek letter next to the kokoshnik identifies the specific assay office — the letter Iota (І) indicates St. Petersburg.

The kokoshnik is one of the most important dating tools for Russian antiques. Its presence confirms a piece was made in 1899 or later. Its absence — with a city crest instead — indicates pre-1899 manufacture.

Soviet Era (1917–1991)

After the 1917 Revolution, the zolotnik system was replaced with millesimal fineness. Soviet gold marks typically include:

  • Hammer and sickle within a star — state assay mark appearing on items from 1927 onwards
  • Worker's head in profile — a facing-right profile mark used from 1958, replacing the earlier star-and-hammer design; the most commonly encountered Soviet-era assay mark
  • A Cyrillic letter identifying the specific assay office
  • A three-digit fineness number

The 583 mark is particularly characteristic of Soviet gold — it was the standard used throughout the USSR period for what is elsewhere called 14-karat gold. Soviet pieces are frequently encountered in antique markets and are generally well-made, with clear, readable marks.

Fabergé Gold Hallmarks: The Collector's Holy Grail

No discussion of gold hallmarks would be complete without addressing Fabergé — the St. Petersburg goldsmith firm that became synonymous with the highest expression of Imperial Russian decorative arts, and whose marks are among the most scrutinised in the antique world.

Authentic Fabergé pieces carry a specific combination of marks:

  1. The Government Assay Mark — a city crest (pre-1899) or kokoshnik mark (post-1899), confirming the metal was tested by the state. The zolotnik purity number appears alongside — 56 for 14K gold, 72 for 18K.
  2. The Workmaster's Initials — Fabergé did not work alone. Each specialist workmaster registered their initials. The most celebrated include Henrik Wigström (HW), Michael Perchin (MP), and Erik Kollin (EK). These initials are a critical authentication marker.
  3. The Fabergé Firm Mark — the name "FABERGÉ" (or "К. ФАБЕРЖЕ" in Cyrillic) appears on most significant pieces. St. Petersburg pieces typically use Cyrillic; Moscow branch pieces use "К. ФАБЕРЖЕ" alongside the double-headed eagle.
  4. The Imperial Warrant (Double-Headed Eagle) — the Romanov double-headed eagle, indicating Fabergé held the title of official supplier to the Imperial Court since 1885. Typically appears on Moscow branch pieces and highest-prestige St. Petersburg work.

Fabergé fakes are unfortunately common. The most reliable authentication red flags are:

  • Incorrect combinations of marks — Moscow eagle on a piece with St. Petersburg workmaster initials
  • Zolotnik numbers that do not match the stated quality
  • Kokoshnik marks on pieces claiming pre-1899 manufacture
  • Maker's initials not corresponding to any registered Fabergé workmaster

Gold Hallmarks in India: The BIS System

India is the world's second-largest gold jewellery market, and its hallmarking system underwent a fundamental transformation in 2021. BIS hallmarking became mandatory across 256 notified districts from 16 June 2021, with subsequent expansion to 288 districts in 2022 and HUID strictly enforced from September 2023.

The current BIS hallmark consists of four elements:

  1. BIS Triangle Logo — a triangle with a tick mark inside, confirming BIS certification
  2. Purity/Fineness Mark — expressed both as karat and fineness: 22K916, 18K750, or 14K585
  3. Assaying & Hallmarking Centre Mark — a unique code identifying the certified centre where the item was tested
  4. HUID (Hallmark Unique Identification Number) — a six-digit alphanumeric code unique to each piece, verifiable via the government's BIS Care app

The HUID system is particularly innovative — it creates a digital record for every hallmarked piece of gold jewellery sold in India, allowing buyers to verify authenticity instantly on their phone. The 22K/916 standard dominates Indian jewellery production, reflecting the traditional preference for high-purity gold in South Asian jewellery culture.

Middle Eastern and Arabic Gold Hallmarks

Gold is central to the culture and economy of the Middle East — Dubai is one of the world's most important gold trading hubs. All gold sold legally in the UAE must be marked with official purity stamps applied after testing by government-approved bodies. The most common karats in the region are:

MarkKaratPurityNotes
99924K99.9%Pure investment gold
91622K91.6%Traditional Middle Eastern jewellery standard
87521K87.5%Dominant Gulf standard — most distinctive regional identifier
75018K75.0%Modern, Western-influenced designs

The 875 fineness mark is one of the clearest indicators of Middle Eastern origin when encountered on gold in Western markets. Saudi Arabia and Egypt follow the same conventions — Egyptian gold is typically 750 or 875 and carries an official government assay stamp alongside the fineness mark.

Chinese Gold Hallmarks

Chinese gold jewellery uses the millesimal fineness system, with 999 and 9999 being particularly common — reflecting China's preference for high-purity gold jewellery, particularly in 24-karat forms. Common Chinese gold marks include:

  • 999 — 24-karat gold (99.9% pure); the standard for most Chinese gold jewellery
  • 999.5 — high-purity gold (99.95% fine); less common but found on specialist pieces
  • 9999 — investment-grade pure gold (99.99% fine)
  • 足金 (Zú jīn) — Chinese characters meaning "full gold" or 24K gold
  • 千足金 (Qiān zú jīn) — "thousand full gold," indicating 999.9 fine

The 999 mark is the most commonly encountered in Western markets and is straightforward to identify — it is pure 24-karat gold.

White Gold Hallmarks: What to Look For

White gold is an alloy of gold with white metals — typically palladium, nickel, or silver — creating a pale, silver-coloured appearance. It is not a separate standard from yellow gold; it is the same purity levels (9ct, 14ct, 18ct) in a different alloy composition.

White gold carries exactly the same hallmarks as yellow gold. A white gold ring hallmarked 750 is 18-carat gold — the 750 mark identifies the gold content regardless of colour. There is no special white gold hallmark.

The confusion arises because many people assume white gold must carry a different mark — perhaps "WG" or a special symbol. It does not. If a piece is white-coloured and carries a 375, 585, or 750 hallmark with a UK assay office mark, it is hallmarked solid white gold. White gold jewellery is often rhodium-plated for a brighter finish — this plating does not affect the hallmark or the gold's value.

Gold Hallmarks on Pocket Watches

Pocket watch hallmarking is a subject of considerable depth. The case of a pocket watch — distinct from the movement — was manufactured and hallmarked separately from the movement itself. A watch can therefore have a case made in a different year, and even a different city, from the mechanism inside it.

A standard British gold pocket watch case carries:

  1. The fineness mark — 9ct, 14ct, or 18ct are all found on British pocket watches; 18ct being the most prestigious
  2. The assay office mark — London, Birmingham, and Chester are the most commonly encountered; Sheffield rarely hallmarked watch cases
  3. The date letter — particularly useful for watch collectors, as it can date the case to a specific year for cross-reference with the movement's serial number
  4. The sponsor's mark — the case maker's initials, often different from the movement maker

Gold-plated pocket watch cases carry no gold hallmarks — the absence of purity marks on a gold-coloured case is a reliable indicator of plating. Cases may be marked GF (gold filled), RGP (rolled gold plate), or show no gold standard mark at all.

Foreign Gold Hallmarks in the UK

When gold jewellery made abroad is imported into the UK for sale, the importer has two choices: either submit the item to a UK assay office for hallmarking, or sell it as unhallmarked (which is only legal if it is not described as gold).

The UK recognises hallmarks from countries that are members of the Vienna Convention Common Control Mark (CCM) system — currently 19 member states — whose marks are accepted without re-hallmarking. Countries outside these agreements — China, much of the Middle East, India — are not automatically recognised under UK law. A piece of Arabic or Indian gold with a 916 or 750 stamp is genuine gold, but it may lack the specific legal recognition that a UK or CCM hallmark provides. A UK assay office will test and confirm it.

How to Identify Gold Hallmarks: A Practical Method

  1. 1
    Find the mark

    Look with a 10× loupe or magnifying glass inside the ring band, at the clasp of a necklace or bracelet, on the back of a pendant, or on the post of an earring. Good light is essential — natural sunlight or a strong LED torch held at an angle will reveal marks that are invisible in overhead indoor lighting.

  2. 2
    Identify the fineness number

    This is always the most important mark. Look for a three-digit number — 375, 585, 750, 875, 916, or 999. This tells you the gold content before anything else. If you see a zolotnik number (56, 72, 92), the piece is Imperial Russian.

  3. 3
    Identify the assay office symbol

    If the piece is British, look for the leopard's head (London), anchor (Birmingham), rose or crown (Sheffield), or castle (Edinburgh). If it is European, compare against the symbols in this guide — an eagle's head points to French, a crescent moon and crown to German.

  4. 4
    Look for the maker's mark

    Usually two or three initials in a shaped cartouche or shield. If you want to research the maker, this mark — combined with the assay office — narrows your search significantly. Assay offices hold registration records going back centuries.

  5. 5
    Check for a date letter

    On pre-1998 British pieces especially, a single letter in a specific typeface and shield shape indicates the year of hallmarking. Reference books or online date letter tables will give you the exact year. A kokoshnik on Russian gold means 1899 or later.

  6. 6
    If no hallmark is found

    The piece may be pre-1973 and legitimately exempt, it may be gold-plated, it may be foreign gold that was not re-hallmarked for UK sale, or it may not be gold at all. A reputable jeweller or buyer will test the piece with acid or an XRF scanner and provide confirmation.

There is no special "WG" mark or separate white gold standard. If a white-coloured piece carries 375, 585, or 750 with a UK assay office mark, it is hallmarked solid white gold.

875 is the millesimal fineness mark for 21-karat gold (87.5% pure). It is most commonly associated with Middle Eastern jewellery — particularly from the UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt — and is one of the clearest regional identifiers when encountered on gold in Western markets. You will rarely see 875 on British-made gold jewellery.
