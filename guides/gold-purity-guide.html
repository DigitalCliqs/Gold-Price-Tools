Pick up a piece of gold jewellery and the first question most people have is deceptively simple: how much gold is actually in this? The answer lives inside a small stamped number — a hallmark that tells you everything about the metal’s composition, value, durability, and even colour. Understanding gold purity is not just for jewellers or investors. Whether you are buying an engagement ring, selling inherited jewellery, or choosing a gold bar for your portfolio, knowing what 9K, 14K, 18K, 22K, and 24K actually mean puts you firmly in control.

This guide explains the entire gold purity scale — what every karat means in plain terms, how purity affects price and performance, how to test gold purity at home, and which karat is right for which purpose.

What Is Gold Purity and How Is It Measured?

Pure gold, as it comes out of the ground and is refined to its highest form, is a remarkably soft, deeply yellow, completely non-reactive metal. It does not tarnish, corrode, or rust. But that softness — the same quality that made early civilisations able to hammer it into intricate shapes — is also a practical problem for everyday objects. A ring made of pure gold would bend and scratch with ordinary wear within days.

To solve this, gold is almost always mixed with other metals to form an alloy — a blend that preserves gold’s beauty while adding the durability needed for jewellery, coins, and industrial use. Gold purity simply describes how much actual gold is present in that alloy. There are two major systems for expressing gold purity used around the world today.

System One
The Karat (K) System

The karat system divides any piece of metal into 24 equal parts and counts how many of those parts are pure gold. Each karat represents one twenty-fourth — or approximately 4.17% — of the total. Standard in North America, the UK, and English-speaking markets.

  • 24K24 / 24 · 100%
  • 18K18 / 24 · 75%
  • 14K14 / 24 · 58.3%
  • 9K9 / 24 · 37.5%
System Two
The Millesimal Fineness System

Millesimal fineness expresses purity in parts per thousand. It is the standard used across continental Europe, in international trade, and on most internationally hallmarked pieces. A ring stamped “585” and a ring stamped “14K” contain identical gold — just different notation.

  • 99924K · 99.9% pure
  • 75018K · 75.0% pure
  • 58514K · 58.5% pure
  • 3759K · 37.5% pure

The spelling “karat” (K) is used for gold purity in most countries; “carat” (ct) is used for the same measurement in the UK, Australia, and parts of Europe. In the diamond trade, carat means something entirely different — a unit of gemstone weight equal to 0.2 grams. Context matters.

Every Karat Level Explained in Detail

Each karat sits at a different point on the purity scale — and serves a different practical purpose. Here is what each level actually means for colour, durability, price, and use.

9K Gold — 37.5% Pure

Nine karat gold sits at the bottom of the mainstream purity scale, containing just 37.5% pure gold — the remaining 62.5% is silver, copper, and zinc. Despite the modest gold content, 9K is widely used in markets where durability and affordability take priority over maximum gold content.

Because of its high alloy content, 9K gold is significantly harder than higher-karat grades and resists scratching well — making it practical for daily wear. It also carries the lowest material cost of any “true” gold alloy. The trade-offs are real: a paler, more washed-out yellow tone, and the higher copper content can trigger skin reactions in people with metal sensitivities.

Best for Budget-conscious buyers, fashion jewellery, pieces that see heavy daily wear. Not recommended for investors — gold content is too low to hold meaningful intrinsic value.

10K Gold — 41.7% Pure

Ten karat gold contains 10 parts gold to 14 parts alloy. It is primarily a North American convention — the minimum legal standard for items sold as “gold” in the United States — and common for budget and mid-range jewellery.

At this purity level, 10K is the most scratch-resistant of all standard gold alloys — harder even than platinum in many conditions — because the base metal components dominate the relatively soft gold.

Best for US buyers seeking affordable gold jewellery, pieces requiring maximum durability (children’s jewellery, sports accessories), budget engagement rings.

14K Gold — 58.3% Pure

At 58.3% gold content, 14K is the most popular jewellery karat in the United States and widely used across Central and Eastern Europe. It strikes a practical balance: high enough gold content for a distinctly warm yellow colour, with enough alloy (41.7%) to provide excellent durability for everyday wear.

14K white gold — widely used for engagement rings in North American markets — uses palladium (9.5%) and silver (32.2%) as whitening agents. At May 2026 prices, 14K gold is worth approximately $85–$86 per gram in pure gold content.

Best for Engagement rings, wedding bands, everyday jewellery, US and Central European markets. The sweet spot for buyers who want the gold aesthetic without the premium cost of 18K.

18K Gold — 75% Pure

Eighteen karat gold is the global standard for fine jewellery. With three-quarters of the metal pure gold, it produces the rich, deep yellow most people associate with quality gold, while the 25% alloy provides the structural strength needed for complex stone-set pieces.

This is the standard inside luxury watches by Patek Philippe, Rolex, and Cartier, and across the world’s top maisons. White 18K uses platinum or palladium (25%) for its silver tone; rose 18K uses approximately 22.25% copper plus 2.75% silver. All retain identical 75% gold value. Approximately $109–$110 per gram in pure gold content at current prices.

Best for Fine jewellery, luxury watches, European engagement rings, pieces where rich gold colour matters, people sensitive to base metal allergies (lower nickel content).

22K Gold — 91.7% Pure

22K represents the highest purity level commonly used in wearable jewellery. The deep, intensely yellow colour is unmistakable and culturally significant in many markets. South Asian weddings — particularly in India — are the defining market: bridal sets, temple jewellery, and heirlooms are almost exclusively 22K or higher.

22K is unsuitable for stones and complex prong settings because the metal remains relatively soft. Its use is largely restricted to plain chains, bangles, and coin manufacture. Many famous investment coins — including the South African Krugerrand and British Sovereign — are struck in 22K gold.

Best for South Asian bridal jewellery, traditional and cultural gold pieces, plain gold chains and bangles, investment coins.

24K Gold — 99.9% Pure

24 karat gold is the purest commercially available form: 99.9% pure (often 999.9 for highest-grade bullion, indicating 99.99% purity). There is no higher karat — you cannot go above 24K. This is the gold of investment bars, world-class minted coins (Canadian Maple Leaf, Chinese Gold Panda), central bank reserves, and industrial electronics.

24K has a vivid, saturated yellow more intense than any lower karat, and produces no allergic reactions associated with base metal alloys. However it is extremely soft — it scratches with a fingernail, bends easily, and cannot hold gemstones — making it impractical for most wearable jewellery.

Best for Investment bars and bullion coins, electronics and aerospace applications, people who want maximum gold content regardless of durability, collectors.

Gold Colours: Yellow, White and Rose Gold Purity

Gold’s natural colour is the warm yellow most people picture — but the same gold can be transformed into white, rose, or even green through alloying. The purity at any given karat is identical regardless of colour; what changes is the composition of the non-gold portion. A white 18K ring and a yellow 18K ring contain the same 75% gold and carry the same intrinsic metal value.

Yellow Gold · Default

Yellow Gold

The “default” — gold alloyed primarily with silver and copper in proportions that preserve the characteristic warm hue. At higher karats (22K, 24K), the deep saturation of pure gold dominates. At lower karats (9K, 10K), more alloy dilutes the colour toward a paler, slightly greenish tone.

White Gold · Pd/Pt alloy

White Gold

Created by alloying gold with whitening metals — primarily palladium or platinum, sometimes nickel and zinc. At 18K, a white gold alloy is 75% gold with 25% palladium/platinum. Almost always finished with a rhodium plating for the bright, silver-like appearance customers expect.

Rose Gold · Cu-heavy alloy

Rose Gold

Gets its characteristic pink-to-red tone from a higher copper content. A typical 18K rose gold alloy contains 75% gold, 22.25% copper, and 2.75% silver. Copper content can be adjusted to produce shades from pale champagne pink through to deep reddish “red gold”.

Gold Purity by Country: Regional Preferences

Gold purity preferences vary significantly around the world, shaped by cultural tradition, market convention, and regulatory standards. What is considered “real gold” in one country may be regarded as low-grade in another — understanding regional norms is essential when buying or selling internationally.

South Asia
India, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka
22K (916) Standard

22K gold dominates jewellery, particularly bridal and traditional pieces. Gold is deeply embedded in wedding traditions and festivals including Diwali and Dhanteras — purity treated as a direct expression of value. 24K coins and bars are also popular for investment.

East Asia
China, Japan, Hong Kong, Taiwan
24K (999) Preferred

China has historically preferred 24K (“pure gold”) jewellery, especially heavy chains and investment pieces. Japan favours 18K for fashion but has a strong 24K tradition for heavy chains (kihei). Hong Kong is a major global trading hub carrying all karat levels.

Middle East
UAE, Saudi Arabia, Turkey
21K & 22K Dominant

21K (875) and 22K (916) are dominant standards for jewellery, reflecting regional preference for high-purity gold. Gold souqs in Dubai carry vast selections of 21K and 22K pieces alongside 18K options for international buyers.

Europe
Western Europe, UK, Germany
18K (750) Fine Standard

18K is the predominant standard for fine jewellery across Western Europe. 9K (375) is common in the UK, Ireland, and some Nordic countries for everyday and budget pieces. 14K is more common in Germany and Central Europe.

North America
United States & Canada
14K (585) Most Popular

14K is the most popular jewellery karat in the US and Canada. 10K (417) represents the legal minimum for gold sale. 18K is positioned as “luxury” and commands a premium. 24K bullion bars and coins are the standard for investment.

Latin America
Brazil, Mexico, Argentina
18K with 14K & 10K

18K dominates as the fine jewellery standard across South and Central America, with 14K and 10K used for fashion pieces. Investment-grade bullion follows the global 999.9 fine convention.

How to Check Gold Purity: A Practical Guide

Knowing the theory is one thing — being able to verify gold purity in your hands is another. Here are all the methods available, from quick home tests to professional laboratory analysis. Used together, these methods give a reliable indication of authenticity; used alone, each has limitations.

1

Look for Hallmarks

The single fastest and most reliable starting point. Use a jeweller’s loupe (10× magnification) or a magnifying glass. On rings, the stamp is typically on the inside of the band. On chains, near the clasp. On larger pieces, check less visible areas like the back or underside.

999 916 750 585 417 375 14K 18K

A hallmark from a recognised assay office or known maker is strong evidence of authenticity. However, hallmarks can be forged — particularly on cheaper mass-produced pieces from unregulated markets — so additional verification is sometimes warranted.

2

The Magnet Test

You need: A strong neodymium (rare-earth) magnet — available at hardware stores for a few dollars.

What it tells you: Gold is non-magnetic. A strong neodymium magnet should produce absolutely zero attraction to genuine gold, regardless of karat. If a piece is attracted to the magnet, it contains iron or steel — either fake or gold-plated over a base metal core.

Limitation: The magnet test only screens for iron/steel cores. It does not detect brass, copper, or aluminium cores — all non-magnetic but not gold. Passing this test eliminates one category of fakes but does not confirm authenticity.
3

The Float / Water Density Test

You need: A glass of water and (optional) a scale accurate to 0.01 grams for the advanced version.

Basic float test: Real gold sinks immediately and completely. Gold has a density of 19.32 g/cm³ — much denser than most common base metals. Any piece that floats or hangs suspended rather than sinking straight to the bottom is almost certainly not gold.

Density calculation (advanced): By weighing in air and again submerged, you can calculate approximate density. Reference values: 24K yellow gold ~19.3 g/cm³; 18K yellow gold ~15.2–15.9 g/cm³; 14K yellow gold ~12.9–14.6 g/cm³; 18K white gold ~14.7–16.9 g/cm³.

Limitation: This is a rough screening method. Composite fakes (gold-plated over heavy base metal) can mimic the density of certain alloys.
4

The Acid Test (Nitric Acid)

You need: A gold testing acid kit (jewellery supply stores, $20–$60) with nitric acid solutions matched to karat levels, a touchstone, plus protective gloves and eyewear.

A small scratch is made on a touchstone using the piece. A drop of karat-specific acid is applied. The acid’s reaction indicates gold content: 24K reacts minimally to all standard gold acids; lower karats show progressively stronger reactions; fake gold (copper, brass, base metal) dissolves or discolours immediately.

This is the most definitive home-accessible test and is widely used by second-hand jewellers, pawn shops, and precious metal buyers worldwide.

Limitation: Slightly destructive (leaves a small scratch mark). Provides approximate karat identification rather than a precise purity reading. Requires careful handling of corrosive acids.
5

Household Item Tests (Use With Caution)

Several informal tests circulate online. Here is an honest assessment — these are quick preliminary filters, not conclusive tests:

VinegarMildly useful

Real gold doesn’t react; base metals may darken or corrode. Vinegar is too weak to discriminate karats reliably and can cause minor surface damage on some alloys.

ToothpasteRough indicator

Genuine gold should emerge clean and bright. Base metals or plating may leave a dark mark. Extremely rough indicator only.

BleachNot recommended

Genuine gold doesn’t react. Bleach can permanently damage jewellery — even genuine pieces — and provides no karat information. Avoid this test.

Skin MarksUnreliable

Fake or heavily alloyed gold can leave green/black marks from copper oxidation. Many genuine low-karat alloys also temporarily mark skin. Not a standalone test.

Professional Gold Purity Testing Methods

For definitive, precise gold purity measurement — needed when buying or selling significant quantities — professional testing is the only reliable standard. These methods are used by assay offices, refineries, and precious metal dealers worldwide.

XRF
Industry Standard

XRF (X-Ray Fluorescence) Testing

XRF gold purity testing is the most widely used professional method and has largely replaced traditional fire assay in commercial buying and selling. An XRF machine fires high-energy X-rays at a sample, causing the metal’s atoms to emit characteristic fluorescent X-rays that identify each element and its concentration.

Results appear on screen within seconds with accuracy typically within 0.1% of true purity. Modern handheld XRF analyzers cost $15,000–$30,000, making them standard equipment for professional dealers and assay offices rather than home buyers.

Key advantages
  • Non-destructive — no scratching or damage
  • Fast — results in seconds
  • Accurate — identifies exact karat to within 0.1%
  • Multi-element — reveals full alloy composition
  • Can distinguish surface plating from core metal
SIGMA
Electromagnetic

Sigma Metalytics Precious Metal Verifier

Uses electromagnetic fields at multiple frequencies to measure the electrical conductivity and magnetic permeability of a metal. Because each metal and alloy has a unique electrical signature, the device can identify gold purity without surface contact — even through a coin flip or sealed packaging.

Particularly useful for testing gold coins quickly. The verifier doesn’t reveal exact percentage composition but excels at confirming whether a coin matches its declared purity profile.

FIRE
Legal Standard

Fire Assay

The oldest and most accurate method for gold purity determination — and the legal standard for trade disputes and official certification. A small sample is dissolved in flux and melted, precious metals are separated chemically, and the gold is weighed against the original sample.

Fire assay is destructive and slow, requiring laboratory conditions and skilled technicians. Used by national assay offices, mints, and refineries for official certification rather than everyday testing.

Pure Gold Value = Weight (g) × Purity Fraction × Spot Price (per gram)

This figure represents the metal’s intrinsic value only. Buyback rates from dealers will typically be 85–95% of this figure depending on the seller and market conditions.

Which Gold Purity Should You Choose?

The right karat depends entirely on the purpose. Use this decision matrix to match your priorities — investment value, durability, cultural fit, or skin compatibility — to the appropriate purity level.

Investment · Bullion
Choose 24K (or 22K coins)

Always 24K (999.9 fine) for bars, or 22K for recognised investment coins (Krugerrand, Sovereign). Maximum gold content equals maximum intrinsic value and the tightest possible spread over spot price.

Fine Jewellery · Everyday Wear
Choose 18K (750)

The optimal balance — rich gold colour, high purity, and sufficient alloy strength for daily rings, chains, and earrings. The global standard used by the world’s finest jewellery makers.

Engagement Rings · Durability Focus
Choose 14K or 18K

14K (585) in North American markets; 18K in European and South American markets. Both offer excellent durability for daily wear. 14K is harder; 18K is richer in colour.

Budget · Fashion Pieces
Choose 9K or 10K

9K (375) or 10K (417) provide the most durability at the lowest cost. Understand that you are buying mostly alloy with a gold content of less than 42%.

South Asian · Bridal & Ceremonial
Choose 22K (916)

22K is the cultural and commercial standard. Pieces below this purity are often viewed with suspicion in these markets regardless of their actual gold content.

Metal Sensitivities · Allergies
Choose 18K+ (palladium)

Higher karat gold (18K and above with palladium-based alloys). For white gold, ensure it uses palladium rather than nickel — nickel is the primary culprit in gold-related allergic reactions.

Frequently Asked Questions on Gold Purity

24K (999.9 fine) is the highest purity grade for commercial gold. Investment bullion is typically 99.99% pure (“four nines fine”). Scientifically, gold can be refined to even higher purities for electronic applications, but 99.99% is the practical ceiling for the bullion market.

916 is the fineness stamp for 22K gold — indicating that 916 parts per thousand (91.6%) of the metal are pure gold. This is the standard for South Asian bridal gold and many world gold coins including the Krugerrand and British Sovereign.

Neither is objectively “better” — it depends on your priorities. 18K contains more gold (75% vs 58.3%), has richer colour, and is less likely to cause skin reactions. 14K is harder, more scratch-resistant, and significantly less expensive. For investment value, 18K wins. For durability and budget, 14K wins.

The most reliable home methods are: (1) hallmark check with a magnifying glass, (2) magnet test with a neodymium magnet, (3) water density test, and (4) acid testing with a nitric acid kit. Using all four together gives a reliable indication. For definitive confirmation, professional XRF testing is the gold standard.

In the context of gold, “karat” (K) and “carat” (ct) mean exactly the same thing — a measurement of gold purity out of 24 parts. “Carat” is the spelling used in the UK, Australia, and parts of Europe; “karat” is used in the US and most other countries. The gem trade uses “carat” to mean something different (a unit of gem weight, 0.2 grams), so context matters.

22 ÷ 24 × 100 = 91.67%. Alternatively, the fineness figure of 916 can be read directly as 91.6%.

White gold purity is the same as its karat equivalent in yellow gold. 18K white gold is 75% pure gold — identical to 18K yellow gold. The only difference is the composition of the 25% alloy: whitening metals (palladium or platinum) replace the silver and copper used in yellow gold.
