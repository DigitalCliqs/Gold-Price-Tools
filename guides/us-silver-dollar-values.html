Few coins attract as much passion — or cause as much confusion — as the US silver dollar. Some are worth little more than their weight in silver; others are worth hundreds of thousands to the right collector. Telling them apart follows clear, learnable rules.

The single most important thing to know: not every coin marketed as a "US silver dollar" contains silver. The Eisenhower dollar is only silver if it carries the "S" mint mark, and the Susan B. Anthony dollar contains none at all.

Melt value — the foundation of all pricing

Every silver dollar has a melt value: the worth of the actual silver it contains, regardless of age or condition. Melt value sets a floor — in a working market no genuine silver coin should sell for less, because a dealer could always melt it for profit.

The universal melt-value formula
Melt value=silver weight (troy oz)×silver spot ($/oz)

The Morgan dollar (1878–1904, 1921)

The Morgan dollar is the most popular US silver coin in history. Named after US Mint engraver George T. Morgan, it was struck from 1878 to 1904, then revived for one final year in 1921 before the Peace dollar replaced it.

What drives Morgan dollar value

Every Morgan has two value components: its melt value (silver × spot) and its numismatic premium (what collectors pay above melt for rarity, condition and mint mark). Common dates trade at a modest premium; key dates trade at huge multiples of their silver content.

  • Date & mint mark — low-mintage or heavily-melted combinations are scarce. The 1893-S and the proof-only 1895 are the two most famous rarities.
  • Condition / grade — an MS-65 coin can be worth 50–100× the same date in Good (G-4).
  • Original surfaces — cleaned coins lose most of their premium and often sell near melt regardless of date.
  • Eye appeal — strike quality, luster and a lack of bag marks all matter.

Mint marks and where to find them

On Morgan dollars the mint mark sits on the reverse, just below the ribbon knot beneath the eagle. Carson City coins are the most sought-after — low production plus the romance of the frontier silver-mining era.

The 1921 Morgan dollar

The 1921 is the final year and the most commonly encountered Morgan — tens of millions were struck at three mints (Philadelphia, Denver and San Francisco) as part of a re-mint programme. Expect roughly $60–$80 in average circulated condition and $65–$120 in lower uncirculated grades. It is an ideal entry coin — affordable and historic — but don't overpay for a circulated example, as they are common.

The Peace dollar (1921–1935)

Introduced in 1921 to mark the end of World War I — "Peace" is inscribed on the reverse as a deliberate statement — the Peace dollar shares the Morgan's 90% silver, 0.7734 oz composition but a distinctly different look, with Liberty facing right rather than left. 1921 Peace dollar — special status

The 1921 is the only High Relief Peace dollar — struck in greater depth than later years for a sculptural look — and was minted at Philadelphia only, in small numbers. As a result it commands a premium: often $100–$200 circulated and $300–$500+ in lower uncirculated grades.

1922 & 1923 — the common dates

The 1922 is the most common Peace dollar — over 51 million struck at Philadelphia alone — and the 1923 Philadelphia issue had one of the highest mintages in the series (30+ million). Both trade near the melt floor: about $58–$75 circulated and $85–$175 uncirculated.

The Eisenhower dollar (1971–1978) — mostly not silver

The Eisenhower ("Ike") dollar causes huge confusion because it exists in both silver and non-silver versions — and most examples contain no silver at all.

The key rule

Only Eisenhower dollars struck in San Francisco (the "S" mint mark) from 1971 to 1976 contain silver. Every Philadelphia (P / no mark) and Denver (D) Ike is copper-nickel clad, with zero silver.

The S-mint silver Ikes are 40% silver (versus 90% for Morgan and Peace) and weigh 24.59 g (vs 22.68 g for the clad version). At $75.00/oz their melt value is about $23.71.

Specific year values

  • 1971: most are clad (no silver) — only the 1971-S is silver. A circulated 1971-S ≈ $10–$20; a proof ≈ $15–$30. The standard clad 1971 is worth $1–$5.
  • 1972: the 1972-S is the silver version ($15–$30). The Philadelphia 1972 has three reverse varieties — the Type 2 reverse is the prize at $25–$200+ in mint state.
  • 1974: the 1974-S silver dollar ≈ $12–$25; the clad 1974 has little value above face.
  • 1976 Bicentennial: dated 1776–1976 with the Liberty Bell-and-moon reverse. Only the 1976-S is silver — ≈ $10–$30.
  • 1977 & 1978: no silver was made in these years at any mint. There are no silver 1977 or 1978 Eisenhower dollars.

The Susan B. Anthony dollar (1979–1981, 1999) — not silver

No silver content

The SBA dollar is copper-nickel clad — the same composition as a modern quarter. It is the same size as a quarter but carries a $1 face value, which is exactly why so many are mistaken for silver.

Most circulated 1979 SBA dollars are worth exactly $1 — their face value. A few varieties and high grades do carry genuine premiums:

  • 1979-P Wide Rim (Near Date) — the date sits closer to the rim than usual. About $6–$10 circulated, $30–$50 in MS-65, and $100–$10,000+ in MS-67 or finer.
  • 1979-S Type II proof — a cleaner "S" from a mid-year die change; about $45+ in proof.
  • 1981-S Type II proof — the rarest SBA variety; about $100+ in proof.

Auction record: ~$10,000

A 1979-P Wide Rim graded MS-68 has sold for roughly $10,000 — the exception that proves the rule. For most circulated SBA dollars, there is no premium above face value.

The American Silver Eagle (1986–present)

2021 modern Morgan & Peace reissues

In 2021 the US Mint reissued both designs in 99.9% fine silver — higher purity than the 90% classics. These were sold as collectibles and each contains 0.8594 troy oz of silver (live melt about $64.46).

  • 2021 Morgan — five mint-mark versions (P, D, S, O, CC). Original issue price was $85–$95; market values now run ~$85–$200+, with the CC and O mint marks carrying the highest collector premiums.
  • 2021 Peace — a Philadelphia-only issue; current market values roughly $90–$150 depending on condition and certification.
  • 2022–2023 — Carson City Morgan and Philadelphia Peace issues followed similar pricing, completing the Mint's revival of the series.

The first US silver dollar: 1794 Flowing Hair

The Flowing Hair dollar is widely considered the first dollar coin ever struck by the United States federal government, with the first examples produced on 15 October 1794. Only about 1,758 were made in 1794, making genuine examples extraordinarily rare.

$10,016,875

The most famous 1794 Flowing Hair dollar sold at auction in January 2013 — at the time, the most valuable coin ever sold at auction. Even worn, authenticated examples are worth $100,000 to $500,000 or more.

Production continued into 1795 (about 160,295 coins) before the Draped Bust design took over. 1795 examples are far more available than 1794 specimens and typically trade in the $1,500–$15,000+ range depending on condition.

How to value your silver dollars, step by step

Identify the coin type and year

Look at the obverse (front). Is it Liberty with flowing hair, a profile bust, a seated Liberty, a Morgan or Peace Liberty, Eisenhower, Susan B. Anthony, or the Walking Liberty of the Eagle? Match it to the series above.

Find the mint mark

  • Morgan & Peace: reverse, below the eagle
  • Eisenhower: obverse, below the neck
  • Susan B. Anthony: obverse, to the left of the portrait
  • Silver Eagle: edge or no mark (Philadelphia)

Confirm it is actually silver

An Eisenhower with no "S" mint mark contains zero silver. Check the edge: a silver coin shows a solid silver-grey edge, while a clad coin shows a visible copper-coloured stripe.

Calculate the melt value

Silver weight × current spot price. For a Morgan or Peace dollar that's 0.7734 × spot — at $75.00/oz, about $58.01. This is the absolute floor for any non-collectible coin.

Look up the numismatic value

Use PCGS, NGC or a bullion dealer's coin guide for your exact date, mint mark and grade. If it's worth significantly more than melt, consider professional grading before you sell.

Frequently asked questions

How much is a US silver dollar worth?
It depends entirely on which dollar. Common Morgan and Peace dollars are worth about $58–$75 at current silver prices thanks to their 0.7734 oz of silver. Key-date Morgans (1893-S, 1895, 1889-CC) can be worth thousands. Eisenhower and SBA dollars may be worth only their $1 face value if they contain no silver.
How much does a US silver dollar weigh?
Morgan and Peace dollars weigh 26.73 grams. Silver (40%) Eisenhower dollars weigh 24.59 grams, and American Silver Eagles weigh 31.10 grams (one troy ounce).
What is a Morgan silver dollar worth today?
At a silver spot near $75.00/oz, a common-date circulated Morgan dollar is worth roughly $58–$80 depending on condition. Key dates and high-grade examples can be worth dramatically more.
What is a 1921 Morgan silver dollar worth?
About $60–$80 in average circulated condition at current silver prices, with a small premium in lower uncirculated grades. The 1921 is the most common Morgan date and holds little numismatic premium in circulated grades.
How much is a 1922 or 1923 Peace dollar worth?
About $58–$75 for a circulated example — essentially the melt value of its 0.7734 oz of silver. Uncirculated coins in MS-63 or better start around $85–$175.
Are 1972 silver dollars worth anything?
The 1972-S silver Eisenhower dollar (40% silver) is worth about $15–$30. The 1972-P Type 2 reverse clad dollar can be worth $25–$200+ in mint state as a variety. The common 1972 clad dollar has minimal value above face.
Is there a silver 1979 dollar?
No — the 1979 Susan B. Anthony dollar is copper-nickel and contains no silver. Most circulated examples are worth exactly $1. The Wide Rim variety and high-grade proofs can be worth $6 to $10,000+ depending on the specifics.
What is the rarest US silver dollar?
By sheer value, the 1794 Flowing Hair dollar is arguably the most valuable — one sold for $10,016,875 in 2013. Among widely collected series, the 1893-S Morgan dollar is the most famous rarity, with high-grade examples worth $100,000+.
