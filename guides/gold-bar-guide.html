Pick up a 1 oz gold bar and it feels deceptively small — a slab roughly the size of a credit card, two millimetres thin, that fits easily in the palm of your hand. Yet at May 2026 prices, that modest rectangle of metal is worth close to $5,000.

Understanding how gold bars are sized, weighed, priced, and valued is the foundation of any serious gold investment plan. Whether you are researching a 1 gram bar as a first purchase, comparing 100 gram vs 1 kg options, or simply want to understand how much a gold bar is worth at today’s prices — everything you need is here.

How Gold Bar Weight Is Measured

Before comparing gold bar sizes, it helps to understand the weight system used across the global bullion trade. Gold is not measured in regular ounces — it uses the troy ounce system, a unit that dates back to medieval Troyes, France, and remains the global standard for precious metals today.

Key Troy Ounce Conversions
31.1035 g = 1 troy ounce
32.1507 oz = 1 kilogram
12 troy oz = 1 troy pound
14.58 oz ≈ 1 regular pound

This distinction matters practically: a bar described as “1 oz” in the bullion market always means one troy ounce, or 31.1 grams — not the 28.35 grams a regular ounce would represent. Every major refinery, exchange, and dealer uses troy ounces.

What Purity Does a Gold Bar Need to Be?

Investment-grade gold bars carry a purity of 999.9 fine (also written as 24 karat, 0.9999, or 99.99% pure gold). This is the global standard for retail bullion bars produced by accredited refineries.

Cast vs Minted Gold Bars: What Is the Difference?

Not all gold bars are made the same way. There are two fundamental manufacturing methods, and understanding the difference helps buyers choose more intelligently.

Cast Gold Bars
Molten gold poured into mould
  • Rough texture, natural surface variations
  • Traditional “authentic” appearance
  • Lower premium above spot
  • Mainly 100g, 250g, 500g, 1kg, 400oz
  • Usually without sealed assay card
  • Hallmark + serial number markings
Best for: Large-scale investment — lowest cost per gram
VS
Minted Gold Bars
Pressed from rolled gold strips
  • Polished finish, sharp precise edges
  • Intricate design on the reverse
  • Slightly higher premium above spot
  • Mainly 1g to 100g, up to 10oz
  • Often in tamper-proof sealed assay card
  • Serial + assay certificate + anti-counterfeiting
Best for: Gifts, new investors — maximum security

Both types are 999.9 pure — purity is identical. The choice between cast and minted is primarily about premium cost, aesthetics, and intended use.

Where to Buy Gold Bars: Trusted Bullion Dealers

The safest and most cost-effective way to purchase gold bars for most investors is through an established, accredited online bullion dealer. Reputable platforms consistently offer lower premiums than banks or jewellers.

APMEX (American Precious Metals Exchange)
North America

One of the largest and most respected bullion dealers globally. Vast inventory from every major refinery. Has handled billions of dollars of transactions. Particularly strong in North America.

JM Bullion
North America

Known for transparent pricing, frequent low-premium promotions on 1 oz bars, and a clean buying experience with free shipping above certain order values.

Kitco
North America · Montreal

Long-established precious metals dealer and information platform. Kitco’s prices are widely referenced as a market benchmark. Offers storage solutions alongside physical purchases.

BullionByPost
United Kingdom

UK’s largest online bullion dealer by volume. Highly competitive pricing on Royal Mint, PAMP, and Valcambi bars. Free insured delivery standard. Among the most transparent buyback programmes in the UK.

BullionVault
Global · Online

Near-wholesale pricing via fractional access to institutional bars stored in allocated vaults in London, New York, Zurich, Singapore, and Toronto. Ideal for cost-sensitive investors who want physical exposure without taking delivery.

What to look for when choosing a bullion dealer
  • Membership in an accredited industry body (LBMA, BNTA, PNG, or national equivalent)
  • Transparent pricing showing the exact premium over spot
  • A published buyback programme with clear, pre-stated pricing
  • Independent customer reviews on Trustpilot or the Better Business Bureau
  • Secure, tracked, fully insured delivery for physical orders
  • Audited vault storage options if you are not taking delivery

Gold Bar Scams: How to Spot Fakes and Protect Yourself

The gold market attracts a small but persistent number of fraudsters. Understanding how to verify authenticity is not paranoia — it is prudent due diligence for any significant purchase.

Tungsten-Core Bars

Tungsten is nearly identical in density to gold (19.25 vs 19.32 g/cm³). Sophisticated fakes can pass visual and weight checks. Only ultrasonic testing reliably detects density inconsistencies.

Gold-Plated Base Metal

Cheaper fakes use copper, lead, or iron with a thin gold electroplate. These fail a magnet test immediately — genuine gold has zero magnetic response. Any attraction is an instant red flag.

Fake Assay Certificates

Convincing certificates with no legitimate digital verification. PAMP Suisse’s Veriscan app authenticates each bar against a global database of unique surface scans captured at manufacture.

How to Verify a Gold Bar — 6 Steps
1
Buy from an accredited dealer only — this single step eliminates the vast majority of counterfeit risk
2
Magnet test — genuine gold has zero magnetic response; any attraction is an immediate red flag
3
Weight and dimensions — use a digital scale accurate to 0.01g; even a 0.1g discrepancy on a 1 oz bar warrants investigation
4
Hallmarks and serial number — every investment-grade bar carries a refiner’s hallmark, purity stamp, weight, and unique serial number
5
Assay packaging integrity — opened or re-sealed assay card is a serious red flag; do not accept it
6
Professional assay — XRF (X-ray fluorescence) or fire assay provides definitive purity confirmation for any significant purchase from an unfamiliar source

Gold Bars vs Gold Coins: Which Is the Better Investment?

This is one of the most common questions for new gold investors. The honest answer is that it depends on your priorities.

Gold Bars
Lower premiums at 100g and above
Better grams-for-dollar at large sizes
1kg bars: just 1–2% over spot
Large bars are less divisible to sell
No CGT exemption in most jurisdictions
VS
Gold Coins
Greater liquidity — sell one coin at a time
Potential CGT exemptions (varies by country)
Widely recognised and universally trusted
Higher per-gram premium (4–8%)
Takes longer to build a large position
For most first-time investors: A 1 oz gold bar or a mix of 1 oz gold coins provides the best starting point — combining moderate premiums, global liquidity, recognisability, and accessibility into a practical entry-level position.

Gold Bar Investment Strategy by Budget

Physical gold bar investment works best when approached systematically. Here is a practical framework for different investment levels.

Under $5,000
1 oz gold bar

Start with a 1 oz bar from PAMP Suisse, Perth Mint, or Valcambi in a sealed assay card. Alternatively, two to three 10 gram bars for slightly more flexibility. Avoid 1 gram bars at this level — the premium-to-content ratio is poor for investment purposes.

$5,000 – $25,000
1 oz + 100 gram bars

A combination of 1 oz and 100g bars balances premium efficiency with resale flexibility. A 100g bar at ~$15,400 carries a lower premium per gram than a 1 oz bar, and is still easy to sell to any major dealer.

$25,000 – $150,000
100g, 250g, or 1kg bars

100g and 250g bars offer solid cost efficiency, or a 1kg bar if you do not anticipate needing to sell in portions. The 1kg gold bar carries the best premium-to-value ratio available to private investors.

Above $150,000
1kg bars + vault storage

Mix of 1kg bars for cost efficiency plus a smaller allocation to 1 oz bars for liquidity. At very large allocations, allocated professional vault storage becomes important — insuring $500,000+ of gold at home is both expensive and practically difficult to arrange.

The Swiss Gold Bar Legacy

Switzerland occupies a singular place in the global gold bar market. Three of the world’s most trusted brands — PAMP Suisse, Valcambi, and Argor-Heraeus — are all Swiss-based refineries, reinforced by the country’s political neutrality, robust financial law, and deep ties to the LBMA-governed global bullion market.

PAMP vs Valcambi: PAMP commands a slightly higher premium due to its design reputation and Veriscan technology. Valcambi offers comparable purity and global recognition at marginally lower cost. Both are excellent investment-grade choices.

Storing Your Gold Bars Safely

Owning gold bars creates a storage and security obligation that is just as important as the purchase decision itself.

Home Storage
Up to ~$20,000–$30,000

Quality in-wall or floor-mounted safe, anchored and concealed. Ensure home insurance explicitly covers bullion — standard contents policies usually do not, or impose very low sub-limits.

Cost: safe price + bullion insurance premium
Bank Safety Deposit
Any size holding

Institutional-grade physical security at very low cost. Limitation: access only during bank hours. Some countries have restricted box contents to non-financial instruments in recent years.

Cost: ~$50–$200 per year
Recommended for large holdings
Professional Vault
Any size · fully insured · allocated

Allocated, insured, independently audited storage. BullionVault, Sharps Pixley, and GoldCore offer vaults across London, Zurich, New York, Singapore, and Toronto.

Cost: ~0.12% of metal value per year

Frequently Asked Questions About Gold Bars

Gold bars come in sizes from 1 gram to 400 troy ounces (12.4 kg). The most popular retail weight is 1 troy ounce (31.1 grams). The standard institutional bar — the LBMA Good Delivery bar — weighs approximately 400 troy ounces or 12.4 kilograms.

At May 2026 spot prices (~$4,675/oz): a 1 oz bar retails for approximately $4,800–$4,950; a 100 gram bar for $15,200–$15,800; a 1 kg bar for $153,000–$156,000; and a 400 oz Good Delivery bar for $1.92–$2.06 million. Use the live prices at the top of this page for the most current valuations.

For most investors, a 1 oz bar from PAMP Suisse, Perth Mint, or Valcambi offers the best combination of low premium, global liquidity, strong brand recognition, and anti-counterfeiting features. For larger budgets, 100 gram and 1 kg bars offer better value per gram.

A 24 karat gold bar means the bar is 99.9% or 99.99% pure gold — the same as 999 or 999.9 fine. All investment-grade gold bars are 24 karat (24K) by definition. Lower karats indicate an alloy and are not used in bullion bar production.

It depends on the bar. The most popular retail size contains 1 troy ounce (31.1 grams). A kilogram bar contains 32.15 troy ounces. The standard Good Delivery bar weighs approximately 400 troy ounces. Bars are also produced in 1g, 2.5g, 5g, 10g, 20g, 50g, 100g, 250g, 500g, and 10 troy oz sizes.

Costco has sold 1 oz gold bars (typically PAMP Suisse) in limited quantities. Walmart lists gold bars through third-party marketplace sellers. While not inherently fraudulent, you should verify the seller’s credentials carefully and compare pricing against specialist bullion dealers, who typically offer wider selection, clearer provenance, and dedicated buyback programmes.

Fractional gold bars (1g, 2.5g, 5g, 10g) are useful for gifts, building a small starting position, or dollar-cost averaging in small increments. As pure investments, their higher per-gram premiums (15–25%) mean you need meaningful price appreciation before breaking even. For investment purposes, 1 oz and larger bars are significantly more cost-efficient.

Online bullion dealers almost always offer better pricing — lower overheads mean lower premiums. The trade-off is that you cannot inspect the bar before purchasing. Buying from an LBMA-accredited dealer online with insured tracked delivery carries minimal additional risk compared to a physical shop, and you are protected by established return and authenticity policies.
