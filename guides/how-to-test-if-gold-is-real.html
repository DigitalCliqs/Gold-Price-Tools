How to Test If Gold Is Real at Home

Ten methods, ranked honestly by how reliable they actually are — from 5-second free checks to the professional standard. Plus the caveats most guides leave out, and a live tool to value your gold the moment you confirm it.

You have a gold ring that came in a box with no receipt. An old chain from a house clearance. A brooch inherited from a relative whose taste for costume jewellery was, to put it diplomatically, eclectic. Whatever the reason you are asking whether your gold is real, there is a spectrum of answers — from quick checks you can do in thirty seconds, to definitive professional methods that read exact purity to within a fraction of a percent.

This guide covers all of them, ranked honestly by how reliable they actually are, along with the important caveats most guides leave out.

No single home test is definitive. The most confident result comes from combining two or three tests and seeing whether they point the same way. Some methods are genuinely useful; others that circulate widely are frankly not worth the effort — and you will find both categories below, clearly labelled.

01Start Here: Check for a Hallmark

Before any physical or chemical test, do this first. It costs nothing, takes thirty seconds, and provides more reliable information than most of the tests further down this page.

Look for a hallmark — a small stamped number inside a ring band, on the clasp of a necklace, on the back of a pendant, or on an earring post. You may need a loupe or a phone camera on zoom to read it clearly.

The millesimal numbers to look for:

  • 375 — 9-carat gold (37.5% gold), a common European standard
  • 585 — 14-carat gold (58.5% gold), the most common standard in the US
  • 750 — 18-carat gold (75% gold)
  • 916 — 22-carat gold (common in South Asian and Middle Eastern jewellery)
  • 999 — 24-carat gold (near-pure gold)

North American pieces are often stamped with the karat directly — 10K, 14K, 18K — sometimes alongside the three-digit millesimal mark. Either is a valid hallmark.

A clear, correctly formatted hallmark from a recognised system (US karat stamp, UK assay marks, or a major international standard) is the most reliable quick confirmation you have. In the UK, all gold over one gram sold legally since 1973 must carry an official assay-office hallmark. See our Gold Hallmarks Explained guide for help decoding specific marks.

What makes you want to test further? Usually one of these: the piece arrived without provenance, the hallmark looks oddly positioned or poorly stamped, the metal reacts strangely on your skin, or you simply want independent confirmation. In those cases, the methods below are your next step.

02The 10 Methods, Ranked by Accuracy

Each method below carries an accuracy rating, the gear and time it needs, what it genuinely tells you, and where it falls short. They are presented in the order you would actually reach for them — but for a sortable side-by-side view, jump to the accuracy comparison.

1
Acid Test Kit (Touchstone Method)
Most reliable home method — recommended
High accuracy
Cost $20–$30 Time 2–3 min Gear Acid kit + stone Risk Files a tiny spot

A gold test kit contains testing acids calibrated to react with gold at different karat levels, plus a smooth black touchstone. Kits are sold by jewellery-supply shops and online retailers. Prices typically start around $20–$30 for a basic kit covering 9K, 14K, 18K, and 22K.

The chemistry is straightforward: gold resists acid dissolution — the higher the purity, the stronger the acid required. The test acids in consumer kits are typically nitric acid solutions of specific concentrations, sometimes combined with small amounts of hydrochloric acid for higher-karat tests.

Step-by-step: using an acid gold testing kit

  1. File a small area in an inconspicuous spot — the inner surface of a ring band is ideal. Filing through any plating is essential; test the surface only and you are testing the plating, not what lies beneath.
  2. Rub the filed area across the test stone with firm, even pressure to leave a clear metal streak a few millimetres long.
  3. Select your starting acid. With no information about the piece, start with the 14K acid; if you suspect higher purity, start with 18K.
  4. Apply a single drop of acid to the streak and watch for 30–60 seconds: streak stays gold = at least as pure as that acid tests; streak dissolves or changes dramatically = below that karat; green or brown foaming = likely base metal.
  5. Work up or down through the acids to find the karat at which the streak first reacts.
  6. Neutralise with bicarbonate solution and clean up promptly.

Safety: These are real chemical acids — nitric acid (HNO₃) at concentrations calibrated for each karat level, with nitric–hydrochloric mixtures (dilute aqua regia) for higher karats. Wear nitrile gloves throughout, work in a well-ventilated space, keep acids away from children, and store them in their original containers.

2
Hallmark Check
Free, instant, legally backed
High accuracy
Cost Free Time 30 sec Gear Loupe Risk None

Already covered above as Step 1 — listed again because it genuinely belongs near the top of the ranking. A clear, correctly formatted hallmark from a reputable system is the most immediate reliable evidence you have.

The hallmark alone is not infallible — sophisticated fakes can carry stamped numerals — but a genuine-looking hallmark combined with one or two consistent test results substantially increases confidence.

3
Ceramic Plate / Streak Test
Quick, no chemicals, slight scratch risk
Moderate accuracy
Cost Free Time 30 sec Gear Ceramic tile Risk Minor scratch

Drag the item across the unglazed underside of a ceramic tile or porcelain surface with moderate pressure. Real gold leaves a gold-coloured streak. Most fake or heavily plated items leave a dark grey or black streak.

The streak colour reflects the metal actually present on the surface — gold leaves a gold streak, while iron oxides common in fakes leave a darker mark. Two limitations: it scratches the surface (use a discreet area), and it cannot distinguish solid gold from thick plating. Both leave a golden streak, so it confirms gold is present on the surface but not what lies beneath.

4
Magnet Test
5 seconds, free — a good fail test only
Moderate (fail only)
Cost Free Time 5 sec Gear Neodymium magnet Risk None

Use a neodymium (rare-earth) magnet — not a fridge magnet, which is far too weak. Place the item on a flat non-metallic surface and bring the magnet slowly toward it. Strong attraction is a red flag — gold is not magnetic, and strong pull almost certainly means ferrous metals are present.

The honest limitation: passing this test proves almost nothing. Copper, brass, aluminium, stainless steel, and most base metals are also non-magnetic. A convincing gold-coloured brass fake passes the magnet test without hesitation. White-gold alloys sometimes contain nickel or cobalt, which can cause a slight magnetic response even in a genuine piece.

How accurate is the magnet test? As a fail test it is reliable — strong attraction means it is not solid gold. As a pass test — no attraction means real gold — it is essentially useless. Run it in 5 seconds because it costs nothing; a strong positive saves you the bother of everything else.

5
Visual Inspection & Skin Test
No equipment — observational only
Moderate accuracy
Cost Free Time Hours–days Gear None Risk None

Visual inspection: look closely for wear that exposes a different-coloured metal underneath — particularly on the inner surface of ring bands and the edges of pendants. A flash of yellow-green brass or dull copper-grey through worn patches is a reliable sign of plating rather than solid gold.

Skin test: wear the piece for a few hours or days and observe. Real gold — 9K and above — should not leave a green or black mark under normal circumstances. If it turns your finger green, the likely explanation is copper or brass oxidising against your skin acids and sweat — meaning either the piece is not gold, or the plating has worn through to a copper-based base metal.

Neither is scientific or fast, but skin discolouration and visible wear-through are real practical signals many people notice before they think to test formally.

6
Float Test (Water Density Test)
Simple, but with a dangerous blind spot
Limited accuracy
Cost Free Time 2 min Gear Water + scale Risk None

Gold is one of the densest naturally occurring metals (19.3 g/cm³ — roughly the same as lead). Drop real gold into water and it sinks immediately and decisively. A piece that floats or sinks slowly is suspicious.

Critical limitation — tungsten fakes: tungsten has a density remarkably close to gold (19.25 g/cm³ vs 19.3 g/cm³), which is precisely why it is used in sophisticated counterfeiting. The float test will not catch a tungsten fake, and a hollow gold piece behaves differently from a solid one. Used properly as a density calculation — weighing the item dry, then measuring displaced water volume — it is more useful, but even that cannot catch tungsten.

7
Vinegar Test
High-purity gold only — risky on lower karats
Low reliability
Cost <$1 Time 5–8 min Gear White vinegar Risk Harms low karat

Drop a few drops of white vinegar onto the piece, or submerge it for 5–8 minutes. Real gold is chemically inert and shows no colour change. A change — darkening, greening, or dulling — suggests reactive base metals are present.

The honest limitations: vinegar is mildly acidic and can affect lower-karat gold alloys even when genuine. The copper content in 14K and 9K gold can react with vinegar, dulling the surface of a real piece. That makes the test unreliable in both directions: it can miss fakes and give a false positive on real lower-karat gold.

It works best — and most safely — on near-pure gold (24K, 22K). For 9K or 14K pieces with significant alloy content, the results are ambiguous enough that the test is not worth risking the piece.

8
Lighter Test (Heat Test)
Widely cited — not recommended
Low reliability
Cost Free Time 1 min Gear Lighter Risk Can ruin pieces

The theory: real gold does not change colour or deform under a lighter flame, while fake gold may discolour or distort. The reality is more complicated.

Gold's melting point is 1,064°C. A standard lighter flame reaches roughly 400–600°C — you will never melt real gold with a lighter. The problem is you will not melt many common fake metals either. Brass melts around 900°C, copper at 1,085°C. The flame is simply not hot enough to produce meaningful differences in melting behaviour between metals.

Real danger: applying prolonged heat to genuine jewellery — particularly anything with enamel, gemstones, or delicate settings — can cause irreversible damage. Gemstones crack, enamel blisters, solder joints fail. The lighter test is not recommended for anything you would not be comfortable potentially destroying. The correct way to test gold with heat is professional fire assay — a controlled laboratory technique.

9
Toothpaste Test
Skip this one
Not recommended
Cost <$1 Time 2 min Gear Toothpaste Risk Abrasive wear

Some sources claim rubbing toothpaste on gold and rinsing leaves a black mark on fakes while genuine gold stays clean. The honest assessment: this has very limited scientific basis. Most toothpastes contain mild abrasives. Rubbing an abrasive onto a plated piece physically removes some plating — which might reveal a different colour beneath, but that is mechanical damage, not a chemical test. On solid gold it tells you nothing reliable.

Skip it. The acid kit, ceramic streak, and magnet tests above are all more useful, cheaper, and less damaging.

10
Electronic Gold Testers & XRF
Professional methods — definitive results
Professional standard
Cost $60–$50k Time Seconds Gear Tester / XRF Risk None

Electronic gold testing pens pass a small electrical current through the metal and measure conductivity. Entry-level devices start at around $60–$130. They are faster and less hazardous than acid testing and work well for standard gold jewellery, though accuracy can vary with alloys and thick plating can interfere with readings.

XRF (X-ray fluorescence) testing is the gold standard for accurate, non-destructive precious-metal analysis. An XRF machine fires high-energy X-rays at the surface; the atoms absorb and re-emit energy at characteristic wavelengths, and a detector identifies every element present. The result is a precise percentage breakdown of gold, silver, copper, and other elements — delivered in seconds, without touching or damaging the piece.

High-quality XRF analysers measure gold purity to within 0.1%. For a piece supposed to be 18K (75% gold), an XRF reading in the 73–77% range is normal and acceptable from a field instrument. XRF reads the surface only — a fraction of a millimetre deep — so it would read gold plating rather than a base-metal core on plated pieces. XRF gold testing machine price: $10,000–$50,000 new for professional handheld models — firmly professional territory, not a home tool.

Sigma Metalytics testers use electromagnetic conductivity at specific frequencies and probe up to 180 microns deep — making them better than XRF at detecting sophisticated plating or tungsten-core counterfeits. Priced typically under $4,000, they are widely used in bullion trading.

Fire assay is the definitively accurate laboratory method used by refineries. A small sample is melted in a controlled furnace with chemical agents and the resulting pure gold is weighed. It is destructive and requires specialist equipment; refineries charge roughly $100 per test. For high-value items where precise purity has financial consequences, fire assay is the appropriate choice.

06Getting Professional Testing: Where to Go

If the piece has meaningful value, or your home tests gave ambiguous results, professional testing is the right next step. Most of these options cost little or nothing.

  1. 1
    Local Jewellers

    Most independent jewellers will test a piece for free or a small fee, especially if you are considering selling or having work done. A jeweller's acid or electronic test takes about thirty seconds and is far more reliable than anything you can do at home.

  2. 2
    Coin Shops & Bullion Dealers

    Most established gold buyers and bullion dealers own electronic testers or XRF equipment and will test your piece free of charge when considering a purchase. In the US, reputable local coin shops are well equipped; in the UK, the dealers of Hatton Garden (London) and Birmingham's Jewellery Quarter are a good bet.

  3. 3
    Assay Offices (UK & Commonwealth)

    The Birmingham, Edinburgh, and London Assay Offices test pieces for the public and, if they meet the legal standard, hallmark them officially. The US has no government assay office for consumer jewellery, so Americans rely on jewellers, bullion dealers, and independent labs instead.

  4. 4
    Gold Testing Laboratories

    Specialist testing labs operate in most major cities. Search "gold testing lab near me" or "gold testing centre near me" for local options — many also offer postal testing if you are not near a facility.

07Where to Buy a Gold Test Kit

Gold test kits are widely available worldwide. Look for one that includes a proper test stone (not just strips), clearly labelled acid bottles for multiple karat levels (at minimum 9K, 14K, 18K), and instructions with reaction colour guides.

  • Online marketplaces: Amazon and eBay carry a wide range of acid test kits, from basic $20 kits to comprehensive professional sets with needles, stones, and multiple acid levels. Search "acid gold testing kit" or "gold test kit."
  • Jewellery tool suppliers: Esslinger and Rio Grande (US), and Cooksongold (UK), sell professional-grade testing equipment and are reliable sources for quality kits. The PuriTEST brand is a widely available acid-kit option.
  • Local jewellery supply shops: in major cities, jewellery-district trade shops stock testing equipment for the trade and sometimes for the public.

08A Note on Search Confusion: the Quantiferon Gold Test

A significant share of searches for "gold test" are looking for something entirely different: the Quantiferon Gold Test (also called QFT-Gold or QuantiFERON-TB Gold) — a blood test used to detect tuberculosis (TB) exposure. It is a medical diagnostic test, completely unrelated to testing gold jewellery, and involves analysing a blood sample for an immune response to TB antigens. If that is what brought you here, this is a medical matter to discuss with your doctor or healthcare provider.

09The Bottom Line: Which Method to Use

For a quick confidence check at home, combine three methods: check for a hallmark, run the magnet test, and observe the piece on your skin over a few days. These cost nothing and catch the most obvious fakes.

If you need more certainty and are comfortable handling chemicals, buy an acid gold testing kit. A $20–$30 kit from a reputable supplier gives results more reliable than any other home method, and with a little practice you can pin the karat within one step.

If the piece has significant value — anything over roughly $150–$250 — the sensible route is a professional buyer or jeweller. Most test for free, it takes minutes, and the result is definitive rather than approximate.

10Frequently Asked Questions

How do I test gold at home?

The most reliable home method is the acid test using a gold test kit — scratching the metal on a test stone and applying calibrated nitric acid solutions to determine karat. Other quick home tests include the magnet test (gold is non-magnetic), the ceramic streak test, and checking for a hallmark stamp. No single test is definitive — combining two or three gives the most confidence.

How accurate is the magnet test for gold?

As a failure indicator, accurate — if a piece is strongly attracted to a magnet, it is not solid gold. As a pass indicator, unreliable — many non-gold metals are also non-magnetic, so a piece that does not react is not necessarily gold. Use it as a 5-second first screen, not a conclusion.

Can I test gold with vinegar?

Yes, but with caution. White vinegar will not react with real gold, so colour change suggests non-gold metals are present. However, vinegar is mildly acidic and can affect lower-karat gold alloys, so this test carries a small risk of surface damage. It is most appropriate for near-pure 22K–24K gold. For 9K or 14K pieces, use an acid test kit instead.

What is the gold test acid name?

The acid used in most gold testing kits is nitric acid (HNO₃) at concentrations specific to different karat levels. Higher-karat tests use mixtures of nitric and hydrochloric acid — essentially dilute forms of aqua regia. The 10K solution is typically dilute nitric acid alone; the 22K solution uses a stronger nitric–hydrochloric mixture.

Where can I buy a gold test kit?

Gold test kits are available online from Amazon and eBay, and from specialist jewellery tool suppliers such as Esslinger and Rio Grande (US) or Cooksongold (UK). Basic kits start from around $20–$30 and cover 9K, 14K, 18K, and 22K acids with a test stone.

What is an XRF gold testing machine?

An XRF (X-ray fluorescence) machine is a professional device that fires X-rays at a metal sample and analyses the fluorescence emitted to identify and measure every element present. It gives accurate, non-destructive karat readings in seconds with no surface contact. Prices range from approximately $10,000 to $50,000 for professional models. Many gold buyers, pawnbrokers, and jewellers own XRF equipment and will test for free when considering a purchase.

How much does a gold testing machine cost?

Electronic gold testing pens and portable testers start from around $60. Mid-range models cost $130–$650. Professional XRF machines cost $10,000–$50,000. Sigma Metalytics testers (used by bullion dealers) typically cost under $4,000. Acid-based gold test kits are available from approximately $20–$130.

What does it mean if my gold turns my skin green?

Green skin discolouration usually means the gold plating has worn through to expose a copper or brass base metal. Copper reacts with skin acids and moisture to form copper compounds that cause the green colour. Solid gold at 18K and above is unlikely to cause this; lower-karat alloys (9K, 14K) with higher copper content can occasionally do so on people with acidic skin chemistry — but that doesn't make the piece fake.
