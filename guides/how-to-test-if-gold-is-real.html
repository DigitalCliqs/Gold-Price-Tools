How to Test If Gold Is Real at Home — The Complete Guide (2026)

By GoldPriceTools Editorial Team · 8 May 2026 · 20 min read

You have a gold ring that came in a box with no receipt. An old chain from a house clearance. A brooch inherited from a relative whose taste for costume jewellery was, to put it diplomatically, eclectic. Whatever the reason you are asking whether your gold is real, there is a spectrum of answers — from quick home checks you can do in thirty seconds, to definitive professional methods that tell you exact purity to within a fraction of a percent.

This guide covers all of them, ranked honestly by how reliable they actually are, along with the important caveats that most guides leave out.

No single home test is definitive. The most confident result comes from combining two or three tests and seeing whether they point in the same direction. Some methods are genuinely useful — others that circulate widely are frankly not worth the effort. You will find both categories below.

Step 1: Check for a Hallmark First

Before trying any physical or chemical test, do this first. It costs nothing, takes thirty seconds, and provides more reliable information than most of the tests further down this page.

Look for a hallmark — a small stamped number inside the ring band, on the clasp of a necklace, on the back of a pendant, or on the post of an earring. You may need a magnifying glass or a phone camera on zoom to read it clearly.

Numbers to look for:

  • 375 — 9-carat gold (37.5% gold); the most common UK standard
  • 585 — 14-carat gold (58.5% gold)
  • 750 — 18-carat gold (75% gold)
  • 916 — 22-carat gold (common in South Asian and Middle Eastern jewellery)
  • 999 — 24-carat gold (near-pure gold)

In the UK, all gold jewellery over one gram sold legally since 1973 must carry an official hallmark from one of the UK assay offices. A clear, legible hallmark from a recognised system (UK, European, or major international) is the most reliable quick confirmation you have. See our Gold Hallmarks Explained guide for help decoding specific marks.

What makes you want to test further? Usually one of these: the piece came without provenance, the hallmark seems oddly positioned or poorly stamped, the piece reacts in unusual ways on your skin, or you simply want independent confirmation. In those cases, the tests below are your next step.

The 10 Methods — Ranked by Accuracy

Each method below shows an accuracy rating, what it genuinely tells you, and where it falls short.

1
Acid Test Kit (Touchstone Method)
Most reliable home method — recommended
High accuracy

A gold test kit contains a set of testing acids calibrated to react with gold at different karat levels, along with a test stone (a smooth black touchstone). These kits are available from jewellery supply shops and online retailers. Prices in the UK typically start around £15–£20 for a basic kit covering 9K, 14K, 18K, and 22K.

The chemistry is straightforward: gold resists acid dissolution — the higher the purity, the stronger the acid required. The test acids in consumer kits are typically nitric acid solutions of specific concentrations, sometimes combined with small amounts of hydrochloric acid for higher-karat tests.

Step-by-step: using an acid gold testing kit

  1. File a small area of your piece in an inconspicuous spot — the inner surface of a ring band is ideal. Filing through any plating is essential; if you only test the surface, you are testing the plating, not what lies beneath.
  2. Rub the filed area across the test stone with firm, even pressure to leave a clear visible streak of metal a few millimetres long.
  3. Select your starting acid. If you have no information about the piece, start with the 14K acid. If you suspect higher purity, start with 18K.
  4. Apply a single drop of acid to the streak and observe for 30–60 seconds: streak remains gold-coloured = at least as pure as that acid tests; streak dissolves or changes dramatically = below that karat; green or brown foaming = likely base metal.
  5. Work up or down through the acids to find the karat at which the streak first shows a reaction.
  6. Neutralise with bicarbonate solution and clean up promptly.

Safety: These acids are real chemical substances — nitric acid (HNO₃) at concentrations calibrated for each karat level. Higher-karat tests use nitric-hydrochloric mixtures (dilute aqua regia). Wear nitrile gloves throughout. Work in a well-ventilated space. Keep acids away from children and store them in their original containers.

2
Hallmark Check
Free, instant, legally backed
High accuracy

Already covered above as Step 1 — it is listed here again because it genuinely belongs near the top of the accuracy ranking. A clear, correctly formatted hallmark from a reputable assay system is the most immediate reliable evidence you have. The UK hallmarking system has been legally enforced since 1300 in various forms and since 1973 in its current structure.

The hallmark alone is not infallible — sophisticated fakes can carry stamped numerals — but a genuine-looking hallmark combined with other consistent test results substantially increases confidence.

3
Ceramic Plate / Streak Test
Quick, no chemicals, some risk of surface scratch
Moderate accuracy

Drag the gold item across the unglazed underside of a ceramic tile or porcelain surface with moderate pressure. Real gold leaves a gold-coloured streak. Most fake gold or heavily plated items leave a dark grey or black streak.

This works because the streak colour reflects the actual metal present on the surface. Gold leaves a gold streak; iron oxides common in fake metal leave a darker mark. The limitation is that this test scratches the surface of your piece — use a discrete area — and it does not distinguish between solid gold and thick gold plating. Both will leave a golden streak, so it confirms gold is present on the surface but not what lies beneath.

4
Magnet Test
5 seconds, costs nothing — good fail test only
Moderate (fail only)

Use a neodymium (rare earth) magnet — not a fridge magnet, which is far too weak. Place the item on a flat non-metallic surface and bring the magnet slowly toward it. Strong attraction is a red flag — gold is not magnetic, and strong attraction almost certainly means ferrous metals are present.

The honest limitation: Passing this test proves almost nothing. Copper, brass, aluminium, stainless steel, and most base metals are also non-magnetic. A convincing gold-coloured fake made from brass would pass the magnet test without hesitation. White gold alloys sometimes contain nickel or cobalt, which can cause a slight magnetic response even in a genuine gold piece.

How accurate is the magnet test for gold? As a fail test, it is reliable — strong magnetic attraction means it is not solid gold. As a pass test — no attraction means genuine gold — it is essentially useless. Run it in 5 seconds because it costs nothing; a strong positive result saves you the bother of everything else.

5
Visual Inspection & Skin Test
No equipment — observational only
Moderate accuracy

Visual inspection: Look closely at the piece for signs of wear that expose a different-coloured metal underneath — particularly on the inner surface of ring bands and the edges of pendants. A flash of yellow-green brass or dull copper-grey visible through worn patches is a reliable indicator of plating rather than solid gold.

Skin test: Wear the piece for a few hours or days and observe. Real gold — at 9K and above — should not leave a green or black mark on your skin under normal circumstances. If the piece turns your finger green, the most likely explanation is copper or brass oxidising on contact with your skin acids and sweat. This suggests either the piece is not gold at all, or the gold plating has worn through to a copper-based base metal.

Neither of these is scientific or fast, but skin discolouration and visible wear-through are real practical signals many people notice before they think to test formally.

6
Float Test (Water Density Test)
Simple but has a dangerous blind spot
Limited accuracy

Gold is one of the densest naturally occurring metals (19.3 g/cm³ — roughly the same as lead). Drop real gold into water and it sinks immediately and decisively. A piece that floats or sinks slowly is suspicious.

Critical limitation — tungsten fakes: Tungsten has a density remarkably close to gold (19.25 g/cm³ vs 19.3 g/cm³), which is precisely why it is used in sophisticated gold counterfeiting. The float test will not catch a tungsten fake. A hollow gold piece will also behave differently from a solid one. The float test is better used as a density calculation — weighing the item dry, then measuring displaced water volume — but even this cannot catch tungsten counterfeits.

7
Vinegar Test
Works on high-purity gold only — risky on lower karats
Low reliability

Drop a few drops of white vinegar onto the piece, or submerge it for 5–8 minutes. Real gold is chemically inert and will show no colour change. A colour change — darkening, greening, or dulling — suggests reactive base metals are present.

The honest limitations: Vinegar is mildly acidic and can affect lower-karat gold alloys even when the piece is genuine. The copper content in 14K and 9K gold can react with vinegar, causing surface dulling even in a real piece. This makes the test unreliable in both directions: it might miss fakes and give a false positive on real lower-karat gold.

The vinegar test works best — and safest — on near-pure gold (24K, 22K). For 9K or 14K pieces with significant alloy content, the results are ambiguous enough that this test is not worth risking the piece.

8
Lighter Test (Heat Test)
Widely cited — not recommended
Low reliability

The theory: real gold does not change colour or deform under a lighter flame, while fake gold may show discolouration or distortion. The reality is more complicated.

Gold's melting point is 1,064°C. A standard lighter flame reaches approximately 400–600°C. You will never melt real gold with a lighter. But here is the problem: you will not melt many common fake metals with one either. Brass melts at around 900°C. Copper at 1,085°C. The lighter flame is simply not hot enough to produce meaningful differences in melting behaviour between many metals.

Real danger: Applying prolonged heat to a genuine piece of jewellery — particularly anything with enamel, gemstones, or delicate settings — can cause irreversible damage. Gemstones can crack. Enamel can blister. Solder joints can fail. The lighter test is not recommended for anything you would not be comfortable potentially destroying. If you want to test gold with heat in a meaningful way, the correct method is professional fire assay — a controlled laboratory technique.

9
Toothpaste Test
Skip this one
Not recommended

Some sources claim rubbing toothpaste on gold and rinsing will leave a black mark on fake gold while genuine gold stays clean. The honest assessment: this test has very limited scientific basis. Most modern toothpastes contain mild abrasives. Rubbing an abrasive onto a gold plated piece physically removes some plating — which might reveal a different colour beneath, but this is mechanical damage, not a chemical test. On solid gold, it tells you nothing reliable.

Skip this one. The acid kit, ceramic streak, and magnet tests above are all more useful, cheaper, and less damaging.

10
Electronic Gold Testers & XRF
Professional methods — definitive results
Professional standard

Electronic gold testing pens work by passing a small electrical current through the metal and measuring conductivity. Entry-level devices start at around £49–£100. They are faster and less hazardous than acid testing and work well for standard gold jewellery, though accuracy can vary with alloys and thick plating can interfere with readings.

XRF (X-ray fluorescence) testing is the gold standard for accurate, non-destructive precious metal analysis. An XRF machine fires a beam of high-energy X-rays at the metal surface. The atoms absorb the energy and re-emit it at characteristic wavelengths — a detector captures these and identifies every element present. The result: a precise percentage breakdown of gold, silver, copper, and other elements, delivered in seconds, without touching or damaging the piece.

High-quality XRF analysers measure gold purity to within 0.1%. For a piece supposed to be 18K (75% gold), an XRF reading in the 73–77% range is a normal and acceptable result from a field instrument. XRF reads the surface only — typically a fraction of a millimetre deep — so it would correctly read gold plating rather than a base metal core on plated pieces. XRF gold testing machine price: $10,000–$50,000 new for professional handheld models — firmly professional territory, not a home tool.

Sigma Metalytics testers use electromagnetic conductivity at specific frequencies and probe up to 180 microns deep — making them more effective than XRF at detecting sophisticated plating or tungsten-core counterfeits. Priced typically under $4,000, they are widely used in bullion trading.

Fire assay is the definitively accurate laboratory method used by refineries. It involves taking a small sample, melting it in a controlled furnace with chemical agents, and weighing the resulting pure gold. It is destructive and requires specialist equipment; refineries charge approximately £80 per test. For high-value items where precise purity determination has financial consequences, fire assay is the appropriate choice.

Getting Professional Testing: Where to Go

If the piece you want to test has meaningful value, or if your home tests have given ambiguous results, professional testing is the right next step.

  1. 1
    UK Assay Offices

    The Birmingham Assay Office, Edinburgh Assay Office, and London Assay Office all offer testing services for the public. They will test your piece and, if it meets the legal standard, hallmark it officially. Testing fees vary; hallmarking is charged per item.

  2. 2
    Reputable Gold Buyers and Bullion Dealers

    Most established gold buyers own electronic gold testers or XRF equipment and will test your piece free of charge when considering a purchase. Specialist bullion dealers and jewellers in Hatton Garden (London) or Birmingham's Jewellery Quarter are well-equipped for this.

  3. 3
    Local Jewellers

    Many independent jewellers will test a piece for free or for a small fee, particularly if you are considering selling or having work done. A jeweller's acid test or electronic tester takes about thirty seconds and is far more reliable than anything you can do at home.

  4. 4
    Gold Testing Laboratories

    Specialist testing labs exist in most major cities. Search "gold test laboratory near me" or "gold testing centre near me" for local options. Some also offer postal testing if you are not near a facility.

Where to Buy a Gold Test Kit

Gold test kits are widely available in the UK and internationally. When choosing a kit, look for one that includes: a proper test stone (not just strips), clearly labelled acid bottles for multiple karat levels (at minimum 9K, 14K, 18K), and instructions with reaction colour guides.

  • Online retailers: Amazon, eBay, and specialist suppliers carry a range of acid test kits, from basic £15 kits to comprehensive professional sets with needles, stones, and multiple acid levels. Search "acid gold testing kit" or "gold test kit."
  • Jewellery tool suppliers: Cookson Gold (cooksongold.com), Quicktest (quicktest.co.uk), and DynaGem sell professional-grade testing equipment and are reliable sources for quality kits.
  • High street jewellery supply shops: In major cities, jewellery quarter shops stock testing equipment for trade buyers and sometimes for members of the public.

A Note on Search Confusion: The Quantiferon Gold Test

A significant number of searches for "gold test" are looking for something completely different: the Quantiferon Gold Test (also called the QFT-Gold test or QuantiFERON-TB Gold test) — a blood test used to diagnose tuberculosis (TB) exposure. The Quantiferon TB Gold test is a medical diagnostic test, entirely unrelated to testing gold jewellery. It involves a blood sample being tested for immune response to tuberculosis antigens. If you are looking for information about the Quantiferon Gold test for TB, this is a medical matter that should be discussed with your GP or healthcare provider.

The Bottom Line: Which Method to Use

For a quick confidence check at home, combine three methods: check for a hallmark, run the magnet test, and observe the piece on your skin over a few days. These cost nothing and catch the most obvious fakes.

If you need more certainty and are comfortable handling chemicals: buy an acid gold testing kit. A £15–£20 kit from a reputable supplier will give you results more reliable than any other home method, and with some practice you will be able to determine karat within one step.

If the piece has significant value — anything over £100–£200 — the most sensible route is to take it to a professional buyer or jeweller for testing. Most will test for free, it takes minutes, and the result is definitive rather than approximate.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I test gold at home?

The most reliable home method is the acid test using a gold test kit, which involves scratching the metal on a test stone and applying calibrated nitric acid solutions to determine karat. Other quick home tests include the magnet test (gold is non-magnetic), the ceramic streak test, and checking for a hallmark stamp. No single test is definitive — combining two or three gives the most confidence.

How accurate is the magnet test for gold?

As a failure indicator, accurate — if a piece is strongly attracted to a magnet, it is not solid gold. As a pass indicator, unreliable — many non-gold metals are also non-magnetic, so a piece that does not react to a magnet is not necessarily gold. Use it as a 5-second first screen, not a conclusion.

Can I test gold with vinegar?

Yes, but with caution. White vinegar will not react with real gold, so colour change suggests non-gold metals are present. However, vinegar is mildly acidic and can affect lower-karat gold alloys, so this test carries a small risk of surface damage. It is most appropriate for near-pure 22K–24K gold. For 9K or 14K pieces, use an acid test kit instead.

What is the gold test acid name?

The acid used in most gold testing kits is nitric acid (HNO₃) at concentrations specific to different karat levels. Higher-karat tests use mixtures of nitric and hydrochloric acid — essentially dilute forms of aqua regia. The 10K solution is typically dilute nitric acid alone; the 22K solution uses a stronger nitric-hydrochloric mixture.

Where can I buy a gold test kit?

Gold test kits are available online from Amazon and eBay, and from specialist jewellery tool suppliers such as Cookson Gold, Quicktest, and DynaGem. Basic kits start from around £15–£20 and cover 9K, 14K, 18K, and 22K acids with a test stone.

What is an XRF gold testing machine?

An XRF (X-ray fluorescence) machine is a professional testing device that fires X-rays at a metal sample and analyses the fluorescence emitted to identify and measure every element present. It gives accurate, non-destructive karat readings in seconds with no surface contact. Prices range from approximately $10,000 to $50,000 for professional models. Many gold buyers, pawnbrokers, and jewellers own XRF equipment and will test for free when considering a purchase.

How much does a gold testing machine cost?

Electronic gold testing pens and portable electronic testers start from around £49. Mid-range models cost £100–£500. Professional XRF machines cost $10,000–$50,000. Sigma Metalytics testers (used by bullion dealers) typically cost under $4,000. Gold test kits (acid-based) are available from approximately £15–£100.

What does it mean if my gold turns my skin green?

Green skin discolouration from gold jewellery usually indicates the gold plating has worn through to expose a copper or brass base metal. Copper reacts with skin acids and moisture to form copper compounds that cause the green colour. Solid gold at 18K and above is unlikely to cause this; lower-karat alloys (9K, 14K) with higher copper content can occasionally do so on people with acidic skin chemistry — but this doesn't mean the piece is fake. See our Gold Filled vs Gold Plated guide for more on plating wear.
