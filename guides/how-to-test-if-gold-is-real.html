How to Test If Gold Is Real At Home

By GoldPriceTools Editorial Team · Published 01 May 2026 · Updated 01 May 2026

Gold has always been a symbol of wealth and prestige. Unfortunately, its high value also makes it a prime target for counterfeiters and fraudsters. Whether you've inherited a piece of family jewelry, found a bargain at a flea market, or purchased a bullion coin from a private seller, knowing how to verify the authenticity of your gold is crucial. In this comprehensive guide, we will explore various methods for how to test if gold is real, ranging from simple at-home checks to professional analyses.

1. The Visual Inspection (Hallmark Test)

The first and easiest step in testing gold is a thorough visual inspection. Specifically, you should look for hallmarks, which are official stamps indicating the purity of the gold.

Most commercially produced gold jewelry and bullion are stamped with a hallmark. In the United States, you might see karat marks like 10K, 14K, 18K, 22K, or 24K. In Europe and many other parts of the world, fineness is expressed as a three-digit number representing the parts per thousand of pure gold. Common fineness stamps include 375 (9K), 585 (14K), 750 (18K), 916 (22K), and 999 (24K). For more details, see our guide on gold hallmarks.

While a hallmark is a good initial indicator, it is not foolproof. Counterfeiters can easily stamp fake gold with legitimate-looking hallmarks. Therefore, if a piece has a hallmark, it's a positive sign, but it should be corroborated with further testing. Conversely, older, antique jewelry or custom-made pieces may lack hallmarks altogether, even if they are solid gold.

During the visual inspection, also look for signs of discoloration or wear. Solid gold does not tarnish easily. If you notice discoloration—such as green, black, or reddish spots—or if you can see a different metal showing through areas of heavy wear, the item is likely gold-plated rather than solid gold.

2. The Magnet Test

The magnet test is a quick, easy, and non-destructive way to weed out many fake gold items. However, it requires a strong neodymium magnet; an ordinary refrigerator magnet will not suffice.

Gold is not magnetic. If your item is strongly attracted to the magnet, it is absolutely not solid gold. It is likely made of iron, nickel, steel, or another magnetic base metal that has been thinly plated with gold.

There are important caveats to the magnet test. First, many non-magnetic metals are also used in counterfeiting, such as brass, copper, lead, and aluminum. Therefore, a piece that does not react to a magnet is not guaranteed to be real gold; it just hasn't failed the test. Second, genuine gold jewelry often uses magnetic metals for clasps, springs, and hinges to provide strength. If the clasp is magnetic but the chain is not, the chain may still be genuine gold.

3. The Float and Density Tests

Gold is an incredibly dense metal. It is significantly heavier than most other metals of the same volume. This characteristic can be exploited to test authenticity.

The Float Test

The float test is incredibly simple. Drop your gold item into a cup or bowl of water. Solid gold is dense and heavy; it will sink straight to the bottom quickly. Many fake gold items, particularly those made of lighter metals or hollowed out, may float or sink very slowly. Furthermore, genuine gold will not rust or tarnish after exposure to water. If discoloration occurs after the item gets wet, it is not real gold.

The Density (Specific Gravity) Test

For a more scientific approach, you can calculate the specific gravity (density) of the item. Pure gold has a specific gravity of 19.32 g/cm³. Different karats have different densities (e.g., 14K gold is roughly 12.9 to 14.6 g/cm³ depending on the alloy). You can find more details in our home weighing guide.

To perform the density test, you need a highly precise digital scale and a graduated cylinder filled with water.

  1. Weigh the gold item on the scale and record the weight in grams.
  2. Fill the graduated cylinder with water and record the exact volume.
  3. Submerge the gold item in the water and record the new water level.
  4. Subtract the initial volume from the final volume to determine the displacement (the volume of the item).
  5. Divide the weight (in grams) by the volume (in cubic centimeters or milliliters).

Compare the resulting number to known gold densities. While highly effective, this test can be inaccurate if the item contains hollow spaces or significant non-gold components like large gemstones.

4. The Ceramic Scratch Test

The ceramic scratch test is a classic method, but it comes with a warning: it can scratch and damage your item. Therefore, it is best used on scrap gold or discreet areas of jewelry, rather than pristine bullion or highly valuable pieces.

You need an unglazed ceramic plate. Drag the gold item across the surface of the unglazed ceramic. If the item leaves a black or grey streak, it is fake gold. If it leaves a golden-yellow streak, it is likely genuine gold.

5. The Acid Test (Nitric Acid)

The acid test is one of the most reliable at-home testing methods, but it involves handling dangerous chemicals (nitric and hydrochloric acids). Extreme caution, protective eyewear, and gloves are mandatory. Acid test kits are widely available online and usually contain a testing stone (touchstone) and several bottles of acid labeled for different karats (10K, 14K, 18K, 22K).

To perform the test:

  1. Rub the gold item firmly against the testing stone to leave a thick, visible streak. Make sure you rub deep enough to bypass any potential gold plating.
  2. Apply a drop of the lowest karat acid (e.g., 10K) to the streak.
  3. Observe the reaction. If the streak dissolves completely, the item is not gold. If it remains intact, the gold is at least 10K.
  4. Proceed to the next highest acid (e.g., 14K) and apply it to a new section of the streak. Continue this process until the streak begins to fade or dissolve. The purity of the gold is the level just below the acid that caused it to dissolve.

For example, if the streak survives the 14K acid but dissolves under the 18K acid, the item is 14K gold.

6. Professional Testing Methods

If you are testing highly valuable bullion, coins, or antique jewelry, at-home tests may be too risky or insufficiently accurate. In these cases, professional testing is highly recommended.

Electronic Gold Testers

Jewelers and pawn shops frequently use electronic gold testers. These devices use electrical conductivity or electromagnetic waves to determine the purity of the gold. They are fast, highly accurate, and completely non-destructive, making them ideal for testing coins and high-end jewelry.

X-Ray Fluorescence (XRF) Spectrometers

The gold standard for non-destructive testing is the X-Ray Fluorescence (XRF) spectrometer. These highly expensive machines bombard the item with X-rays and measure the fluorescent X-rays emitted in response. The XRF spectrometer provides an exact elemental breakdown of the item, revealing precisely how much gold, silver, copper, zinc, or other metals are present. XRF analysis is incredibly accurate and is widely used by assay offices, refineries, and major bullion dealers.

Fire Assay

Fire assay is the most accurate method for determining gold purity, but it is destructive. The gold is melted down and chemically separated from impurities. Because it destroys the original item, fire assay is typically reserved for large-scale refining operations and mining samples, rather than consumer testing.

Conclusion

Knowing how to test if gold is real requires a combination of common sense, simple tools, and, occasionally, professional assistance. By utilizing visual inspections, magnet tests, density calculations, and acid testing, you can confidently identify most fake gold items and verify the authenticity of your precious metals.

Remember that counterfeiters are constantly improving their techniques. When dealing with significant investments in gold bullion or high-value jewelry, relying on a combination of tests or seeking the expertise of a reputable professional with access to electronic testers or XRF technology is always the safest approach.
