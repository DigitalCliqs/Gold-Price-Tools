How Much Is a Gold Ring Worth?

By GoldPriceTools Editorial Team · Published 01 May 2026 · Updated 01 May 2026

Whether you've inherited a piece of jewelry, found an old wedding band, or are considering selling pieces you no longer wear, you're likely asking: "How much is a gold ring worth?" The answer depends on whether you are looking at its retail value, its appraisal value for insurance, or its intrinsic "melt value." For most second-hand jewelry sold for cash, the melt value is the most important figure.

In this guide, we will walk you through exactly how to determine the intrinsic melt value of your gold ring using three key pieces of information: karat purity, weight, and the current gold spot price.

1. Determine the Karat Purity

The first step is to figure out how pure the gold is. Pure gold (24 karat) is too soft to be made into durable jewelry, so it is alloyed (mixed) with other metals like copper, silver, or zinc to make it harder. The karat (K) indicates the ratio of pure gold to alloy metals.

Look inside the band of the ring for a hallmark. You will typically see a stamp indicating the purity. Common stamps include:

  • 9K or 375: Contains 37.5% pure gold (Common in the UK)
  • 10K or 417: Contains 41.7% pure gold (Minimum standard in the US)
  • 14K or 585: Contains 58.3% pure gold (Most common US standard)
  • 18K or 750: Contains 75.0% pure gold (High-end jewelry standard)
  • 22K or 916: Contains 91.6% pure gold (Common in Asian and Middle Eastern jewelry)

If your ring has no hallmark, or if it is heavily worn, you may need to have it tested by a jeweler using the methods described in our gold testing guide to confirm its exact purity.

Note: If you see stamps like "14K GF" (Gold Filled), "GP" (Gold Plated), or "EP" (Electroplated), the ring is not solid gold and has negligible scrap value.

2. Weigh the Ring

Next, you need to know how much the ring weighs. The most accurate way to do this at home is with a digital kitchen scale or a pocket jeweler's scale. The weight must be measured in grams (g).

Most standard men's and women's solid gold rings weigh between 2 grams (very thin bands) and 8 grams (thick, heavy bands), though signet rings or large statement rings can weigh significantly more.

Crucial Exception for Gemstones: The melt value calculation only applies to the actual gold metal. If your ring contains diamonds, sapphires, or other gemstones, their weight must be subtracted from the total weight of the ring. A jeweler can help estimate the weight of the stones or remove them entirely before weighing the gold.

3. Check the Live Spot Price

Gold is a global commodity, and its price fluctuates every minute the markets are open. This base price for pure 24K gold is known as the "spot price." The spot price is usually quoted in US Dollars per Troy Ounce (1 Troy Ounce = 31.1035 grams).

You can check the current live spot price on our gold price page.

4. Calculate the Melt Value

Once you have the karat, weight in grams, and the current spot price per gram for pure gold, you can calculate the intrinsic melt value of your ring.

The formula is:

(Weight in Grams) × (Purity Percentage) × (Spot Price per Gram of 24K Gold) = Melt Value

Example Calculation

Let's say you have a 14K gold ring that weighs 5 grams, and the current spot price for pure 24K gold is $80 per gram.

  1. Find the purity percentage: 14K gold is 58.3% pure (or 0.583).
  2. Calculate the pure gold weight: 5 grams × 0.583 = 2.915 grams of pure gold.
  3. Calculate the value: 2.915 grams × $80/gram = $233.20.

The intrinsic melt value of the ring is $233.20. You can skip the manual math by using our automated scrap gold calculator.

Melt Value vs. Resale Value

It is important to understand that the melt value is the absolute baseline value of the gold itself. It is what a refinery would pay for the raw metal.

If you sell to a Cash-for-Gold buyer or Pawn Shop: They will rarely pay you 100% of the melt value. They need to make a profit and cover refining costs. Expect to be offered anywhere from 60% to 85% of the melt value.

If the ring is vintage, antique, or from a designer brand (like Cartier or Tiffany & Co.): The ring's retail or resale value as a piece of jewelry may be significantly higher than its melt value. In this case, you should not sell it for scrap. Instead, consider selling it to an antique dealer, through a specialized auction, or on platforms like eBay to a private buyer who wants to wear the ring.

Conclusion

Determining how much a gold ring is worth requires knowing its karat purity and weight, and applying those to the live gold spot price. While calculating the melt value gives you a solid baseline, always consider the piece's history, craftsmanship, and potential designer premium before deciding to sell it for scrap.
