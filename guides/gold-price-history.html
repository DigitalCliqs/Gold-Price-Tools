Gold Price History — All-Time Highs and Key Milestones

From a fixed rate of $20.67 per troy ounce that held for decades, gold has journeyed through monetary upheaval, runaway inflation, global crises, and bull markets so powerful they seemed unthinkable just years earlier. In January 2026, gold struck an intraday record of $5,589.38 per troy ounce — exceeding the inflation-adjusted 1980 peak for the first time in over four decades.

01
Pre-1971 · The Fixed Era

The Gold Standard Era: Price Fixed Before the Modern Market

For most of the 19th and early 20th centuries, the price of gold was not a market price at all — it was a government decree. The gold standard meant major currencies were directly convertible to gold at fixed rates, making the gold price effectively static for long stretches of time.

The most significant fixed price in US history was set in 1934 by President Franklin D. Roosevelt, who signed the Gold Reserve Act and raised the official gold price from $20.67 to $35.00 per troy ounce — a 69% devaluation of the dollar against gold. This $35 price would hold for the next 37 years.

The post-World War II Bretton Woods Agreement of 1944 formalised this arrangement on an international scale: major world currencies were pegged to the US dollar, which was in turn convertible to gold at exactly $35 per ounce. Central banks around the world held dollars as reserves, trusting they could always exchange them for gold at that price. The system worked while the United States ran trade surpluses and held sufficient gold reserves — but by the late 1960s, the pressures were becoming impossible to sustain.

02
August 15, 1971 · The Founding Event

The Nixon Shock — Birth of the Modern Gold Market

On the evening of August 15, 1971, President Richard Nixon appeared on television and announced what became known as the Nixon Shock: the United States would suspend the convertibility of the US dollar into gold, effective immediately. Foreign governments could no longer send dollars to Washington and expect gold in return. The Bretton Woods system — the international monetary architecture that had governed the global economy for 27 years — was over.

The implications were immediate and far-reaching. For the first time in modern history, gold was free to find its price in open markets. The result was a price explosion. Within months, gold surged past the old $35 fixed rate into the high $40s, then approached $70 by mid-1972 — effectively doubling within a year of being freed from its peg. Investors and governments around the world had known for years that $35 an ounce was artificial. The moment the constraint was removed, the market rapidly closed the gap between the fixed price and gold's perceived real value.

Key Takeaway

Every modern analysis of gold price history effectively starts here. The pre-1971 “price history” is largely an administrative record, not a market price. The Nixon Shock is the founding event of the gold market as it exists today.

03
1971–1979 · +2,300% Bull Run

The 1970s: Inflation, Oil, and Gold's First Great Bull Market

The decade following the Nixon Shock was gold's first experience of open-market price discovery — and the result was extraordinary. Gold gained roughly 2,300% from 1971 to its peak in early 1980 as a confluence of forces drove prices higher with increasing urgency.

1973–1974: The first oil crisis

The Arab oil embargo of 1973 quadrupled oil prices and triggered the first global inflationary shock of the modern era. Investors recognised that the dollar's purchasing power was eroding at an accelerating rate, and gold moved above $100 per ounce for the first time in January 1974 — a level it has never fallen below since. By December 1974, gold had set a then-record of $193 per ounce.

1976–1977: Temporary pullback

After the initial surge, gold briefly corrected to a low of $112.50 in August 1976 as markets stabilised and confidence in the dollar briefly recovered. This proved to be the last significant buying opportunity before the decade's climactic rally.

1978–1979: The second oil crisis and inflation panic

The Iranian Revolution of 1979 triggered the second oil shock, removing Iran's substantial oil production from global markets and sending energy prices soaring again. US consumer price inflation exceeded 13% in 1979 while real interest rates turned sharply negative. Investors who held cash or bonds were watching their purchasing power erode in real time. Gold responded with unprecedented force:

  • Start of 1979$233 / oz
  • July 1979$300 / oz
  • October 1979$400 / oz
  • November 1979$500 / oz
  • New Year's Day 1980$600 / oz

The pace was extraordinary — gold added $367 in the final six months of 1979 alone. The Soviet invasion of Afghanistan in December 1979 added a geopolitical premium on top of the inflation panic, and the two forces together created the environment for gold's most dramatic single month in history.

04
January 21, 1980 · The First Great Peak

January 1980: The First Great Peak — $850 per Ounce

Intraday All-Time High
$850

21 January 1980 · The inflation-adjusted record that would stand for 45 years. Equivalent to approximately $3,500 in 2025 dollars.

On January 21, 1980, gold reached what would remain the highest real (inflation-adjusted) price ever recorded for the next 45 years: $850 per troy ounce intraday. The official London PM Fix recorded a peak close of approximately $665–$850 depending on the specific date reference, with some sources citing the intraday spike as the defining mark.

To put that 1980 peak in context: adjusting for inflation, $850 in January 1980 was equivalent to approximately $3,500 in 2025 dollars. This inflation-adjusted record would not be broken until April 2025 — a 45-year wait. The nominal record of $850 itself would stand until February 2008, when gold finally reclaimed that level after a 28-year absence.

The simultaneous collapse of the gold-silver ratio to approximately 17:1 during this period reflects the extraordinary intensity of the panic. Silver hit $49.45 per ounce on January 17, 1980, partly driven by the Hunt Brothers' famous attempt to corner the silver market, which also contributed to gold's atmospheric prices at the time.

05
1980–1999 · The Long Decline

The Forgotten Two Decades: Gold's Deepest Bear Market

What followed the 1980 peak was one of the most sustained bear markets in gold's modern history — 20 years during which the metal ground lower in both nominal and real terms while equity markets delivered their greatest bull run of the century.

The Volcker shock

The reversal came through decisive, painful monetary policy. Federal Reserve Chairman Paul Volcker raised the federal funds rate above 19% in 1981 to break the back of inflation. This policy was brutal for the economy in the short term — it triggered a deep recession — but it worked. Inflation fell sharply, the dollar strengthened, and the rationale for holding gold as an inflation hedge evaporated almost overnight.

Gold dropped from its $850 peak to around $300 by the mid-1980s — a 65% decline — in what remains the deepest crash in gold's modern history. It then spent most of the decade oscillating between $300 and $500 while stocks surged.

1990s: Goldilocks era for everything except gold

The 1990s brought a combination of low inflation, strong economic growth, rising equity valuations (the dot-com boom), and a strong dollar — the perfect environment for everything except gold. By 1999, gold hit a 20-year low of approximately $252 per ounce, reflecting widespread institutional belief that inflation had been permanently tamed and that gold's role as a monetary hedge was an artefact of a bygone era.

The 1990s bear market was also deepened by central bank gold sales. The UK's infamous sale of 395 tonnes of its gold reserves between 1999 and 2002 — at prices near the market bottom, averaging approximately $275 per ounce — became widely known as one of the most costly policy decisions in British monetary history.

The Lesson of 1980–1999

Long gold bear markets are real, can last decades, and typically follow periods of very high real interest rates combined with strong equity market performance. Investors who bought gold at the 1980 peak did not break even in nominal terms for almost 30 years.

06
2000–2011 · +660% Bull Market

Gold's Second Great Bull Market — $252 to $1,921

Gold's recovery from its 1999 lows was gradual at first, then accelerating. Several structural shifts drove the new bull market:

2001–2004: Dollar weakness and geopolitical risk

The bursting of the dot-com bubble in 2000 damaged confidence in equities. The September 11, 2001 attacks added geopolitical uncertainty. The dollar began a prolonged weakening trend as the US ran large trade deficits. Gold responded, rising steadily from $252 to cross $400 per ounce for the first time since the early 1980s in late 2003.

2005–2007: The commodities supercycle

Rising demand from China, India, and other emerging markets drove a broad commodities boom. Gold followed, pushing through $500 in late 2005, then $600 in 2006, then $700 in 2007, and finally reclaiming the old 1980 nominal high of $850 in early 2008 for the first time in 28 years.

2008–2009: The global financial crisis

The collapse of Lehman Brothers in September 2008 triggered the worst financial crisis since the Great Depression. In the immediate panic, gold was initially sold alongside equities as investors desperately sought cash — a short-term reaction that temporarily pushed gold down to $765 in November 2008. But as central banks responded with unprecedented monetary stimulus, gold reasserted its role as a safe haven. The Federal Reserve cut rates to near zero and launched the first quantitative easing programme. Gold crossed $1,000 per ounce for the first time in history on February 20, 2009.

2010–2011: QE, sovereign debt crisis, and peak

As the recovery struggled and the European sovereign debt crisis raised questions about the survival of the eurozone, gold continued to rise. From $1,000 in early 2009, gold hit $1,500 in April 2011, then $1,900 in August 2011 before reaching its 2011 peak of approximately $1,921 per troy ounce in September 2011. The 2000–2011 bull market produced a total gain of approximately 660% from the 1999 low to the 2011 peak.

07
2011–2015 · −46% Correction

The Second Major Correction

The 2011 peak marked the beginning of a four-year bear market in gold. As the global economy stabilised, the European debt crisis receded, and the Federal Reserve began signalling the eventual end of its emergency monetary policy, the rationale for holding gold at above $1,900 faded.

Gold fell from $1,921 to approximately $1,200 by mid-2013 — a decline of 38% — and continued grinding lower through 2015 to reach a trough near $1,050 in December 2015. The total decline from the 2011 peak to the 2015 low was approximately 46% — substantial, though far less severe than the 65% crash of 1980–1985.

The drivers were a mirror image of the bull market conditions: normalising inflation expectations, a stronger dollar, rising real interest rates as the Fed discussed tapering its bond purchases, and improving equity markets that drew investors away from defensive assets.

08
2016–2019 · Recovery & Consolidation

Quiet Years That Set the Stage

Gold began its recovery in January 2016, rising from its $1,050 trough as the Fed's rate-hiking cycle proved more modest than feared and global growth showed signs of faltering. From 2016 to 2019, gold traded in a broadly consolidating pattern between $1,200 and $1,350, punctuated by periods of strength:

09
August 2020 · First $2,000 Breakthrough

COVID-19 and the $2,000 Breakthrough

The COVID-19 pandemic of 2020 produced exactly the conditions that drive gold higher: extraordinary monetary stimulus, negative real interest rates, currency debasement concerns, and broad economic uncertainty. The Federal Reserve cut rates to near zero and launched unlimited quantitative easing. Governments worldwide announced multi-trillion-dollar fiscal stimulus programmes. Financial investors added a record 734 tonnes worth $39.5 billion to gold holdings in the first half of 2020 alone.

COVID Pandemic Peak
$2,075

Intraday, 7 August 2020 · The first cross of $2,000 in history. Gold gained 32% in the first eight months of 2020 — one of the strongest annual performances in the modern era.

Gold crossed $2,000 per ounce for the first time in history on August 4, 2020, and hit an intraday all-time high of $2,075.47 on August 7, 2020. The 2020 high would hold as the nominal record for the next three and a half years, until gold's 2024 breakout launched the current era of new highs.

10
2024–2026 · The $3,000 · $4,000 · $5,000 Era

New Records, New Territory: The Most Sustained Bull Market in History

The period from late 2023 through early 2026 has been the most sustained, record-breaking bull market in gold's history — surpassing even the 1970s run in terms of consistency and pace.

2024: The breakout year

Gold entered 2024 at approximately $2,000 and proceeded to break the 2020 record multiple times. The metal crossed $3,000 for the first time in mid-March 2025 and set 50+ new all-time highs during 2025 alone.

2025: An extraordinary year

One of the most extraordinary years in the gold price record. Gold gained approximately 64–67% during 2025, a performance that outpaced essentially every other major asset class. The key milestones came in rapid succession — each $500 increment was reached faster than the last:

January 2026: The historic $5,000 and $5,589 peak

The new year opened with gold already above $4,700 and still climbing. On January 19, 2026, gold hit $4,689 intraday. Then, on January 26, 2026, spot gold surged past $5,100 — breaching the psychologically significant $5,000 level for the first time in history. Just two days later, on January 28, 2026, gold struck its all-time record high:

The drivers of 2025–2026: escalating geopolitical tensions (particularly US-China trade conflict), central bank buying at record levels, de-dollarisation pressure, persistent inflation concerns, and weakening confidence in fiat currencies — a constellation of forces that mirrors, in updated form, the dynamics of the 1970s.

Spring 2026: After the January record, gold consolidated below the $5,589 peak — trading broadly in the $4,300–$4,750 range as markets absorbed the extraordinary 2025–2026 gains. See the live price band and chart above for exactly where gold is trading right now.

Gold-to-Silver Ratio · Historical Context

The GSR Through History

The gold-to-silver ratio (GSR) measures how many ounces of silver are needed to purchase one ounce of gold. It is one of the most closely watched metrics in the precious metals market because it reflects both relative supply-demand dynamics and broader market sentiment.

Historical GSR Scale · Where We Stand

The long-term historical average sits at approximately 55–65:1. When the ratio moves significantly above this range, silver is historically undervalued relative to gold.

The 2000–2026 reversal

Look at the past 25 years and the picture flips. From 2000 to March 2026, gold delivered a total return of approximately +1,580% — compared to +615% for the S&P 500 over the same period. The 2000–2026 period is unusual in gold's favour because it includes two major equity bear markets (2000–2003 and 2008–2009) and a period of sustained monetary expansion.

The Critical Investor Takeaway

Gold is not a consistent wealth-compounder in the way diversified equity portfolios are — it pays no dividends and generates no cash flow. Its primary role is as a store of value and portfolio hedge: it tends to hold value relative to fiat currencies over the very long run, and it tends to rise in periods of high inflation, dollar weakness, financial stress, and geopolitical crisis.

Market Mechanics · Day-to-Day Drivers

What Drives Daily Gold Price Moves

Understanding the big milestones is one thing; understanding what moves gold on any given day helps investors interpret current price action in its historical context.

The Dollar Relationship

Gold is priced in US dollars globally, so a weaker dollar generally makes gold cheaper for buyers using other currencies, increasing demand and pushing prices up.

Real Interest Rates

The most important medium-term variable. When real rates turn negative (inflation exceeds yields), holding gold becomes attractive vs. interest-bearing assets.

Central Bank Activity

Central banks hold ~35,000 tonnes of gold — over 20% of all gold ever mined. Their record buying in 2022–2025 has been a major support for prices.

Geopolitical Events

Iranian Revolution (1979), Gulf War (1990), 9/11 (2001), Russia-Ukraine (2022), US-China trade conflict (2025) — all visible as sharp upward spikes.

FAQ · Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the highest price of gold in history?

Gold's all-time high is $5,589.38 per troy ounce, reached intraday on January 28, 2026. This price also, for the first time, exceeded the 1980 peak adjusted for inflation.

What was the highest gold price in history when adjusted for inflation?

For decades, the 1980 peak of $850 held the record in real terms — equivalent to approximately $3,500 in today's dollars. The 2026 nominal high of $5,589 finally surpassed it on an inflation-adjusted basis — the first time in 45 years that real-terms parity was achieved.

What caused the biggest crash in gold price history?

The 1980–1999 bear market is the largest in gold's modern history — a 65% decline over 19 years. It was caused primarily by Paul Volcker's aggressive interest rate hikes (rates above 19%), which crushed inflation, restored dollar credibility, and made interest-bearing assets far more attractive than gold.

What is the historical gold-to-silver ratio?

The long-term historical average is approximately 55–65:1. It peaked at a record 124:1 during the COVID-19 panic in March 2020 and compressed back toward its long-run average by 2026 as silver recovered strongly. The live ratio on this page shows the current figure, updated every 60 seconds.

What is gold's historical rate of return compared to stocks?

From 2000 to 2026, gold has significantly outperformed equities (+1,580% vs +615% for the S&P 500). Over the very long run (since 1928), equities have averaged approximately 9.9% annually versus gold's approximately 5%. Gold outperforms in specific periods — high inflation, financial crises, and dollar weakness — while equities outperform in periods of growth and price stability.

When did gold first cross $1,000, $2,000, $3,000, $4,000, and $5,000?
  • $1,000: First crossed February 2009
  • $2,000: First crossed August 4, 2020
  • $3,000: First crossed mid-March 2025
  • $4,000: First crossed October 8, 2025
  • $5,000: First crossed January 24–26, 2026

Each $500 increment was reached faster than the last — a compression that reflected the accelerating momentum of the 2024–2026 bull market.

What is the lowest gold price in modern history?

Since the start of free-market gold trading in 1971, the lowest price was approximately $112.50 in August 1976. Before that, the fixed $35 price under the Bretton Woods system (1944–1971) represents the long-term floor of the fixed-price era.
