Is 2026 a good time to sell?

The short answer is yes — probably the best window in a generation. Gold crossed $5,000 per troy ounce for the first time in history in early 2026, peaking at $5,589.38 on 28 January. Even after pulling back from that peak, prices remain extraordinarily elevated compared to any historical average.

Analysts at J.P. Morgan, UBS, and Goldman Sachs have all published bullish forecasts through 2026 into 2027 — meaning prices may continue climbing. But timing the absolute peak is a fool's game. If you have jewellery you genuinely don't want or wear, current prices represent an exceptional window that may not return for years.

Practical rule of thumb. If you believe gold has further to run and the piece has no emotional or daily use, you can hold. If you've been meaning to clear a drawer of broken chains, mismatched earrings, or rings from a past chapter — sell now while the market is this strong. The melt value alone, in USD, is the highest it has ever been.

Step 1 — Know what your gold is actually worth

Before you approach a single buyer, you need to know the value of what you are selling. This one step alone separates sellers who get fair prices from those who get ripped off.

Gold jewellery value is determined by three things only: purity (how much of the piece is actually gold, measured in karats), weight (how many grams), and live spot price (the current global market price per gram of pure gold, quoted in USD).

Most jewellery sold in the United States is 10K or 14K. In the UK, 9K is common for everyday jewellery. In India and the Middle East, 22K is most prevalent. In Europe, 18K is the most widely sold standard for fine jewellery.

How to find your hallmark. Look for a small stamped number on the inside of a ring band, the clasp of a necklace, or the back of a pendant. That stamp is the most important number in determining value. If you cannot find one, a reputable buyer will test the piece for free using acid testing or an XRF scanner.

The melt-value formula

Once you know the karat and weight, calculating the melt value is straightforward:

Melt value (USD) = weight in grams × (karat ÷ 24) × current spot price per gram

Example 1 — 18K gold necklace, 8 grams:
Purity factor: 18 ÷ 24 = 0.75 • Pure gold content: 8g × 0.75 = 6g • At $—/g × 6g = $— melt value

Example 2 — 9K gold ring, 3.5 grams:
Purity factor: 9 ÷ 24 = 0.375 • Pure gold content: 3.5g × 0.375 = 1.31g • At $—/g × 1.31g = $— melt value

The melt value is the ceiling — the theoretical maximum if someone paid you 100% of spot. In practice, buyers pay a percentage of melt value, and that percentage varies enormously depending on who you sell to. The melt value is your negotiating baseline.

A note on gemstones, clasps and damaged parts

Most buyers calculate the weight of your gold after removing any stones, since gemstones are not gold. Some will make a separate offer for diamonds or precious stones, but for most standard pieces the gem value is negligible unless you have certified, high-quality stones. Clasps, pins, and attachments made from lower-purity alloys or non-gold materials are similarly excluded from the gold calculation.

Step 2 — The percentage buyers actually pay

Here is the number most sellers do not know before they walk into their first buyer — and it costs them money. Every buyer applies a margin to the melt value, paying you a percentage of what the gold is worth and keeping the rest as profit and operating cost.

This range is staggering. On a piece with a $1,000 melt value, the difference between a pawn shop offering 25% ($250) and a top online buyer offering 90% ($900) is $650 — for the exact same piece of gold. Getting three quotes takes an afternoon. That afternoon can be worth hundreds of dollars.

The practical lesson: always get multiple quotes before selling, and always calculate the melt value yourself first so you can immediately identify whether an offer is fair or exploitative.

Step 3 — Where to sell: all your options compared

Option 01

Online Gold Buyers

Best for price on standard scrap jewellery.

Best for Price
Typical payout
80–98%

Dedicated online gold buyers are consistently the highest-paying channel for standard scrap gold jewellery. Because they operate at scale with lower overhead than physical shops, the best operators pass more of the melt value back to sellers. The process typically works like this:

  • Request a free insured postal kit from the buyer's website
  • Weigh, photograph, and list your items before sending
  • Post your gold using the prepaid, tracked, insured packaging
  • Receive a detailed offer (weight × purity × buyer's USD rate) within 24–48 hours
  • Accept or reject — if you reject, reputable buyers return your items free of charge

What to look for. Clear, published USD price per gram by karat before you send anything; free, fully insured postal service with tracked delivery; free returns within a specified cooling-off period; transparent breakdown of offer (weight, purity, price per gram, total); strong independent reviews on Trustpilot or Google.

Examples in the US: Cash for Gold USA, APMEX scrap gold programme, Express Gold Cash, Worthy (for fine jewellery with gems).

Examples in the UK: Hatton Garden Metals, Gold Traders UK, Lois Bullion, BullionByPost, Cash4Gold-Now.

Best for: standard gold jewellery, broken pieces, mismatched earrings, chains without resale value beyond their gold content. Not ideal for designer or antique pieces that could command a premium above melt.

Option 02

Local Jewellers

Best for wearable, resellable pieces.

Good Choice

Reputable local jewellers consistently outpay pawn shops and often match or beat online buyers for pieces that have resale value beyond the raw metal. A jeweller who can sell your ring in their case at retail will pay more for it than a buyer who can only melt it down.

If your piece is in good condition, has a recognised design, or comes from a known brand, a jeweller is often a better channel than a scrap buyer.

Tip: Visit two or three local jewellers on the same day and compare written offers. Jewellers expect negotiation. Use one written offer as leverage with another. Phrases like “I have another offer at $X — can you match that?” are entirely reasonable and often effective.

Best for: wearable, good-condition pieces; branded jewellery; pieces with quality gemstones; estate jewellery.

If your jewellery is antique, vintage, from a luxury brand (Cartier, Van Cleef & Arpels, Tiffany, Bulgari) or features exceptional gemstones, an auction house can achieve prices that dwarf both melt value and local retail offers. Competitive bidding between collectors can push prices far above anything a dealer will offer.

Real-world example. An 18ct gold chain weighing 20g has a melt value around $3,040 at today's spot. A scrap buyer would pay roughly $2,432–$2,736 (80–90% of melt). At auction, a comparable chain in good condition recently achieved ~29% above its scrap value.

Top-tier: Sotheby's, Christie's, Bonhams and regional fine-art auction houses handle high-value jewellery regularly. Most offer free valuations.

Mid-tier: regional and online-only auction platforms (including eBay's auction format) can achieve retail-adjacent prices for pieces in good condition.

Note. Auction houses charge seller commission — typically 15–25% of the hammer price. Factor this into your net USD return.

Option 05

Pawn Shops

Last resort — only if you need cash within the hour.

Last Resort

Pawn shops are convenient and fast, but they are the lowest-paying option for almost every category of gold jewellery. Their business model is built around short-term loans, not specialist precious-metals dealing — which means they apply wide margins to cover the uncertainty of their diverse inventory.

Reports indicate pawn shops may pay as little as 20% of an item's melt value. Even the best pawn shops rarely exceed 60%. For context, that is less than half what the best online buyers pay for the same gold.

Use a pawn shop only if: you need cash within the hour, cannot access a bank or online payment, or are in a location with no other options.

How to sell gold jewellery online: a step-by-step process

If you have decided that an online buyer is the right route for your gold, here is the full process from drawer to bank account:

  1. 1
    Weigh your items at home

    Use a digital kitchen scale accurate to 0.1 g. Weigh each piece separately and record it. This gives you a reference to cross-check the buyer's weighing when they confirm the offer.

  2. 2
    Check the hallmarks

    Look for karat stamps (375 = 9K, 585 = 14K, 750 = 18K, 916 = 22K, 999 = 24K) on each piece. Record these alongside the weights.

  3. 3
    Calculate the melt value in USD

    Use the formula above, or open the free scrap gold calculator. This gives you a firm baseline to compare every offer against.

  4. 4
    Photograph everything before posting

    Take clear photos of each item alongside a scale showing its weight. These photos are your evidence if there is any dispute about condition or weight on arrival.

  5. 5
    Request free postal kits from two or three buyers

    There is no obligation to use them all, and comparing offers from multiple buyers is the single most effective way to maximise your return.

  6. 6
    Send using the buyer's insured, tracked packaging

    Never send gold in your own envelope. Reputable buyers provide free, fully insured postal bags. Confirm the insurance covers the full USD value — some caps are lower than the gold value.

  7. 7
    Review the offer and compare to your melt value

    A fair offer should be 75–95% of melt value. Below 70% — particularly from a well-resourced online buyer — is worth negotiating or declining.

  8. 8
    Accept or decline

    Reputable buyers must return your items if you reject the offer, at no cost to you. Confirm this policy before sending.

What about gold-plated jewellery?

This is one of the most common sources of disappointment for first-time gold sellers — and it is worth addressing directly.

Gold-plated jewellery is not solid gold, and most professional gold buyers will not purchase it as a gold piece. Gold plating is a thin coating of gold applied over a base metal (usually brass, copper, or silver). The amount of actual gold is so small that it has negligible value as scrap metal — a typical gold-plated necklace contains only ~0.05% gold by weight. If a buyer offers to purchase your plated jewellery by gram as though it were solid gold, that is a scam.

What you can do with gold-plated jewellery:

  • Sell on eBay, Vinted, Depop, or Facebook Marketplace as jewellery — private buyers will pay for the piece's aesthetic appeal regardless of metal content
  • Sell to vintage or costume jewellery dealers
  • Upcycle or repurpose — craft markets value decorative pieces regardless of metal value

Do not confuse gold-plated with gold-filled, which has a much thicker layer of gold bonded to a base metal core — typically 5% gold by weight. Some specialist buyers will purchase gold-filled items, though at a steep discount to solid gold.

Scams to avoid: protect yourself when selling gold

The gold-buying market contains a significant number of operators who rely on sellers not knowing the value of what they are selling. The most common 2026 scams to watch for:

Lowball "per item" offers

A buyer quotes a price per piece instead of per gram, hiding the calculation. Always ask for: weight × purity × price per gram = total. If they can't or won't show this, leave.

Underweighting

The buyer's scales "happen" to show your piece is lighter than your measurement at home. Weigh items before you go and insist on transparent, in-front-of-you weighing.

Purity downgrading

"Testing" claims your 18K piece is only 14K. Ask to see the test performed in front of you. A legitimate XRF tester or acid test is quick and visible. Refusal is a red flag.

Postal scams

You send your gold, receive a shockingly low offer, then discover returning items requires significant postage — or the company simply doesn't respond. Only use postal buyers with hundreds of verified reviews, a clear returns policy in writing, and free insured return postage.

"Today only" pressure

Any buyer who tells you their offer expires in minutes is using a manipulation tactic. A fair offer does not need to be rushed. Legitimate buyers are happy for you to shop around.

Hidden deductions

An offer is quoted at $500, but payment arrives at $420 — deductions for "testing" or "processing" fees never disclosed upfront. Always confirm the net USD payout in writing before agreeing.

Gold parties / travelling buyers

Pop-up "cash for gold" events at hotels or community halls use social pressure and quick-turnaround formats to prevent comparison shopping. Prices are almost always significantly below what a specialist would pay.

Plated-as-gold pricing

A buyer offers to purchase gold-plated jewellery at a per-gram rate as if it were solid. Real gold content in plating is negligible. This offer is a setup for an even smaller payout once "tests" come back.

Selling second-hand gold jewellery: beyond the melt value

Not all gold is created equal when it comes to resale. Before deciding on a buyer, ask yourself:

Is this piece from a recognised designer or brand? Cartier, Tiffany, David Yurman, Bulgari — branded jewellery commands significant premiums in the resale market. A certified Cartier Love bracelet in 18K weighing 30g has a melt value around $—. A second-hand luxury reseller might offer $4,500–$6,500 for the same piece as a branded item.

Is this piece antique or vintage? Pre-1940s jewellery, Art Deco pieces, Victorian gold, Edwardian settings — these often attract collector premiums that far exceed metal content. An auction house valuation costs nothing and could reveal a piece is worth multiples of its melt value.

Is this piece in excellent condition? Jewellery that is clean, undamaged, and retains its original design is worth more to a jeweller or private buyer than to a refiner. Light cleaning before valuation — but not polishing that could remove hallmarks — is worthwhile.

Second-hand luxury resale platforms for designer gold jewellery include: The RealReal, Vestiaire Collective, Worthy, and I Do Now I Don't (for bridal jewellery). Commissions typically run 20–30%, but the premiums over melt value often make these platforms worthwhile for the right pieces.

The expert's checklist: ten tips to maximise your payout

Here are the ten steps that separate sellers who get excellent prices from those who walk away disappointed:

  1. Weigh every item at homeon a digital scale before approaching any buyer. Record each piece separately with its hallmark karat.
  2. Calculate the melt value in USDusing the formula: grams × (karat ÷ 24) × current price per gram. Use our free scrap gold calculator.
  3. Get a minimum of three quotes— from at least one online buyer, one local jeweller, and one other specialist. Prices can legitimately vary by 20–30%+ on identical items.
  4. Separate pieces by category— fine jewellery goes to a jeweller or auction house; plain scrap gold goes to a specialist buyer; designer or antique pieces get their own assessment first.
  5. Know that gold-plated is negligible— route plated pieces to private resale platforms instead of gold buyers.
  6. Time your sale— selling when gold is near recent highs maximises your USD return. Check the live spot on the day you plan to sell.
  7. Negotiate— most buyers expect it. Use competing offers as leverage. Even 2–3% improvement is real money at today's prices.
  8. Never accept a "per item" offerwithout asking for the per gram breakdown. If a buyer cannot explain their calculation transparently, the offer is almost certainly unfair.
  9. For postal sales: photograph, weigh, insure everythingbefore it leaves your hands. Use fully insured tracked delivery — not a standard letter.
  10. For high-value pieces, get a pro appraisal first— a $50 appraisal from a certified gemologist can reveal whether a piece is worth selling as jewellery or as scrap. That distinction can be worth thousands.

Frequently asked questions

How much can I sell my gold jewellery for?

Your payout depends on weight, karat purity, and which buyer you choose. Use the formula: grams × (karat ÷ 24) × current USD spot price per gram to get melt value. Competitive buyers will pay 75–95% of this figure. Our interactive gold jewellery calculator gives you an instant live estimate.

What is the best place to sell gold jewellery?

For standard scrap gold, specialist online gold buyers and dedicated gold dealers consistently pay the most — typically 80–95% of melt value in USD. For wearable or designer pieces, local jewellers or luxury resale platforms will often pay more. For antique or high-value items, auction houses can achieve prices well above melt value.

Where can I sell gold jewellery near me?

Local options include reputable jewellers, specialist bullion dealers, and established pawn shops (as a last resort). Search for gold dealers with strong Google Reviews or Trustpilot ratings. 47th Street in New York, Hatton Garden in London, and similar jewellery districts in major cities concentrate specialist buyers who typically offer competitive rates.

Can I sell gold jewellery online safely?

Yes — if you use an established buyer with clear published USD rates, free insured postal packaging, a transparent offer process, and a free returns policy if you decline. Always photograph and weigh items before posting, use tracked and insured delivery, and check independent reviews before sending anything.

How do gold jewellery buyers calculate their price?

Buyers use the formula: weight × purity percentage × their buying price per gram (a percentage of the live USD spot price). The variation is entirely in that final percentage — the best buyers pay 85–95% of spot, while pawn shops may pay as little as 20–40%.

Should I sell gold jewellery now or wait?

Gold prices are near all-time highs in 2026, making it an excellent time to sell for cash if you have unwanted jewellery. Major financial institutions forecast continued strength through 2026 and 2027, so waiting could be rewarding — but there is no guarantee. If you need the cash or simply want to declutter, current USD prices represent an exceptional opportunity.

Can I sell gold-plated jewellery?

Not to gold buyers — the gold content in plated jewellery is negligible and most specialist buyers will not purchase it as gold. Sell gold-plated pieces as jewellery on eBay, Vinted, or Facebook Marketplace, where private buyers value the aesthetic appeal rather than the metal content.

What happens if I sell gold jewellery without a receipt?

In most countries, there is no legal requirement to have a purchase receipt to sell second-hand gold jewellery. Buyers are required by law in many jurisdictions to record the seller's identity (name, address, ID) as part of anti-money-laundering regulations — this is standard and applies to everyone. The absence of a receipt does not affect your USD price.

The bottom line

Selling gold jewellery in 2026 is one of the most financially rewarding decisions many people can make with unwanted or broken items they already own. The gold price has never been higher in USD terms. The online market has never been more competitive or accessible. And the gap between what informed sellers receive and what uninformed sellers accept has never been clearer.

The sellers who do best do three things: they know their gold's melt value before they talk to any buyer, they get multiple competing quotes, and they match the type of piece to the right channel. A plain 9K chain goes to a specialist scrap buyer. A Cartier ring goes to a luxury resale platform. An Art Deco brooch goes to an auction house for valuation first.

Everything else — the negotiation, the timing, the fraud avoidance — flows naturally from those three principles. Know what you have. Know what it is worth in USD. Know who will pay you the most for it.
