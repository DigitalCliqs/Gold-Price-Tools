How to Sell Gold Jewellery — Get the Best Price in 2026

By GoldPriceTools Editorial Team · Updated 8 May 2026 · 14 min read

Gold prices are sitting near all-time highs — above $4,700 per troy ounce as of May 2026, versus around $2,000 just two years ago. A broken chain in a drawer, a ring from a past relationship, a cluttered jewellery box of unworn items — all of these are worth significantly more today than at almost any previous point in history. But knowing your gold is worth something and actually getting a fair price for it are two very different things. This guide tells you exactly how to calculate your gold's value, where to sell it, and how to protect yourself from the scams that are common in this market.

Is 2026 a Good Time to Sell Gold Jewellery?

The short answer is yes — probably the best in a generation.

Gold surpassed $5,000 per ounce for the first time in history in early 2026, reaching an all-time high of $5,589.38 on 28 January 2026. Even after pulling back from that peak, prices remain extraordinarily elevated compared to any historical average. For sellers, this translates directly into higher cash offers for every gram of gold they bring to market.

That said, trying to time the exact peak is a fool's game. Analysts from J.P. Morgan, UBS, and Goldman Sachs have all published bullish forecasts for gold through 2026 and into 2027 — which means prices may continue rising. But for sellers who need the cash, or who simply have gold sitting unused, current prices represent a genuinely exceptional window that may not return for years.

One practical consideration: if you believe gold has further to run, keep it. If you have jewellery you genuinely do not want or need, sell it now while the market is this strong.

Step 1 — Know What Your Gold Is Actually Worth

Before you approach a single buyer, you need to know the value of what you are selling. This one step alone separates sellers who get fair prices from those who get ripped off.

Gold jewellery value is determined by three things only: purity (how much of the piece is actually gold, measured in karats), weight (how many grams), and live spot price (the current global market price per gram of pure gold).

Understanding Karats: What Do They Mean?

The karat system tells you what proportion of your jewellery is pure gold. The rest is alloy metals — typically copper, silver, or zinc — added to improve durability.

KaratPurityGold ContentCommon Hallmarks
24K99.9% pure99.9% gold999
22K91.6% pure91.6% gold916
18K75.0% pure75.0% gold750
14K58.5% pure58.5% gold585
10K41.7% pure41.7% gold417
9K37.5% pure37.5% gold375

Most jewellery sold in the United States is 10K or 14K. In the UK, 9K is common for everyday jewellery. In India and the Middle East, 22K is most prevalent. In Europe, 18K is the most widely sold standard for fine jewellery.

How to find your hallmark: Look for a small stamped number on the inside of a ring band, the clasp of a necklace, or the back of a pendant. This is your karat mark — the most important number in determining value. If you cannot find a hallmark, a reputable buyer will test the piece for free using acid testing or an XRF scanner.

How to Calculate Your Gold's Value

Once you know the karat and weight, calculating the melt value is straightforward:

Gold Melt Value = Weight (grams) × Purity % × Current Gold Price per gram

Example 1 — 18K gold necklace, 8 grams:
Purity factor: 18 ÷ 24 = 0.75 • Pure gold content: 8g × 0.75 = 6 grams • At $152/gram: 6 × $152 = $912 melt value

Example 2 — 9K gold ring, 3.5 grams:
Purity factor: 9 ÷ 24 = 0.375 • Pure gold content: 3.5g × 0.375 = 1.31 grams • At $152/gram: 1.31 × $152 = $199 melt value

The melt value is the ceiling — the theoretical maximum if someone paid you 100% of spot. In practice, buyers pay a percentage of melt value, and that percentage varies enormously depending on who you sell to. The melt value is your negotiating baseline.

A Note on Gemstones and Clasps

Most buyers calculate the weight of your gold after removing any stones, since gemstones are not gold. Some buyers will make a separate offer for diamonds or other precious stones, but for most standard pieces, gem value is often negligible unless you have certified, high-quality stones. Clasps, pins, and attachments made from lower-purity alloys or non-gold materials are similarly excluded from the gold calculation.

Step 2 — Understand What Percentage Buyers Actually Pay

Here is the number that most sellers do not know before they walk into their first buyer — and it costs them money. Every buyer applies a margin to the melt value, paying you a percentage of what the gold is worth and keeping the rest as profit or operating costs.

Buyer TypeTypical Payout (% of Melt Value)
Pawn shops20%–60%
High street jewellers40%–75%
Average online buyers60%–75%
Specialist gold buyers75%–90%
Top competitive buyers80%–98%

This range is staggering. On a piece with a $1,000 melt value, the difference between a pawn shop offering 25% ($250) and a top online gold buyer offering 90% ($900) is $650 — for the exact same piece of gold. Getting three quotes takes an afternoon. That afternoon could be worth hundreds of dollars.

The practical lesson: always get multiple quotes before selling, and always calculate the melt value yourself first so you can immediately identify whether an offer is fair or exploitative.

Step 3 — Know Where to Sell: All Your Options Compared

Option 1: Online Gold Buyers
Best for price on standard scrap jewellery
Best for Price

Dedicated online gold buyers are consistently the highest-paying channel for standard scrap gold jewellery. Because they operate at scale with lower overhead than physical shops, the best operators are able to pass more of the melt value back to sellers. The process typically works as follows:

  • Request a free insured postal kit from the buyer's website
  • Weigh, photograph, and list your items before sending
  • Post your gold using the prepaid, tracked, insured packaging
  • Receive a detailed offer (weight × purity × buyer's rate) within 24–48 hours
  • Accept or reject — if you reject, reputable buyers return your items free of charge

What to look for in a reputable online buyer: Clear, published price per gram by karat before you send anything; free, fully insured postal service with tracked delivery; free returns within a specified cooling-off period; transparent breakdown of offer (weight, purity, price per gram, total); strong independent reviews on Trustpilot or Google.

Online buyers in the US: Cash for Gold USA, APMEX scrap gold programme, Express Gold Cash, Worthy (for fine jewellery with gems)

Online buyers in the UK: Hatton Garden Metals, Gold Traders UK, Lois Bullion, BullionByPost, Cash4Gold-Now

Best for: Standard gold jewellery, broken pieces, mismatched earrings, chains without resale value beyond the gold content. Not ideal for designer or antique pieces that could command a premium above melt value.

Option 2: Local Jewellers
Best for wearable, resellable pieces
Good Choice

Reputable local jewellers consistently outpay pawn shops and often match or beat online buyers for pieces that have resale value beyond the raw metal. A jeweller who can sell your ring or necklace in their case at retail will pay more for it than a buyer who can only melt it down.

If your piece is in good condition, has a recognised design, or comes from a known brand, a jeweller is often a better channel than a scrap buyer. They can evaluate the piece as a whole rather than reducing it to its metal content.

Tip: Visit two or three local jewellers on the same day and compare written offers. Jewellers expect negotiation. Use one offer as leverage with another. Phrases like "I have another offer at $X — can you match that?" are entirely reasonable and often effective.

Best for: Wearable, good-condition pieces; branded jewellery; pieces with quality gemstones; estate jewellery.

Option 3: Auction Houses
Best for antique, designer or high-value pieces
High Value

If your jewellery is antique, vintage, from a luxury brand (Cartier, Van Cleef & Arpels, Tiffany, Bulgari), or features exceptional gemstones, an auction house may achieve prices that dwarf both melt value and local retail offers. Competitive bidding between collectors can push prices far above anything a dealer will offer.

A real-world example: an 18ct gold chain weighing 20 grams has a melt value of around $2,280. A scrap buyer would pay approximately $1,824–$2,052. At auction, the same chain achieved $2,940 — 29% above its scrap value.

For top-tier pieces: Sotheby's, Christie's, Bonhams, and regional fine art auction houses handle high-value jewellery regularly. Most offer free valuations.

For mid-tier pieces: Regional and online-only auction platforms (including eBay auction format) can achieve retail-adjacent prices for pieces in good condition.

Best for: Antique jewellery; designer or branded pieces; heirloom items with provenance; rings with certified diamonds.

Note: Auction houses charge seller commission — typically 15–25% of the hammer price. Factor this into your net return calculation.

Option 4: eBay & Online Marketplaces
Best for niche, fashion and wearable pieces
Good Choice

Selling gold jewellery directly on eBay puts you in front of 134 million active buyers globally — including private collectors who may pay above scrap value for the right piece. Unique, vintage, or unusual pieces often command a premium here that no trade buyer would match.

eBay fees for jewellery: Listings up to 250 items: free. Items priced $1,000–$7,500: 6.5% final value fee. Items priced above $7,500: 3% final value fee.

Other platforms worth considering: Facebook Marketplace for local cash sales with no fees (carry personal safety awareness when meeting strangers); Etsy for handmade or vintage pieces; Worthy for certified diamond jewellery.

Best for: Wearable pieces; jewellery with unique design or vintage appeal; items that a private buyer might value above melt.

Not ideal for: Broken pieces, mismatched sets, or damaged jewellery that a private buyer would not want to wear.

Option 5: Pawn Shops
Last resort — use only if you need cash immediately
Last Resort

Pawn shops are convenient and fast, but they are the lowest-paying option for almost every category of gold jewellery. Their business model is built around short-term loans, not specialist precious metals dealing — which means they apply wide margins to cover the uncertainty of their diverse inventory.

Reports indicate pawn shops may pay as little as 20% of an item's melt value. Even the best pawn shops rarely exceed 60%. For context, that is less than half what the best online buyers pay for the same gold.

Use a pawn shop only if: you need cash immediately (same hour), cannot access a bank or online payment, or are in a location with no other options.

How to Sell Gold Jewellery Online: A Step-by-Step Process

If you have decided that an online buyer is the right route for your gold, here is the complete process:

  1. 1
    Weigh your items at home

    Use a digital kitchen scale accurate to 0.1 grams. Weigh each piece separately and record it. This gives you a reference to cross-check the buyer's weighing when they confirm the offer.

  2. 2
    Check the hallmarks

    Look for karat stamps (375 = 9K, 585 = 14K, 750 = 18K, 916 = 22K, 999 = 24K) on each piece. Record these alongside the weights.

  3. 3
    Calculate the melt value

    Use the formula from Step 1 of this guide, or use a free online scrap gold calculator. This gives you a firm baseline to compare all offers against.

  4. 4
    Photograph everything before posting

    Take clear photos of each item alongside a scale showing its weight. These photos are your evidence if there is any dispute about condition or weight on arrival.

  5. 5
    Request free postal kits from two or three buyers simultaneously

    There is no obligation to use them all, and comparing offers from multiple buyers is the single most effective way to maximise your return.

  6. 6
    Send using the buyer's insured, tracked packaging

    Never send gold in your own envelope. Reputable buyers provide free, fully insured postal bags. Make sure the insurance covers the full value — some caps are lower than the gold value.

  7. 7
    Review the offer and compare to your melt value

    A fair offer should be 75–95% of melt value. Below 70% — particularly from a well-resourced online buyer — is worth negotiating or declining.

  8. 8
    Accept or decline

    Reputable buyers must return your items if you reject the offer, at no cost to you. Confirm this policy before sending.

What About Gold-Plated Jewellery?

This is one of the most common sources of disappointment for first-time gold sellers — and it is worth addressing directly.

Gold-plated jewellery is not solid gold, and most professional gold buyers will not purchase it as a gold piece. Gold plating is a thin coating of gold applied over a base metal (usually brass, copper, or silver). The amount of actual gold in a plated piece is so small that it has negligible value as scrap metal. A typical gold-plated necklace contains only 0.05% or less of actual gold by weight. If someone offers to buy your gold-plated jewellery by gram as though it were solid gold, that is a scam.

What you can do with gold-plated jewellery:

  • Sell on eBay, Vinted, Depop, or Facebook Marketplace as jewellery — private buyers will pay for the piece's aesthetic appeal regardless of metal content
  • Sell to vintage or costume jewellery dealers
  • Upcycle or repurpose — craft markets value decorative pieces regardless of metal value

Do not confuse gold-plated with gold-filled, which has a much thicker layer of gold bonded to a base metal core — typically 5% gold by weight. Some specialist buyers will purchase gold-filled items, though at a steep discount to solid gold.

Scams to Avoid: Protect Yourself When Selling Gold

The gold buying market contains a significant number of operators who rely on sellers not knowing the value of what they are selling. Here are the most common scams in 2026:

Warning signs that should prompt you to walk away:

  • Lowball offers without explanation. A buyer quotes a price per item rather than per gram, making the calculation opaque. Always ask for the breakdown: weight × purity × price per gram = total. If a buyer cannot or will not show you this calculation, leave.
  • Underweighting. The buyer's scales "happen" to show your piece is lighter than your own measurement at home. Always weigh items before you go and insist on transparent, in-front-of-you weighing.
  • Purity downgrading. The buyer claims your 18K piece is only 14K after "testing." Ask to see the test performed in front of you. A legitimate XRF tester or acid test is quick and visible. If a buyer refuses, be very sceptical.
  • Postal scams. You send your gold, receive a shockingly low offer, and discover returning items requires significant postage — or the company is unresponsive. Only use postal gold buyers with hundreds of verified reviews, a clear returns policy in writing, and free insured return postage.
  • "Today only" pressure tactics. Any buyer who tells you their offer expires in minutes is using a manipulation tactic. A fair offer does not need to be rushed. Legitimate buyers are happy for you to shop around.
  • Hidden deductions. An offer is made for $500, but payment arrives at $420 — deductions for "testing fees" or "processing fees" never disclosed upfront. Always confirm the total net payout in writing before agreeing to any sale.
  • Gold party / travelling buyers. Pop-up "cash for gold" events at hotels or community halls use social pressure and quick-turnaround formats to prevent comparison shopping. The prices at these events are almost always significantly below what a specialist buyer would pay.

Selling Second-Hand Gold Jewellery: Beyond the Melt Value

Not all gold is created equal when it comes to resale. Before deciding on a buyer, ask yourself:

Is this piece from a recognised designer or brand? Cartier, Tiffany, David Yurman, Bulgari — branded jewellery commands significant premiums in the resale market. A certified Cartier Love bracelet in 18K gold weighing 30 grams has a melt value of around $3,420. A second-hand luxury reseller might offer $4,500–$6,000 for the same piece as a branded item.

Is this piece antique or vintage? Pre-1940s jewellery, Art Deco pieces, Victorian gold, Edwardian settings — these often attract collector premiums that far exceed their metal content. An auction house valuation costs nothing and could reveal a piece is worth multiples of its melt value.

Is this piece in excellent condition? Jewellery that is clean, undamaged, and retains its original design is worth more to a jeweller or private buyer than to a refiner. Light cleaning before valuation — but not polishing that could remove hallmarks — is worthwhile.

Second-hand luxury resale platforms for designer gold jewellery include: The RealReal, Vestiaire Collective, Worthy, and I Do Now I Don't (for bridal jewellery). Commissions typically run 20–30%, but the premiums over melt value often make these platforms worthwhile for the right pieces.

Tips to Maximise What You Get: The Expert's Checklist

Here are the ten steps that separate sellers who get excellent prices from those who walk away disappointed:

  1. Weigh every item at home on a digital scale before approaching any buyer. Record each piece separately with its hallmark karat.
  2. Calculate the melt value using the formula: grams × (karat ÷ 24) × current gold price per gram. Use our free scrap gold calculator for convenience.
  3. Get a minimum of three quotes — from at least one online buyer, one local jeweller, and one other specialist. Prices can legitimately vary by 20–30% or more for identical items.
  4. Separate pieces by category. Fine jewellery goes to a jeweller or auction house for valuation. Plain scrap gold goes to a specialist buyer. Designer or antique pieces get their own assessment first.
  5. Know that gold-plated jewellery has negligible scrap value — route it to private resale platforms instead.
  6. Time your sale strategically. Selling when gold prices are near recent highs maximises your return. Check the live spot price on the day you plan to sell, as buyers' offers move daily with the market.
  7. Negotiate. Most buyers expect it. Use competing offers as leverage. Even a 2–3% improvement is real money when gold is $152 per gram.
  8. Never accept a "per item" offer without asking for the per gram breakdown. If a buyer cannot explain their calculation transparently, the offer is almost certainly unfair.
  9. For postal sales: photograph, weigh, and insure everything before it leaves your hands. Use fully insured tracked delivery — not a standard letter.
  10. For high-value or unusual pieces, get a professional appraisal first. A $50 appraisal from a certified gemologist can reveal whether a piece is worth selling as jewellery or as scrap — and that distinction can be worth thousands.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much can I sell my gold jewellery for?

Your payout depends on the weight, karat purity, and which buyer you choose. Use the formula: grams × (karat ÷ 24) × current spot price per gram to get the melt value. Competitive buyers will pay 75–95% of this figure. Our interactive gold jewellery calculator gives you an instant live estimate.

What is the best place to sell gold jewellery?

For standard scrap gold, specialist online gold buyers and dedicated gold dealers consistently pay the most — typically 80–95% of melt value. For wearable or designer pieces, local jewellers or luxury resale platforms will often pay more. For antique or high-value items, auction houses can achieve prices well above melt value.

Where can I sell gold jewellery near me?

Local options include reputable jewellers, specialist bullion dealers, and established pawn shops (as a last resort). Search for gold dealers with strong Google Reviews or Trustpilot ratings in your city. Hatton Garden in London, 47th Street in New York, and similar jewellery districts in major cities concentrate specialist buyers who typically offer competitive rates.

Can I sell gold jewellery online safely?

Yes — if you use an established buyer with clear published rates, free insured postal packaging, a transparent offer process, and a free returns policy if you decline the offer. Always photograph and weigh items before posting, use tracked and insured delivery, and check independent reviews before sending anything.

How do gold jewellery buyers calculate their price?

Buyers use the formula: weight × purity percentage × their buying price per gram (which is a percentage of the live spot price). The variation is entirely in that final percentage — the best buyers pay 85–95% of spot, while pawn shops may pay as little as 20–40%.

Should I sell gold jewellery now or wait?

Gold prices are near all-time highs in 2026, making it an excellent time to sell for cash if you have unwanted jewellery. Major financial institutions forecast continued strength through 2026 and 2027, so waiting could also be rewarding — but there is no guarantee. If you need the cash or simply want to declutter, current prices represent an exceptional opportunity.

Can I sell gold-plated jewellery?

Not to gold buyers — the gold content in plated jewellery is negligible and most specialist buyers will not purchase it as gold. Sell gold-plated pieces as jewellery on eBay, Vinted, or Facebook Marketplace, where private buyers value the aesthetic appeal rather than the metal content.

What happens if I sell gold jewellery without a receipt?

In most countries, there is no legal requirement to have a purchase receipt to sell second-hand gold jewellery. You own the item and are free to sell it. Buyers are required by law in many jurisdictions to record the seller's identity (name, address, ID) as part of anti-money laundering regulations — this is standard and applies to everyone. The absence of a receipt does not affect your price.

The Bottom Line

Selling gold jewellery in 2026 is one of the most financially rewarding decisions many people can make with unwanted or broken items they already own. The gold price has never been higher. The online market has never been more competitive or accessible. And the gap between what informed sellers receive and what uninformed sellers accept has never been clearer.

The sellers who do best are the ones who do three things: they know their gold's melt value before they talk to any buyer, they get multiple competing quotes, and they match the type of piece to the right selling channel. A plain 9K gold chain goes to a specialist scrap buyer. A Cartier ring goes to a luxury resale platform. An Art Deco brooch goes to an auction house for valuation first.

Everything else — the negotiation, the timing, the fraud avoidance — flows naturally from those three principles. Know what you have. Know what it is worth. Know who will pay you the most for it.
