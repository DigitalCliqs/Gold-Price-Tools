How to Sell Gold Jewellery — Get the Best Price

By GoldPriceTools Editorial Team · Published 3 May 2026 · Updated 3 May 2026

Selling gold jewellery is straightforward once you know the melt value of your items and understand what a fair offer looks like. This guide covers the five steps to getting the best price, the different types of buyers, red flags to avoid, and how to calculate what your jewellery is worth before you accept any offer.

Before You Sell: Know Your Melt Value

The melt value is the intrinsic worth of the gold content at today’s spot price. Dealers pay a percentage of this value — typically 85–95% for reputable online buyers or 70–85% for high-street shops. Knowing your melt value before approaching any buyer gives you a firm negotiating position.

5 Steps to Sell Gold Jewellery

  1. Calculate the melt value using our calculator. Weigh each item separately (standard kitchen scales accurate to 0.1g are sufficient).
  2. Identify the karat from the hallmark (375 = 9K, 585 = 14K, 750 = 18K, 916 = 22K).
  3. Get at least 3 quotes — one from an online buyer, one from a local jeweller, one from a specialist refiner.
  4. Compare offers as a % of spot, not just as a cash amount. The best offer pays the highest percentage of the melt value.
  5. Complete the sale securely — use Royal Mail Special Delivery (insured) for postal services; get a receipt for walk-in transactions.

Jewellery vs Scrap Value

If your gold jewellery is from a designer brand (Tiffany, Cartier, Bulgari) or has significant gemstones, the piece may be worth substantially more than its melt value. In this case, selling as scrap would leave significant money on the table. Consider auction or a specialist pre-owned jewellery dealer who can assess craftsmanship value.

Red Flags to Avoid

  • Buyers who don’t tell you the karat they tested or the weight they recorded
  • Offers quoted as a fixed “per gram” rate with no mention of karat
  • Pressure to accept immediately or “the price is only good today”
  • Any buyer who asks you to send jewellery before receiving a quote

Step 1: Weigh Your Gold Correctly

Accurate weighing is the most important step before approaching any buyer. Use a digital jeweller's scale accurate to 0.01g. Weigh each item individually and note the weight. Avoid kitchen scales — they are typically only accurate to 1–5g, which can mean a significant error in calculated value.

Weigh items as they are (don't attempt to remove stones unless you can do so without damage). When you present items to a buyer, they will weigh the gold portion only, deducting stones and non-gold components. Getting your own accurate weights first gives you a negotiating baseline.

Step 2: Identify the Karat and Calculate Melt Value

Check each item for a hallmark — a small stamped number or mark indicating gold purity. Common UK hallmarks:

  • 375 = 9K gold (37.5% pure)
  • 585 = 14K gold (58.33% pure)
  • 750 = 18K gold (75.0% pure)
  • 916 = 22K gold (91.67% pure)
  • 999 = 24K gold (99.9% pure)

Use our live gold calculator to find the current melt value per gram for each karat. Multiply by your item's weight to get its intrinsic gold value. This is your minimum benchmark — no legitimate dealer should offer substantially less.

For maximum return on scrap gold, get quotes from at least 3 online buyers before committing. Reputable UK online gold buyers include Hatton Garden Metals, BullionByPost Gold Buyers, and Gold Buyers UK — all publish transparent daily buy rates.

Step 4: Red Flags to Avoid

Warning signs of a poor or dishonest buyer:

  • Refuses to show you how they weighed your gold or which karat they identified
  • Gives a price per pennyweight rather than per gram (makes comparison harder)
  • "Cash4Gold" style envelopes with no independent valuation
  • Pressuring for an immediate decision — reputable buyers will give you time to compare quotes
  • Testing gold with acids in front of you without explaining the karat result

What About Gold-Plated or Gold-Filled Items?

Gold-plated items (the vast majority of yellow jewellery) have only a very thin surface layer of gold — typically worth pennies per item. Most gold buyers will decline to purchase gold-plated items or offer nominal amounts. Look for a solid hallmark (375, 585, 750, etc.) stamped into the metal — not just a "GP", "GF", or "GE" mark, which indicate plated or filled items with no significant gold content.
