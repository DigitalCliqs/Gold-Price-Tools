Every time you check the gold or silver price — on a dealer website, a financial news feed, or a commodity chart — the price you see is quoted in troy ounces. Not regular ounces, not grams, not pounds. Troy ounces. And yet the troy ounce is not something most people ever learned about in school. It is not the same ounce you use to weigh food or measure liquids. It is a separate, older unit of weight that has been used to measure precious metals for centuries and shows no sign of going away.

Understanding the troy ounce is not a niche interest for coin collectors. It is the key that unlocks every precious metals calculation — the price per gram of your gold jewellery, the value of a silver bar, the weight of a gold coin you are considering buying, and the melt value of anything made of gold, silver, platinum, or palladium. Once you grasp it, everything in the precious metals world starts to make a great deal more sense.

The troy ounce is the single most important number in precious metals — commit 31.1035 grams to memory and every other calculation in this guide follows from it.

What Is a Troy Ounce?

A troy ounce is a unit of weight used exclusively for precious metals — gold, silver, platinum, and palladium — and certain gemstones. It is the global standard: every spot price, every bullion coin weight, every gold bar specification, and every dealer quote in the precious metals industry uses the troy ounce.

The Master Conversion
1 troy ounce = 31.1034768 grams

For everyday calculations, 31.1035 grams is used universally and is accurate enough for all real-world precious-metals valuation. This single figure unlocks every conversion on this page.

Troy Ounce vs Regular Ounce

The “regular” ounce most people know is the avoirdupois ounce (abbreviated “oz”), used for food, general goods, postal weights, and body weight in countries using the imperial system. It weighs 28.3495 grams. The troy ounce is heavier — by 9.7%, or about 2.75 grams more than a regular ounce.

This gap may not sound dramatic, but it is significant with large quantities or when valuing a specific item. Using the wrong ounce produces a 9.7% error — which on a $5,000 gold purchase is a $485 mistake.

The critical practical rule: In the bullion world, when someone says “an ounce of gold” and writes “oz,” they always mean a troy ounce. “Ounce” is shorthand for “troy ounce” throughout the industry — even though, technically, ozt = troy ounce and oz = avoirdupois ounce.

Why Is the Troy Ounce Different From a Regular Ounce?

The two ounces exist because they come from two completely separate historical weight systems developed for different purposes.

The Origins of the Troy System

The troy weight system is believed to have originated at the trade fairs of Troyes, a prosperous commercial city in the Champagne region of north-eastern France, during the 12th and 13th centuries. Merchants from across the continent gathered at its annual fairs to trade silk, spices, textiles, and precious metals. The weights used there became standardised, and the troy system spread throughout Europe as the accepted standard for monetary metals.

12th–13th c.
The Fairs of Troyes, France

One of medieval Europe’s great trading hubs standardises weights for precious and monetary metals. The “troy” name endures to this day.

1527
England Adopts Troy by Statute

Troy weight becomes the official standard for gold and silver trading in the British Isles. Coins are struck to troy standards, and troy becomes the basis for monetary value.

Today
The Global Spot-Price Standard

The LBMA in London, COMEX in New York, and the Shanghai Gold Exchange all quote gold and silver in troy ounces. It is the universal language of bullion.

The troy system is built on a unit called the grain (the same grain used in both the troy and avoirdupois systems). Each step up is a clean multiple of the last:

24 grains= 1 pennyweight (dwt)
20 dwt= 1 troy ounce (480 grains total)
12 ozt= 1 troy pound (5,760 grains total)

The Origins of the Avoirdupois System

The avoirdupois system — the “regular” system for everyday weights — developed separately, primarily for bulk goods such as wool, grain, and food. Its pound contains 16 ounces, each of 437.5 grains, totalling 7,000 grains per pound.

A troy ounce is heavier than a regular ounce — yet a troy pound is lighter than a regular pound.

This seeming paradox trips up almost everyone the first time. How can it be true? Because a troy pound has only 12 troy ounces, whereas a regular pound has 16 regular ounces:

  • 1 troy pound = 12 × 31.1035 g = 373.24 grams
  • 1 avoirdupois pound = 16 × 28.3495 g = 453.59 grams

This is why gold prices quoted per pound are not simply 16 times the per-ounce price — the conversion is more involved, and you must always confirm which pound is meant.

How Many Grams in a Troy Ounce?

Exactly 31.1034768 grams — this is the precise, internationally defined value. For practical calculations, 31.1035 grams is used universally and is sufficiently accurate for all real-world precious-metals valuation. This single conversion unlocks the per-gram price from any quoted spot price:

Price Per Gram Formula
Price per gram = Spot price per troy oz ÷ 31.1035

Gold at ~$4,513/oz (May 2026): $4,513 ÷ 31.1035 = $145.09 / gram of 24K gold.

Silver at ~$76/oz (May 2026): $76 ÷ 31.1035 = $2.44 / gram of 999 fine silver.

Troy Ounces in a Kilo, Pound & Tonne

How Many Troy Ounces in a Kilo?

1 kilogram = 32.1507 troy ounces (precisely 32.150746568628). This is one of the most important conversions for bullion investors, because kilo bars are a popular investment size for both gold and silver.

Kilo Bar Value

1 kg gold bar at ~$4,513/oz: 32.1507 × $4,513 = ~$145,076

1 kg silver bar at ~$76/oz: 32.1507 × $76 = ~$2,443

The inverse — grams per troy ounce — is 31.1035, making these two conversions perfectly reciprocal.

How Many Troy Ounces in a Pound?

This has two very different answers depending on which pound you mean:

  • 1 avoirdupois pound (regular pound) = 14.5833 troy ounces
  • 1 troy pound = exactly 12 troy ounces

The troy pound is almost never used in modern precious-metals trading — you will essentially never see gold or silver priced per troy pound. For all practical purposes, if someone quotes a price “per pound,” clarify whether they mean a regular pound (14.5833 troy oz) or a troy pound (12 troy oz). The difference is roughly 22% — enormous.

How Many Troy Ounces in a Metric Tonne?

1 metric tonne (1,000 kg) = 32,150.7 troy ounces. This conversion matters for understanding gold production statistics, central-bank reserves, and mine output — all reported in metric tonnes.

At ~$4,513/oz, 1 tonne of gold is worth ~$145 billion. Global annual mine production is about 3,600 tonnes (~115.7 million troy oz). All the gold ever mined — roughly 212,582 tonnes — equals about 6.83 billion troy ounces.

The 400 Troy Ounce Gold Bar

The 400 troy ounce gold bar is the famous “Good Delivery” bar — the standard unit used for trading gold between central banks, governments, and institutional participants in the London Bullion Market. These are the bars you see stacked in vaults in news footage: far too heavy and expensive for individual investors.

1 Troy Ounce Silver: Value & Coins

A 1 troy ounce silver coin or bar contains exactly 31.1035 grams of silver at the stated purity (usually 99.9% fine). At a silver spot price of ~$76/oz (May 2026), the melt value of a 1 oz 999 silver coin is exactly $76. Retail prices are higher due to dealer premiums; scrap or resale prices may be slightly lower.

Troy Ounce to Gram: The Master Conversion

Because global spot prices are quoted in troy ounces but most jewellery, scrap items, and smaller quantities are weighed in grams, the troy ounce–to–gram conversion is the single most frequently needed calculation in precious metals.

Both Directions
Grams = Troy oz × 31.1035  •  Troy oz = Grams ÷ 31.1035

Worked example — an 8 g, 18K gold necklace at $4,513/oz:

1. Convert to troy oz: 8 ÷ 31.1035 = 0.2572 ozt
2. Apply purity: 0.2572 × 0.750 (18K = 75%) = 0.1929 ozt pure gold
3. Multiply by spot: 0.1929 × $4,513 = ~$870.56 melt value

Or directly in grams: 8 g × ($4,513 ÷ 31.1035) × 0.75 = 8 × $145.09 × 0.75 = ~$870.54

Pennyweight (DWT): The Jeweller’s Unit

Alongside the troy ounce and grams, precious-metals dealers — particularly those handling jewellery and dental gold — often use the pennyweight (dwt).

  • 1 troy ounce = 20 pennyweights
  • 1 pennyweight = 1.55517 grams
  • 1 pennyweight = 0.05 troy oz

Some pawn shops, jewellers, and scrap-gold buyers quote prices per pennyweight rather than per gram. This can cause confusion, so always verify which unit is being used before agreeing a price.

If a dealer offers “$8 per pennyweight” for 18K gold, that is $8 ÷ 1.55517 = $5.14/gram, or $8 × 20 = $160 per troy ounce of 18K. Compare that to melt value ($4,513 × 0.75 = $3,385/oz of 18K) to instantly see what percentage of melt is being offered.

Tola, Tael & Baht: Regional Weight Units

In South Asia, the Middle East, and parts of East Asia, regional units that predate the troy ounce remain in everyday use for gold trading.

The “100 Mills 999 Fine Gold” Warning

A product that causes endless confusion is the “100 mills 999 fine gold 1 troy ounce” bar or novelty coin, sold widely on auction sites. Despite the impressive wording, it is not solid gold and is not worth anywhere near the gold spot price.

Not Gold
“100 mills” means plated — not solid.

“100 mills” refers to a layer of gold 100 thousandths of an inch thick — a vanishingly thin coating electroplated over a base-metal core (usually brass or copper). The total gold content is a tiny fraction of a troy ounce, typically worth less than $1.

The “999 fine gold” describes the purity of the plating layer only, not the item overall. A genuine 1 troy ounce gold bar (99.9% fine) at current prices would cost approximately $4,500+. If the price looks too good to be true, it is.

Why Do We Use Troy Ounces? Why Not Just Grams?

This is a reasonable question, and the honest answer is: inertia, standardisation, and historical entrenchment. The troy ounce became the global standard for precious-metals pricing centuries before the metric system existed. By the time the metric system was adopted internationally, the entire gold and silver trading infrastructure — exchanges, contracts, price quotes, banking conventions — was already built around troy ounces. The cost and complexity of switching has simply never been worth it.

The troy ounce is also well suited to its purpose. At 31.1035 grams it represents a meaningful, tradable quantity of gold at any historical price — neither too large nor too small. The grain (1/480 of a troy ounce, 0.0648 g) let ancient assayers and moneyers measure coin weights with extraordinary precision for their time.

The troy ounce is the Esperanto of precious metals — a universal unit everyone in the industry understands, regardless of their national measurement system.

Countries that operate entirely in metric — most of the world outside the United States — still quote precious metals in troy ounces when trading internationally, then convert to local gram or kilogram prices for domestic retail.
