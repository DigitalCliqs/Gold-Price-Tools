01
Foundations · The Benchmark

What Is the Silver Spot Price?

The silver spot price is the current market price for one troy ounce of pure (99.9% fine) silver, quoted for immediate settlement. It is the global benchmark — the single number from which every other silver price is derived. When you see silver quoted on financial sites, bullion dealer pages, or commodity terminals, that number is almost always the spot price.

As of mid-May 2026, silver is trading at approximately $75–$78 per troy ounce on the COMEX exchange in New York, having pulled back substantially from its all-time high of $121.62 reached on January 29, 2026. At these levels, silver has still delivered extraordinary returns compared to its price of approximately $30 per ounce at the start of 2025.

Quoted in US dollars per troy ounce globally — other currencies derived via exchange rates
Represents 99.9% pure silver — no alloys or manufacturing costs included
Updates continuously during market hours — every few seconds via live data feeds
Does not include dealer premiums — retail silver always costs more than spot

The Troy Ounce

All silver and gold prices worldwide are quoted in troy ounces, not regular ounces. This distinction matters:

1 troy ounce = 31.1035 grams
1 regular (avoirdupois) ounce = 28.3495 grams
Common Mistake

Using the regular ounce by mistake leads to systematic undervaluation of silver by about 10%. Always confirm that any silver price is expressed per troy ounce before converting to grams or kilograms.

03
Mechanics · Three Global Venues

How the Silver Spot Price Is Determined

The silver spot price is not fixed by any single institution. It emerges from continuous global trading across three main venues and session times:

COMEX (New York)

The Chicago Mercantile Exchange's COMEX division is the world's most active silver futures market. The COMEX price sets the dominant reference for the global spot price during North American hours.

LBMA (London)

The London Bullion Market Association publishes the LBMA Silver Price once daily at 12:00 PM London time via electronic auction. This is the primary benchmark for large physical silver transactions worldwide.

Shanghai Futures Exchange

China is one of the world's largest silver consumers. The Shanghai exchange plays an increasingly significant role in price discovery, particularly for Asian session moves.

Continuous Real-Time Spot

The number that updates in real time on financial sites is effectively derived from near-term COMEX futures activity, with the LBMA fixing as the daily institutional reference.

Kitco Silver Spot Price

Kitco is one of the most widely referenced live silver price feeds, particularly among North American and international precious metals investors. The Kitco silver spot price reflects real-time COMEX data and is published in US dollars per troy ounce, with intraday charts, historical data, and multiple weight and currency conversions available on their platform.

As of May 19, 2026, Kitco showed silver at approximately $75.87 per troy ounce, within a recent weekly range of $75.61 to $79.02.

04
Everyday Silver · 92.5% Standard

Sterling Silver Price: What It Is & How to Calculate It

Sterling silver is the silver you encounter most often in everyday life — cutlery, jewellery, teapots, photograph frames, and decorative items. It is not pure silver. Understanding the difference between sterling silver and pure silver, and knowing how to calculate its value, is essential for anyone looking to buy, sell, or assess silver items.

What Is Sterling Silver?

Sterling silver is an alloy that is 92.5% pure silver and 7.5% other metals — almost always copper. The “925” hallmark (or the number .925 stamped on an item) confirms this composition. The copper is added because pure silver is too soft for most practical applications — jewellery and silverware would bend and scratch easily without it.

The “sterling” standard has been the quality benchmark for silverware and jewellery in most of the world for centuries, regulated in many markets by assay offices that test and hallmark items to confirm they meet the 925 purity threshold.

How to Calculate the Sterling Silver Price

The value of sterling silver is simply 92.5% of the pure silver spot price for the same weight. The formula:

Sterling silver melt value
Weight (g)×Price/g (999)×0.925
Worked Example

At ~$2.44/gram for 999 silver: a 100-gram sterling silver item contains 92.5 grams of actual silver → 100g × $2.44 × 0.925 = $225.70 in pure silver value.

When selling scrap sterling silver, dealers will typically offer less than this theoretical melt value — they apply a margin to cover refining, handling, and overhead costs. Typical dealer buy prices for sterling silver scrap run at approximately 80–90% of the calculated melt value.

06
Five Decades · Peaks & Crashes

Silver Price History: Key Milestones

Silver's price history is full of extraordinary peaks, multi-decade bear markets, and dramatic recoveries. Understanding this history provides important context for where prices stand today.

  • Pre-1970s
    Fixed and Suppressed

    For most of the 20th century, silver traded in a narrow range under US government price support programmes. When silver was freed alongside gold's liberation from Bretton Woods in the early 1970s, prices began to rise.

  • January 1980 · $52.80
    The Hunt Brothers Peak

    Brothers Nelson Bunker Hunt and William Herbert Hunt attempted to corner the global silver market, accumulating ~130 million troy ounces. Silver surged from $6/oz in early 1979 to a record $52.80 intraday on January 18, 1980 on the Chicago CBOT. COMEX intervened, the price collapsed, and the Hunts were bankrupted.

  • 1980–2000
    The Long Bear Market

    Silver languished below $8/oz for most of the 1980s and 1990s, weighed down by high real interest rates, a strong dollar, and declining inflation expectations.

  • April 2011 · $47.94
    The Second Bull Market Peak

    Silver tracked gold higher through the 2000s, rising from ~$4.50 in 2001 to its first challenge of the 1980 peak in 31 years. Driven by safe-haven demand, dollar weakness and the beginning of genuine industrial demand growth from solar and electronics.

  • 2020–2021
    COVID Surge & Silver Squeeze

    Silver surged from $12 in March 2020 to ~$30 by August 2020. A viral “silver squeeze” social media campaign in early 2021 briefly pushed prices above $28–30 before they retreated.

  • January 2026 · $121.62
    The Historic Breakout Above $100

    Silver gained more than 279% at its peak, reaching $103.26 intraday on January 23, 2026 on COMEX — the first time in history silver traded above $100. The all-time intraday high of $121.62 was set on January 29, 2026.

$121.62

Per troy ounce on COMEX, 29 January 2026 — the first time silver had ever traded above $100. Since then, silver has corrected sharply to the $73–$78 range, a decline of approximately 37% from peak — though still dramatically above 2024 levels.

07
Forces · What Moves Silver

Factors Affecting Silver Prices

Silver responds to many of the same forces that move gold, but with additional drivers unique to its industrial character. Understanding these factors helps investors and users make sense of day-to-day price movements.

1. US Dollar & Real Interest Rates

A weaker US dollar lifts silver prices in dollar terms; rising real interest rates increase the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding silver. The Fed's rate policy is one of silver's most important macro drivers.

2. Solar Panels & Photovoltaics

Each solar panel contains ~0.64 troy oz of silver. Solar already consumed over 25% of annual global silver supply in 2024 and demand from this sector alone is projected to potentially double between 2020 and 2030.

3. Electric Vehicles

EVs use roughly twice the silver of conventional vehicles — in power electronics, charging systems, sensors, and safety systems. EV-related silver demand jumped an estimated 20% in 2025.

4. AI Data Centres & 5G

AI infrastructure requires high-efficiency silver-containing components for extreme power loads. Smartphones, 5G base stations, medical devices and industrial electronics all rely on silver's unmatched conductivity.

5. Structural Supply Deficits

2026 marks the fifth consecutive year of structural deficit, projected at ~46.3 million troy ounces (up 15% from 2025). Cumulative 2021–2025 deficit totalled ~820 million ounces.

6. Investment & Safe-Haven Flows

Bars, coins, ETFs and financial instruments respond to fear, uncertainty and inflation. Silver is a smaller, less liquid market, so a surge that barely moves gold can produce dramatic silver price moves.

7. Currency Movements

Because silver is priced in US dollars globally, dollar exchange rate movements affect non-US buyers exactly as they do gold. Outside the US, both spot movement and the USD rate affect home-currency returns.

8. Inflation Sensitivity

Silver benefits from inflation as both a store of value and an industrial input. Historically it has outperformed gold in early and middle phases of inflationary episodes when investment and industrial demand rise together.

Why Silver Differs from Gold

Approximately 50–55% of annual silver consumption is industrial, compared to roughly 8% for gold. This makes silver far more sensitive to global economic activity. Strong manufacturing data and growing renewable energy investment are specifically bullish; economic weakness that reduces industrial production is specifically bearish.

The Ratio · Relative Value Signal

The Gold-Silver Ratio

The gold-silver ratio — how many ounces of silver are needed to purchase one ounce of gold — is one of the most closely watched signals in precious metals markets. A higher ratio means silver is cheap relative to gold; a lower ratio means silver is expensive relative to gold.

Gold-Silver Ratio · Live Level vs Historical Range
The long-term modern average is 60–80:1. Readings above 80:1 have historically signalled silver is cheap relative to gold; readings below 40:1 mean gold is relatively cheaper.

2026 Outlook · Bull vs Bear

Is Silver a Good Investment in 2026?

Silver offers a genuinely distinct investment case from gold — one that is simultaneously more compelling in some respects and more complicated in others.

The Bull Case for Silver

  • Structural supply deficit Five consecutive years of deficits. When demand systematically exceeds supply for years, it creates conditions for sustained price appreciation.
  • Industrial growth locked in Solar, EV and AI build-outs are decade-long structural investments that will consume increasing silver regardless of investment demand.
  • Still cheap vs gold At a ratio of ~60:1 there is a credible argument that it should compress further as silver's industrial demand premium grows.
  • Affordability At $75–$78/oz versus gold at $4,500+/oz, silver allows accumulation of meaningful quantities at much lower entry points — making dollar-cost averaging more practical.

The Bear Case & Real Risks

  • High volatility The 37% pullback from January 2026's high to May 2026 happened within months — a decline that would require gold to fall from $5,589 to roughly $3,500 to match.
  • Industrial demand is cyclical The same demand that provides structural upside becomes a drag during recessions. Silver consistently underperforms gold when global manufacturing contracts sharply.
  • Inflation-adjusted highs still distant The January 2026 peak of $121.62 is still significantly below the inflation-adjusted 1980 high of approximately $200+ per troy ounce.
  • No income or yield Like gold, silver pays no dividends or interest — it is a pure price appreciation asset.

Medium-Term Structural Case (2026–2030)

If solar and EV demand growth continues at current trajectories, cumulative silver demand from these two sectors alone could reach 1.5 billion ounces annually by 2030. Annual mine production of ~820–830 million ounces cannot meet this without prices rising significantly. Some forecasters therefore see $150–$200+ silver as plausible by 2030 — not certain, but backed by supply-demand mathematics.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the sterling silver price per gram?

Sterling silver (925) is 92.5% pure silver. At a spot price of approximately $76/troy oz ($2.44/gram for pure silver), sterling silver is worth approximately $2.44 × 0.925 = $2.26 per gram in melt value. This changes with the live silver spot price.

What is the difference between sterling silver and pure silver?

Sterling silver (925) is 92.5% silver and 7.5% copper — harder and more durable than pure silver, making it suitable for jewellery and cutlery.

Pure silver (999 fine) is 99.9% silver and is used for investment bars, coins and industrial applications. Sterling silver is worth approximately 7.5% less than fine silver of the same weight.

How is the silver spot price determined?

The silver spot price emerges from continuous global trading on COMEX (New York), the LBMA (London), and the Shanghai Futures Exchange.

The LBMA publishes a daily Silver Price benchmark at 12:00 PM London time through an electronic auction. The continuous real-time spot price is primarily derived from near-term COMEX futures activity.

Is silver a good investment in 2026?

Silver has structural tailwinds — persistent supply deficits, booming solar and EV demand, and investment demand aligned with gold — but is significantly more volatile than gold.

Most analysts see it as a complementary position to gold rather than a replacement. At a gold-silver ratio of ~60:1, silver is near its historical fair value relative to gold.

What is the silver price forecast for 2026?

Institutional forecasts range widely: J.P. Morgan forecasts an average of $81/oz, Commerzbank targets $90 by year-end, Deutsche Bank projects $100, and Bank of America's bull case reaches $135–$309.

The LBMA analyst consensus is $79.57 on average with a $42–$165 range. The wide range reflects genuine uncertainty about industrial demand sustainability.

The most widely used real-time sources are Kitco (kitco.com), BullionVault (bullionvault.com), GoldPrice.org, and GoldPriceTools.com.

All draw on COMEX and LBMA data and display prices per troy ounce, per gram, and per kilogram.

Silver's all-time intraday high was $121.62 per troy ounce, set on January 29, 2026 on COMEX — the first time silver had ever traded above $100.

This surpassed the previous all-time nominal high of $52.80 set during the Hunt Brothers silver squeeze on January 18, 1980.
