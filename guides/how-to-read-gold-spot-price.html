These aren't complicated questions, but getting them wrong is surprisingly costly — and surprisingly common. This guide covers the spot price end-to-end: what it is, how it is set, how to convert it across weights and currencies, the relationship between spot and physical prices, and how to make sense of a live gold price chart.

01 What Is the Gold Spot Price?

The gold spot price is the current market price for one troy ounce of 24-karat (pure) gold, quoted for immediate settlement and delivery. It is the global benchmark — the single number from which every other gold price is derived, whether you are buying a 1-gram gold bar, a 400-troy-ounce institutional ingot, or a gold ETF share.

"Spot" refers to the settlement type: a spot trade settles immediately (typically within two business days in gold markets), as opposed to a futures contract, which settles at a specified future date. When you see "gold price" quoted on financial sites, bullion dealer pages, or news tickers, that number is almost always the spot price — the real-time market rate for pure gold right now.

Where Gold Is Now

As of mid-May 2026, gold spot is approximately $3,200–$4,700 per troy ounce depending on the day, having consolidated after its all-time high of $5,589.38 in January 2026.

Key Properties of the Spot Price

  • Quoted in US dollars (USD) globally — all other currency prices are derived by applying the relevant exchange rate
  • Represents 24-karat (100% pure) gold — no alloys or manufacturing costs included
  • Updates continuously during market hours — typically every few seconds
  • Does not include dealer premiums — it is the wholesale benchmark, not the retail price

02 The Troy Ounce: The Foundation of Every Gold Price

Before you can read a gold price meaningfully, you need to understand the unit it is quoted in — and it is not the ounce you might expect.

Troy Ounce vs. Regular Ounce

Gold has been priced in troy ounces since the Middle Ages, a system that became standardised globally when Britain formally adopted it in 1527 and the United States followed in 1828. The troy ounce and the everyday "avoirdupois" ounce used in cooking are not the same:

Troy Ounce

The troy ounce is heavier by about 2.75 grams. This seems small but becomes significant when calculating the value of larger quantities. Anyone converting between ounces and grams who accidentally uses the regular 28.35-gram ounce will consistently undervalue their gold.

All ultimately convert to troy ounces — which is how they connect to the global spot price.

What the Spread Tells You

For the wholesale gold spot market, the spread is typically very narrow — often just $0.50 to $2.00 per troy ounce, reflecting extremely high liquidity. A narrow spread signals a liquid, efficiently traded market; a wide spread signals lower liquidity or elevated market stress.

LBMA Auction Mechanic

The LBMA Gold Price benchmark uses an electronic auction administered by ICE Benchmark Administration. The 15 participating institutions submit buy/sell orders, and the platform iterates until supply and demand balance within a 20,000 troy-ounce threshold — at which point the price is "fixed," published, and becomes the official benchmark for institutional contracts worldwide.

When you buy from a retail dealer, the spread you encounter will be much wider than the wholesale spot spread — dealers layer their premium on top, which we explain in the next section.

05 Spot Price vs. Physical Gold Price: The Premium Explained

This is where many first-time gold buyers are caught off guard. You check the spot price online — say, $4,600 per troy ounce — then visit a bullion dealer to find a 1-oz gold coin listed at $4,740 or more. You are not being cheated. You are paying the premium.

What Is the Premium?

The premium is the amount added to the spot price to reflect the real costs of producing and delivering a physical gold product: refining, minting, packaging, insurance, storage, transport, distribution, dealer operating costs, and profit margin.

Physical gold price = Spot price + Premium

The Premium Rule

The larger the bar, the lower the premium as a percentage of spot. A 400-oz institutional bar carries almost no premium because the per-unit minting and handling costs are negligible relative to the gold value. A 1-gram bar has a much higher percentage premium because fixed fabrication costs are spread across a tiny amount of gold.

Dealer Spread: The Round-Trip Cost

Beyond the purchase premium, there is also the dealer spread — the difference between what a dealer charges you when you buy and what they will pay you when you sell back the same product. This is your round-trip cost of getting in and out of physical gold. Popular, liquid products like the 1-oz Britannia or American Eagle have narrower dealer spreads because they are easy to resell; obscure products have wider spreads.

Why You Cannot Buy Gold at Spot

It is nearly impossible to purchase retail quantities of physical gold at raw spot price with no premium attached. Even the most competitive online bullion dealers add at least a small premium to cover their costs. The spot price is a wholesale benchmark used by professional market participants trading 400-oz institutional bars — it is the starting point from which retail prices are derived, not the retail price itself.

06 How Is the Spot Price Set? The 24-Hour Trading Cycle

The gold spot price is not set by a single institution. It emerges from continuous global trading activity across three overlapping sessions that together provide near-round-the-clock liquidity.

The Three Global Gold Trading Sessions
Together they keep gold trading roughly 23 hours per weekday
SESSION 01
Asia
Tokyo · Singapore · Shanghai
The trading day opens in Asia, where significant physical demand — particularly from China and India — sets the early tone. The Shanghai Gold Exchange (SGE) has growing influence on the global price.
SESSION 02

London still accounts for the majority of professional market activity. The LBMA publishes the primary global benchmark prices twice daily through electronic auctions administered by ICE Benchmark Administration.
SESSION 03
New York (COMEX)
CME Group · Futures
COMEX is the world's most important gold futures exchange. Enormous volumes trade here — mostly settled in cash. COMEX and London spot track each other very closely thanks to professional arbitrage.

The gold price that appears on your screen throughout the day is a continuous real-time price emerging from whichever of these sessions is active at that moment. The market is effectively open 23 hours a day on weekdays, with a brief gap between the New York close and the Asian open.

07 Gold Spot Price vs. Gold Futures Price

The futures price is the agreed price for gold delivery at a specified future date — typically one, three, or six months from now. It is almost always slightly higher than the spot price, a condition called contango.

Cost-of-Carry Model
Futures price ≈ Spot price × e(r × t) + Storage costs
Where r is the risk-free interest rate and t is time to delivery.

In plain English: the futures price reflects the spot price plus the cost of storing and financing gold for the contract duration. In a normal market, a 3-month futures contract will price gold slightly above today's spot price — the difference is essentially the cost of owning gold for three months.

Spot Price
Immediate Settlement
  • Settles within 2 business days
  • Real-time wholesale benchmark
  • Used by physical bullion dealers
  • No time-value adjustment
Futures Price
Forward Delivery
  • Settles 1, 3 or 6 months ahead
  • Slightly above spot (contango)
  • COMEX is the main venue
  • Most settled in cash, not gold

When the futures price falls below the spot price, it is called backwardation — a rarer condition that typically signals unusually tight near-term physical supply or extreme demand for immediate delivery.

Why It Matters for Investors

If you own gold through a futures-linked product, you may experience "roll costs" — each time an expiring contract is replaced with a new one, the slight contango premium creates a small drag on returns. Gold ETFs backed by physical metal (like SPDR Gold Trust) avoid this issue because they hold the actual metal.

08 How to Read a Gold Price Chart

A gold price chart is not just a line going up or down — it is a compressed history of market sentiment, carrying patterns and signals that experienced investors use to understand where gold might go next.

Line Charts vs. Candlestick Charts

The simplest gold charts show a single line connecting closing prices — clean and easy to read, good for spotting long-term trends. More detailed charts use candlesticks, where each "candle" shows four data points for the chosen time period: the open, high, low, and close.

Anatomy of a Candlestick
Bullish Candle
Buyers won. The close was higher than the open. Body height shows the size of the up-move.
Bearish Candle
Sellers won. The close was lower than the open. The thin lines above and below are "wicks" showing the extremes reached during the period.

A long-bodied candle signals strong conviction in one direction; a short-bodied candle with long wicks (called a "doji") signals indecision between buyers and sellers — often a warning that the current trend is losing momentum.

Time Frames

Most gold chart platforms let you choose the time period for each candle:

  • Intraday (1-minute → 1-hour): For traders watching gold's day-to-day or hour-by-hour movements
  • Daily: Each candle covers one trading day — most commonly used by medium-term investors and analysts
  • Weekly / Monthly: Longer time frames revealing the big structural trends, filtering out daily noise

For long-term investors, the weekly or monthly chart is often more informative than the daily — it shows where gold has been over years and decades without being distracted by short-term volatility.

Support and Resistance Levels

Support and resistance are perhaps the most important concepts in reading any chart:

  • Support is a price level where gold has previously fallen to and bounced back up — a "floor" where buyers have repeatedly stepped in
  • Resistance is a price level where gold has previously risen to and reversed — a "ceiling" where sellers have repeatedly emerged

Round numbers ($4,000, $4,500, $5,000) often act as psychological support and resistance levels. In early 2026, the $5,000 level became a major technical pivot: having been resistance on the way up, it became support once gold broke above it, and then resistance again once gold consolidated below it during the post-peak correction.

Moving Averages

A moving average (MA) is the average closing price over a specified period, plotted as a smooth line through the raw price data. It filters out day-to-day noise and reveals the underlying trend:

  • 50-day MA — short-term trend indicator. Gold trading above its 50-day MA is in a near-term uptrend
  • 200-day MA — the most widely watched long-term trend indicator. Gold above its 200-day MA is in a structural bull market

The "golden cross" — when the 50-day MA crosses above the 200-day MA — is a widely-watched bullish signal. The "death cross" (50-day MA crossing below the 200-day MA) is the bearish equivalent. An EMA (Exponential Moving Average) weights recent prices more heavily, making it more responsive than a simple MA.

Step-by-Step: Reading Any Gold Chart

  1. 1
    Zoom out first.Look at the 1-year or 5-year chart before the daily. Understand the big trend — is gold in a long-term uptrend, downtrend, or trading range?
  2. 2
    Identify key levels.Where has price previously reversed? Mark those horizontal levels as support or resistance.
  3. 3
    Check the moving averages.Is gold above or below its 50-day and 200-day MAs? In 2026, the 200-day MA has been consistently below price, confirming the structural uptrend.
  4. 4
    Read the most recent candles.What has sentiment been short-term? Are candles predominantly green (buyers in control) or red (sellers in control)?
  5. 5
    Interpret in macro context.A technical signal means more when it aligns with macro drivers — a weakening dollar, Fed cuts, or central bank buying.

09 Understanding Gold Prices in Different Currencies

The spot price is set in US dollars, but it is available — and consumed — in every major currency worldwide. Converting is straightforward: take the dollar spot price and divide by the relevant USD exchange rate.

Example
If gold = $4,600/oz and EUR/USD = 1.08:  Gold in EUR = $4,600 ÷ 1.08 = €4,259/oz

Gold prices in other currencies can move even when the dollar price is flat — because the exchange rate itself moves. A falling dollar pushes gold up in dollar terms, but gold may not move much in euros if the euro is strengthening at the same pace.

For Non-Dollar Investors

This creates an important consideration: do you want exposure to gold, or to gold + dollar movements? Buying gold priced in your local currency hedges the currency effect; buying dollar-denominated gold also gives you exposure to USD movements.

10 Common Mistakes When Reading the Gold Spot Price

  • 1
    Confusing troy ounces with regular ounces.This error systematically undervalues gold by about 10%. Always confirm which unit a price is quoted in before converting.
  • 2
    Treating spot price as the purchase price.You cannot buy physical gold at the spot price — there is always a premium. The spot price is the starting point, not the invoice total.
  • 3
    Ignoring the bid-ask spread.When selling gold, you will receive the bid price — less than the "spot" you see on a website. The round-trip cost includes both the entry premium and the bid-ask differential on exit.
  • 4
    Checking a delayed price.Many free financial websites show spot prices with a 15–20 minute delay. For time-sensitive transactions, always confirm you are looking at a live feed (Kitco, BullionVault, LBMA, or any major dealer with real-time updates).
  • 5
    Assuming a different currency price reflects a different gold value.Gold is gold anywhere in the world. A £3,410 per troy ounce in sterling and a $4,600 per troy ounce in dollars reflect the same underlying gold at the prevailing exchange rate.

11 Frequently Asked Questions

What is the gold spot price right now?

As of May 2026, the gold spot price is approximately $3,200–$4,700 per troy ounce depending on the date, consolidating after the January 2026 all-time high of $5,589.38. For a live reading, BullionVault, Kitco, or GoldPrice.org all publish real-time updates — or use our live calculator.

What is the gold price per gram today?

Divide the current spot price per troy ounce by 31.1035 to get the per-gram price. At $4,600/oz, that is approximately $147.90 per gram of pure (24-karat) gold.

What is the gold price per kilogram?

Multiply the spot price per troy ounce by 32.1507. At $4,600/oz, that gives approximately $147,893 per kilogram.

Why does the physical gold I buy cost more than the spot price?

Because the spot price is a wholesale benchmark for 400-oz institutional bars traded between professional market participants. Physical retail gold requires refining, minting, packaging, insurance, transport, and dealer margin — all added as a premium over spot.

The premium typically ranges from 2–15% depending on the product, with larger bars carrying lower percentage premiums than small coins.

What is the difference between bid and ask price for gold?

The bid is what buyers will pay; the ask is what sellers will accept. The spread between them reflects market liquidity and transaction costs. In the wholesale gold market, this spread is typically very narrow ($1–$2). Retail dealers apply a wider effective spread through their buy/sell pricing.

What is the LBMA Gold Price and why does it matter?

The LBMA Gold Price is the globally recognised benchmark published twice daily — at 10:30 AM and 3:00 PM London time — by the London Bullion Market Association through an electronic auction. It is the standard reference for institutional contracts, central bank valuations, mining royalties, and most large physical gold transactions worldwide.

How do I read a gold price chart?

Start by looking at the overall trend on a longer time frame (weekly or monthly). Identify support and resistance levels where price has previously reversed. Check whether price is above or below key moving averages (50-day, 200-day). Then zoom in to the daily or shorter time frame to interpret recent candle patterns and short-term momentum. Always interpret technical signals in the context of the macroeconomic environment.

What time does the gold market open and close?

Gold trades essentially 23 hours a day on weekdays as trading passes between Asian, London, and New York sessions. The COMEX gold futures market opens at 18:00 ET Sunday and closes at 17:00 ET Friday, with a brief daily maintenance gap.
