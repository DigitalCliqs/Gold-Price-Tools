How to Read the Gold Spot Price

By GoldPriceTools Editorial Team · Published 3 May 2026 · Updated 3 May 2026

The gold spot price is the current market price for immediate delivery of one troy ounce of pure (24K) gold, quoted in US dollars. Understanding how to read, convert, and apply the spot price is essential whether you are buying jewellery, selling scrap gold, or making an investment.

Step 1: Find the XAU/USD Spot Price

Gold is quoted globally using the ISO currency code XAU. The spot price you see on financial data sites (Bloomberg, Reuters, Kitco) is XAU/USD — the price of one troy ounce of pure gold in US dollars. The LBMA Gold Price, set twice daily in London at 10:30 and 15:00 GMT, is the globally accepted benchmark.

Tip: Our live calculator shows the real-time XAU/USD and XAU/GBP spot price, refreshed every 60 seconds from market data feeds.

Step 2: Convert Troy Ounces to Grams

One troy ounce equals exactly 31.1035 grams. To find the price per gram, divide the spot price by 31.1035:

Price per gram (24K) = Spot price (USD/oz) ÷ 31.1035
Example: $3,300 ÷ 31.1035 = $106.10/g

Step 3: Convert to GBP (or Your Currency)

Multiply the USD/gram price by the current GBP/USD exchange rate:

Price per gram (GBP) = USD/gram × GBP/USD rate
Example: $106.10 × 0.79 = £83.82/g

Step 4: Adjust for Karat Purity

The spot price is always for 24 karat (pure) gold. To find the value of gold at a lower karat, multiply by the purity factor:

Example: At £83.82/g (24K), the 9K gold price per gram = £83.82 × 0.375 = £31.43/g

Bid, Ask, and Spread

Like any traded market, gold has a bid (what buyers will pay) and an ask (what sellers want). The difference is the spread, typically $0.50–$2.00 per troy ounce in the spot market. When you buy physical gold from a dealer, they add a premium of 1–5% above spot; when you sell, dealers typically pay 85–95% of spot. Our calculator displays the mid-market spot price.

Understanding COMEX vs LBMA: Two Key Benchmarks

Gold trading happens around the clock, but the two most influential price benchmarks are set by the COMEX (Commodity Exchange, part of CME Group, New York) and the LBMA (London Bullion Market Association). COMEX futures contracts drive most of the intraday price movement you see on charts. The LBMA publishes two official "fixings" each business day — the LBMA Gold Price AM (10:30 London time) and the LBMA Gold Price PM (15:00 London time) — which serve as reference prices for bullion contracts, mining royalties, and fund valuations worldwide.

When you see a "spot price" on a website or app, it typically reflects the current COMEX front-month futures contract, adjusted for the cost-of-carry to strip out the time premium. The difference between the COMEX futures price and the true spot price is usually less than $1–2/oz during normal market conditions.

Reading an Intraday Gold Price Chart

Most gold price charts use candlestick or line format. On a candlestick chart, each candle represents a time period (1 min, 5 min, 1 hour, etc.) and shows four data points:

  • Open: Price at the start of the period
  • High: Highest price reached during the period
  • Low: Lowest price reached during the period
  • Close: Price at the end of the period

A green (bullish) candle means the close was higher than the open. A red (bearish) candle means the close was lower. Long "wicks" (the thin lines above and below the candle body) show that the price briefly reached much higher or lower levels before pulling back — a sign of volatility or rejection at key price levels.

What Moves the Gold Spot Price Intraday?

Key events that cause sharp gold price moves within a single trading day include:

  • US economic data releases (Non-Farm Payrolls, CPI, PCE, FOMC statements) — released at 13:30 or 14:00 London time
  • Fed Chair speeches and press conferences
  • Geopolitical news — sudden escalations (sanctions, conflicts, elections) cause safe-haven buying spikes
  • USD strength/weakness — the DXY (US Dollar Index) moves inversely with gold most of the time
  • LBMA Fix publication — can cause brief algorithmic reactions if the fix deviates significantly from pre-fix expectations

Spot Price vs What You Actually Pay

The spot price you see is the theoretical wholesale price for 400 troy oz LBMA Good Delivery bars traded between professional counterparties. When buying retail — coins, small bars, jewellery — you will always pay a premium over spot:

  • Gold Sovereign (Royal Mint): spot + 3–5% in normal market conditions
  • 1 oz Britannia coin: spot + 4–7%
  • 1 kg gold bar (LBMA-accredited): spot + 0.3–1%
  • Gold jewellery (retail): melt value + 50–200% fabrication/design premium

When selling, you will receive below spot — typically 96–99% of spot for investment-grade bullion sold to a reputable dealer, and 70–85% of melt value for jewellery.
