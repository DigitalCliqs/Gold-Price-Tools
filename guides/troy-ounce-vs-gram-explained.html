Troy Ounce vs Gram — What’s the Difference?

By GoldPriceTools Editorial Team · Published 3 May 2026 · Updated 3 May 2026

A troy ounce is not the same as a standard (avoirdupois) ounce. One troy ounce equals 31.1035 grams, while a standard ounce is only 28.3495 grams. Gold, silver, platinum, and palladium are always quoted in troy ounces on global markets — understanding this difference is essential to avoid costly pricing errors.

Why Do Precious Metals Use Troy Weight?

The troy weight system originated in the medieval trading city of Troyes, France, which held major European trade fairs in the 12th and 13th centuries. Merchants established standardised weights for gold and silver trading there, and the system spread across Europe. When the London Bullion Market formalised in the 17th century, troy weight became the global standard for precious metals, where it has remained ever since.

Why it matters: If a dealer quotes you a “per ounce” price and you assume they mean a standard ounce, you could overpay by about 10%. Always confirm whether prices are per troy ounce or standard ounce. Gold spot prices are always troy ounces.

Troy Weight in Practice

  • The standard London Bullion Market good delivery gold bar weighs approximately 400 troy ounces (12.44 kg)
  • The standard good delivery silver bar weighs approximately 1,000 troy ounces (31.1 kg)
  • UK Sovereign gold coins weigh 7.9881 grams = 0.2354 troy ounces of fine gold
  • American Gold Eagle 1 oz coins contain exactly 1 troy ounce of gold
  • LBMA quotes always use troy ounces — this is the global benchmark

The Historical Origin of the Troy Ounce

The troy ounce traces its origins to Troyes, France, a major trading city in the medieval period. The Troyes fair was one of the most important in 12th–15th century Europe, and merchants developed a standardised system of weights for trading gold and silver. The English adopted the Troyes system for precious metals in the 15th century, and it has remained the international standard for gold, silver, platinum, and palladium ever since.

It was formally codified in the US in 1828 when the US Mint adopted the troy system exclusively for coinage metals. Today, every precious metals price you see — from LBMA benchmarks to COMEX futures — uses troy ounces.

Troy Ounce vs Avoirdupois Ounce

The key distinction that catches people out:

MeasurementGramsUsed for
1 Troy ounce31.1035 gGold, silver, platinum, palladium
1 Avoirdupois ounce28.3495 gEveryday goods, food, packages
Difference+2.754 g (+9.7%)Troy oz is heavier

This matters practically: if you weigh a gold coin on a kitchen scale showing "oz", it is reading avoirdupois ounces. A 1 troy oz Britannia coin (31.1035 g) would show as 1.097 avoirdupois oz. Always use a calibrated jeweller's or gold scale set to grams or troy ounces when weighing precious metals.

Practical Conversions for UK Buyers and Sellers

The most useful conversions to memorise:

  • Grams to troy oz: divide by 31.1035 (e.g. 5g ÷ 31.1035 = 0.1608 troy oz)
  • Troy oz to grams: multiply by 31.1035 (e.g. 0.5 troy oz × 31.1035 = 15.55g)
  • Kilograms to troy oz: multiply by 32.1507 (e.g. 1 kg = 32.1507 troy oz)
  • Tola to grams: multiply by 11.664 (common for South Asian gold)
